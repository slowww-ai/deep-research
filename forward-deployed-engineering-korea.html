1. Market definition, rationale, and role boundaries

Define the forward-deployed role family, why it exists, and how it differs from adjacent engineering, sales, service, and consulting models.

Forward-deployed engineering is a delivery model at the customer–product boundary, not simply a new title for any customer-facing engineer (Palantir, OpenAI, Scale AI). In its narrow form, an FDE is a vendor-side production engineer who works in sustained proximity to customers, converts an ambiguous operational problem into deployed software, owns a material span from discovery through rollout or stabilization, and returns field evidence to the provider’s reusable product or model roadmap (Palantir, OpenAI, Scale AI). Physical residence at a customer site is not essential, because current Seoul roles combine hybrid work with substantial travel and end-to-end customer responsibility (OpenAI, Palantir).

The model exists because enterprise deployment is often an information-and-ownership problem rather than a software-installation problem (Management Science, RAND). Technical problem solving can depend on “sticky” information that is costly to transfer away from its original context, and RAND’s practitioner research shows that AI projects fail when stakeholders misunderstand the problem, optimize the wrong metric, or build a model that does not fit the business workflow (Management Science, RAND). The gap is visible at scale: most respondents in McKinsey’s 2025 survey had not moved beyond AI experimentation or pilots, while high performers were nearly three times as likely to redesign workflows; PwC’s 2026 survey found only 12% of CEOs reporting both cost and revenue benefits and associated stronger returns with responsible-AI and enterprise-integration foundations (McKinsey, PwC).

FDE addresses these failures by joining domain discovery, production engineering, integration, evaluation, adoption, and product learning in one short feedback loop (OpenAI, Palantir, Scale AI). OpenAI measures the role through production adoption, workflow impact, and feedback that changes product and model roadmaps, while Palantir describes field teams working with core engineering to synthesize customer evidence and ship new features (OpenAI, Palantir). The buyer gains a tighter link between operational truth, technical decisions, production behavior, and adoption, while the provider gains higher-signal roadmap input, reusable deployment assets, and evidence-based expansion opportunities (RAND, OpenAI, Scale AI).

The boundary with adjacent work is functional rather than nominal (OpenAI, GitLab, NIST). Ordinary software developers can build custom customer software; solution and sales engineers perform discovery, demonstrations, proofs, and technical guidance; professional-services engineers can implement, migrate, and write custom code; systems integrators can own customized development, test, operations, and maintenance; and implementation consultants can remain embedded through execution and capability building (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, GitLab, Salesforce, NIST, McKinsey & Company). The narrow FDE distinction is the combined presence of production-build accountability, post-sale deployment ownership, measurable customer outcomes, and privileged field-to-core-product learning rather than coding, embedding, or customer specificity alone (OpenAI, GitLab, Red Hat). The broader functional market should include only the deployable share of professional services, SI, technology and implementation consulting, and buyer-owned engineering teams that actually build or operate production capability, rather than counting their entire organizations (GitLab, NIST, McKinsey & Company).

Delivery can be organized as an individual embed, a multidisciplinary pod, or a dedicated deployment unit (OpenAI, Accenture, OpenAI). OpenAI’s FDE description supports a generalist embed spanning discovery through stable production; Accenture’s Palantir practice uses small teams of engineers, domain experts, and Deployment Strategists; and OpenAI’s 2026 Deployment Company centralizes approximately 150 acquired FDEs and deployment specialists while linking them to consulting and systems-integration partners (OpenAI, Accenture, OpenAI). The broader role family can also divide problem framing, product ownership, reliability, security, and enablement among specialists while preserving a shared connection to customer reality (Palantir, Scale AI, Palantir reliability, Palantir security, Palantir enablement).

The economic test is whether avoided translation and iteration costs, account value, and reusable learning exceed scarce engineering, travel, coordination, and maintenance costs (Management Science, Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). Palantir reported an 82% aggregate gross margin in 2025 alongside increased field-service-representative expense, which shows that an FDE-associated company can retain software-like company economics while still bearing direct field-delivery cost, but it does not disclose an FDE-specific margin or establish causality (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). The model approaches a services trap when effort remains permanently customer-specific; it preserves product leverage when field patterns become tools, playbooks, building blocks, or core-product changes that reduce future deployment effort (OpenAI, Palantir).

Korea shows both the narrow role and its broader substitutes (OpenAI, LG CNS). Palantir lists Seoul-based engineering and deployment-strategy roles that require Korean and English and substantial travel, while OpenAI lists a Seoul FDE responsible for end-to-end production deployment (Palantir FDSE, Palantir Deployment Strategist, OpenAI). LG CNS offers a domestic functional analogue at organization scale through an AI Center of about 300 experts and a stated lifecycle from consulting and architecture through development, testing, and operations (LG CNS). Korean SMEs nevertheless have broad capability needs but constrained direct-fit economics: the OECD and Korea Labor Institute report only 31% AI use among Korean SMEs, identify skills as their largest adoption barrier, and describe cautious investment linked to technology maturity and uncertain returns, while Palantir’s filing shows that field-service delivery carries direct labor cost (OECD/Korea Labor Institute, Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). These conditions support a two-layer market view in Korea: scarce direct vendor FDE capacity for strategic, complex deployments and a wider partner, SI, consultancy, and in-house ecosystem supplying equivalent functions where dedicated embeds are uneconomic (OpenAI, LG CNS, OECD/Korea Labor Institute).

1.2 Why the FDE model exists

The forward-deployed engineering model exists because complex enterprise deployment is often an information-and-ownership problem, not simply a software-installation problem (RAND; Management Science). Technical problem solving frequently depends on “sticky” information that is costly to acquire, transfer, and use away from where it originates; when that information resides at one site, problem solving tends to move toward that site rather than rely entirely on remote specifications (Management Science). The FDE places solution-building capability close to the customer’s operational context so that requirements, workflow realities, data constraints, and implementation choices can be tested and revised together (Management Science).

The failures that create demand

The first failure is loss of context across organizational handoffs (RAND). RAND’s interviews with experienced AI practitioners found that stakeholders often misunderstand or miscommunicate the problem, and that resulting models may optimize the wrong metrics or fail to fit the overall business workflow (RAND). RAND consequently identifies effective interaction between technologists and business experts as potentially decisive for project success (RAND). An FDE addresses this failure by keeping the people who understand the domain and the people who can change production software in the same iterative problem-solving loop (RAND; Management Science).

The second failure is the gap between a technically impressive pilot and an adopted production workflow (McKinsey). In McKinsey’s 2025 survey, 88% of respondents reported regular AI use in at least one business function, yet the majority of organizations remained in experimentation or piloting and only about one-third had begun to scale their AI programs (McKinsey). McKinsey’s AI high performers were nearly three times as likely as other organizations to have fundamentally redesigned workflows, and agile product delivery was strongly correlated with value (McKinsey). PwC’s 2026 CEO survey similarly found that only 12% of CEOs reported both cost and revenue benefits from AI, while organizations with responsible-AI frameworks and technology environments that enabled enterprise-wide integration were three times more likely to report meaningful financial returns (PwC). These findings explain why the role must reach beyond configuration: deployment value depends on integration, workflow redesign, adoption, governance, and measurement operating together (McKinsey; PwC).

The third failure is a slow or distorted learning loop between customers and the provider’s core product organization (Palantir; OpenAI). Palantir describes forward-deployed engineering as engineers getting as close as possible to a problem while working with core engineering teams to synthesize feedback and ship new features (Palantir). OpenAI likewise expects FDEs to feed deployment evidence into product and model roadmaps and to codify working patterns into reusable tools, playbooks, and building blocks (OpenAI). Scale’s forward-deployed product model is designed to translate messy operational reality into high-signal platform input and distinguish product constraints from execution, integration, and change-management blockers (Scale AI). Privy reports that customer-specific FDE work became a core capability available to every customer, but this is a company-authored example rather than independent outcome evidence (Privy). ⚠️

When proximity is worth its cost

Customer proximity is most valuable when important need information is difficult to transfer and a deployment requires repeated movement between customer context and provider solution knowledge (Management Science). The evidence supports FDE use where the problem is ambiguous, the workflow and integration constraints are consequential, the system must be evaluated in its actual operating context, and failure to translate business intent into technical choices has material cost (RAND; Management Science). It is also more defensible when field work produces reusable platform capabilities, because OpenAI and Palantir explicitly treat codified learning and core-product feedback as part of the model rather than as incidental account service (OpenAI; Palantir).

The economic counterweight is direct labor and coordination cost (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). Palantir reported an aggregate gross margin of 82% in 2025, showing that a company associated with the FDE model can retain software-like company-level gross margins, but its filing does not disclose an FDE-specific margin or demonstrate that FDE caused that result (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). The same filing reported that increased cost of revenue included an additional $29.1 million in field-service-representative expense, confirming that customer-proximate delivery creates direct cost (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). Palantir’s average revenue from its top twenty customers grew 45% to $93.9 million in 2025, which is consistent with an expansion-oriented account model but does not prove that FDE caused the expansion (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). Scale explicitly expects expansion opportunities to be grounded in demonstrated customer value, linking the commercial objective to production outcomes rather than deployment activity alone (Scale AI).

The practical test is therefore whether avoided translation and iteration costs, account value, and reusable learning exceed scarce engineering, travel, coordination, and maintenance costs (Management Science; Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). A deployment that repeatedly converts field patterns into shared product capability follows the intended product-learning model, while permanent one-off work without a core-product feedback loop lacks the documented mechanism that Palantir and OpenAI describe (Palantir; OpenAI). Privy argues that assumption-led engineering can be slow and misaligned in fast-moving markets, but this claimed advantage remains weak evidence because it is the provider’s own account of its model (Privy). ⚠️

Value for buyer and provider

For the buyer, the model is intended to reduce the chance that a team builds the wrong system, shorten the path from prototype to stable production, and connect technical delivery to workflow adoption and measurable impact (RAND; OpenAI). OpenAI defines success through production adoption and measurable workflow impact, while Scale expects its forward-deployed team to identify and drive the correct resolution channel for product, integration, execution, and change-management blockers (OpenAI; Scale AI). The buyer is consequently purchasing a tighter loop between domain truth, engineering decisions, production behavior, and organizational adoption rather than engineering hours alone (RAND; Scale AI).

For the provider, the model creates high-signal product learning, reusable deployment assets, and a route from successful initial use to evidence-based expansion (OpenAI; Scale AI). Palantir’s formulation makes the field-to-core-engineering loop the method by which its platforms are continuously developed, and OpenAI requires field feedback that changes model and product roadmaps (Palantir; OpenAI). The model succeeds strategically only if the provider becomes more capable after each engagement instead of accumulating isolated customer code (Palantir; OpenAI).

Korea-specific implications

The OECD and Korea Labor Institute report that only 31% of Korean SMEs use AI and identify lack of skills as their most important adoption barrier (OECD/Korea Labor Institute). The same report summarizes a Korea Labor Institute survey in which firms’ cautious AI investment was attributed to concerns about technology maturity and uncertain returns, with many preferring gradual and incremental adoption (OECD/Korea Labor Institute). These conditions support demand for problem selection, technical translation, integration, adoption, and proof of value rather than model access alone (OECD/Korea Labor Institute; RAND). They do not, however, establish that dedicated vendor FDEs are the economically appropriate channel for most SMEs, because the available evidence does not provide an FDE-specific cost or ROI threshold (OECD/Korea Labor Institute; Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).

Tensions:

  • Proximity versus scalability. Sticky customer information favors moving problem solving toward the customer, while provider economics require the resulting knowledge to become reusable beyond one engagement (Management Science; OpenAI).
  • Customization versus productization. OpenAI and Palantir require field learning to change reusable products, while Privy’s reported conversion of a bespoke solution into a shared capability is supportive but weak, self-reported evidence (OpenAI; Palantir; Privy). ⚠️
  • Plausible mechanism versus causal proof. McKinsey and PwC associate workflow redesign, agile delivery, integration foundations, and governance with AI value, but those surveys do not isolate the marginal effect of the FDE organizational form (McKinsey; PwC).
  • Buyer outcome versus provider expansion. Scale links expansion to demonstrated customer value, yet separate buyer outcome and provider growth objectives still require explicit joint measurement and governance (Scale AI).
  • Korea’s broad capability need versus FDE cost. Korean SMEs report a substantial skills barrier and cautious investment, while Palantir’s filing confirms that field delivery carries direct labor cost (OECD/Korea Labor Institute; Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).

Unresolved questions:

  • No independent study isolates the causal incremental value of an FDE from a capable SI, internal product team, or multidisciplinary consultancy (McKinsey; PwC).
  • Public evidence does not establish universal ACV, utilization, staffing-ratio, or FDE-specific gross-margin thresholds (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).

1.3 Boundaries and delivery-model archetypes

Forward-deployed engineering is best treated as an intersection of responsibilities rather than a protected title or a monopoly on customer-facing technical work (OpenAI, GitLab, NIST). Ordinary developers can build custom software for a single customer, Professional Services Engineers can work directly in a customer environment, implementation consultants can remain alongside users through execution, and systems integrators can own development, testing, operations, and maintenance (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, GitLab, McKinsey & Company, NIST). The narrow FDE category is distinguished by the simultaneous presence of production engineering, sustained customer proximity, end-to-end deployment ownership or co-ownership, measurable adoption or workflow accountability, and a direct feedback path into a vendor’s core product or model roadmap (OpenAI).

OpenAI’s Seoul posting provides a current example of this narrow model: its FDE owns discovery, technical scoping, system design, build, and production rollout, contributes production-grade full-stack code, and is measured on production adoption, workflow impact, and feedback that changes product and model roadmaps (OpenAI). This combination matters because none of the ingredients is individually exclusive to FDE: coding overlaps with software engineering and professional services, embedding overlaps with implementation consulting, customer-specific delivery overlaps with systems integration, and discovery or proofs of concept overlap with solution architecture and sales engineering (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, GitLab, Salesforce, McKinsey & Company, NIST).

Three delivery-model archetypes

1. Individual embed. In this archetype, one generalist FDE or technical lead works directly with a customer’s engineering and domain teams across discovery, build, rollout, and adoption (OpenAI). The model fits a bounded deployment in which one person can coordinate the relevant customer stakeholders and contribute code without requiring many parallel specialties (OpenAI). It minimizes internal coordination overhead, but its broad end-to-end responsibility concentrates knowledge and delivery risk in one role (OpenAI).

2. Multidisciplinary pod. Accenture’s Palantir FDE description provides direct evidence of a small pod combining engineers, domain experts, and Deployment Strategists to translate objectives into a plan, prototype with users, and ship production-grade solutions (Accenture). McKinsey’s implementation model similarly changes specialist profiles over a transformation while preserving continuity through a core client-service team, although its work emphasizes execution and capability building rather than production code alone (McKinsey & Company). The pod is therefore suited to deployments where engineering, domain translation, workflow change, and adoption must advance together and where shared outcome ownership is more credible than assigning every responsibility to one engineer (Accenture, McKinsey & Company).

3. Dedicated deployment unit. OpenAI established its Deployment Company as a standalone business unit intended to develop a distinct operating model, with approximately 150 acquired FDEs and Deployment Specialists and formal links to consulting and system-integration partners (OpenAI). Its typical engagement is designed to place FDEs inside an organization to design, build, test, and deploy production systems, while the unit supplies portfolio-scale staffing and partner orchestration (OpenAI). LG CNS offers a Korean functional analogue: its AI Center has about 300 experts across eleven roles and describes a one-stop delivery lifecycle from consulting and architecture through development, testing, and operations (LG CNS). The deployment-unit model is thus appropriate when a provider needs multiple pods, shared specialist capacity, reusable methods, and coverage across a portfolio rather than one strategic account (OpenAI, LG CNS).

Implications for market definition and Korea

The market should be reported in two concentric layers (OpenAI, GitLab). The narrow layer counts vendor or platform teams that directly build customer production systems, share deployment outcomes, and return field evidence to the core product or model roadmap (OpenAI). The broader functional layer includes only the deployable share of professional-services engineers, SI teams, technology and implementation-consulting pods, and buyer-owned engineering teams that actually build or operate the production capability; it should not count entire advisory, sales, support, or consulting organizations (GitLab, NIST, McKinsey & Company).

Korea has observable examples in both layers. OpenAI’s live Seoul role is a direct narrow-market signal because it combines production engineering, customer embedding, adoption guidance, and product feedback in one FDE position (OpenAI). LG CNS is a broad-market deployment-unit substitute because it combines research, platform, business, and delivery capabilities and advertises an end-to-end process through operations while applying partner technologies in Korean business environments (LG CNS). These actors need not be permanent competitors: OpenAI’s Deployment Company explicitly combines direct FDE capacity with consultancies and systems integrators, so a platform vendor, local SI, implementation consultant, and customer engineering team can compete in one phase and co-deliver in another (OpenAI). Market sizing should consequently classify capacity by lifecycle phase, production artifact, accountability, and decision right, then remove overlap across vendor, partner, and customer teams involved in the same deployment (OpenAI, GitLab).

Tensions:

  • Coding is necessary but not sufficient: OpenAI and Accenture require production code from FDEs, but ordinary software developers can build custom software and GitLab’s Professional Services Engineers are explicitly hands-on (OpenAI, Accenture, U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, GitLab). Outcome ownership and the field-to-product loop supply the additional boundary (OpenAI).

  • Solution architecture and sales engineering overlap with FDE in discovery, technical design, and proofs, but GitLab and Salesforce place these activities primarily in opportunity-oriented advisory or pre-sales motions (GitLab, Salesforce). The defensible distinction is primary production-build accountability rather than title or simple involvement after close (OpenAI, GitLab).

  • Professional-services accountability varies: Red Hat limits work by purchased units and term and leaves overall successful use to the customer, whereas OpenAI and Accenture describe FDEs as owning production delivery or client outcomes (Red Hat, OpenAI, Accenture). Some professional-services teams are therefore functional substitutes, but the categories are not interchangeable (GitLab, Red Hat, OpenAI).

  • Implementation consulting can be as embedded and outcome-oriented as FDE, but McKinsey’s documented center of gravity is execution, coaching, performance management, and capability building, while OpenAI’s is a production system tied to product and model learning (McKinsey & Company, OpenAI). Accenture’s Palantir pod demonstrates that a modern technology consultancy can combine both centers of gravity within one delivery team (Accenture).

  • Vendor versus consultancy is no longer a stable organizational boundary because Accenture directly employs Palantir FDEs and OpenAI’s Deployment Company is linked to consultancies and systems integrators (Accenture, OpenAI). The same co-delivered deployment capacity must not be counted once under the platform provider and again under its delivery partner (OpenAI).

  • Individual accountability and multidisciplinary delivery coexist: OpenAI describes a broad end-to-end individual role, while Accenture, OpenAI’s Deployment Company, and LG CNS evidence pods or units with multiple specialties (OpenAI, Accenture, OpenAI, LG CNS).

  • What are the comparable utilization, gross-margin, and cost-to-serve profiles of individual embeds, pods, and dedicated deployment units?

2. Delivery operating model

Describe the end-to-end work system through which an FDE engagement converts a customer problem into an adopted, governed, and reliable production capability.

The delivery operating model is a jointly governed sequence of investment and ownership decisions, not a single implementation phase: current provider models span exploration, production readiness, rollout, adoption, expansion, and eventual handoff or exit (OpenAI enterprise deployment role; Google SRE engagement model). Scarce FDE capacity should first be allocated using customer need, strategic value, technical complexity, and repeatable impact, while a bounded pilot should test business value, data readiness, technical feasibility, and risk against pre-agreed decision criteria (OpenAI enterprise deployment role; AWS generative-AI lifecycle guidance). Customer readiness is part of that qualification because production progress depends on named business, domain, technical, risk, and future operating owners as well as access to the relevant data and environments (Databricks LLM POC service description; AWS program-governance guidance).

Once selected, the engagement should move through explicit gates for discovery and chartering, contract and mobilization, pilot evidence, production approval, build and stabilization, adoption and value validation, expansion or productization, and handoff or exit (OpenAI pilot role; AWS generative-AI lifecycle guidance; AWS exit-strategy guidance). Governance should assign asymmetric decision rights: the provider owns technical discovery, delivery sequencing, product escalation, and remediation evidence, while customer executives and control owners retain authority over business priority, material scope, user acceptance, residual risk, and long-term operations (Scale AI forward-deployed product role; AWS program-governance guidance; NIST AI RMF Core). A jointly reviewed statement of work, RACI, decision and risk logs, escalation ladder, acceptance mechanism, support boundary, and exit plan turn those rights into an executable operating system (AWS implementation-planning guidance; AWS program-governance guidance).

Technical execution and domain translation should proceed as one loop because OpenAI’s Seoul FDE owns discovery, technical scoping, system design, build, and production rollout while working directly with customer engineering and domain teams (OpenAI Seoul FDE role). The team should map the real workflow, distinguish the stated request from the underlying operational problem, translate domain rules and exceptions into acceptance criteria, and use representative domain-authored evaluation cases before production approval (Scale AI forward-deployed product role; AWS evaluation guidance; IIBA acceptance-criteria guidance). The resulting technical package should include production-grade code, documented architecture, small and reviewable releases, reproducible deployment and rollback, layered testing, and—for agentic systems—evaluation of complete tool-use traces and actual resulting state rather than the agent’s asserted success alone (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Google Cloud Architecture Framework; Anthropic agent-evaluation guidance).

Production approval is not only a technical gate because NIST treats AI risk management as continuous, requires clear risk roles and communication lines, and assigns deployment-risk decisions to executive leadership (NIST AI RMF Core). The FDE should therefore implement assurance by design—permissions, data controls, evaluations, logging, human intervention, evidence, and remediation—while independent validators challenge higher-risk systems and customer security, privacy, legal, model-risk, business, and operational owners approve their respective domains (NIST AI RMF Core; NIST Generative AI Profile). In Korea, high-impact-AI obligations now include risk-management measures, explanation, human supervision, and retained evidence, which makes assurance artifacts and ownership allocation part of deployment rather than a post-launch compliance exercise (Korea AI Basic Act; Korea AI Basic Act Enforcement Decree).

Adoption is likewise part of delivery: OpenAI measures the Seoul FDE through production adoption and workflow impact, while Databricks assigns embedded delivery architects responsibility for aligning people, process, and technology and tracking adoption and risk across teams (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Databricks Delivery Solutions Architect service). The FDE should own technical adoption barriers, instrumentation, documentation, administrator and champion enablement, and go-live stabilization, but the customer’s sponsor, process owner, change team, and long-term operator must own policy, incentives, training at scale, routine support, and business outcomes (OpenAI Deployment Company; Palantir adoption program). Capability transfer is complete only when the customer can administer, govern, support, train for, and extend the system without routine dependence on the original FDE, and Palantir explicitly treats embedded center-of-excellence personnel as temporary rather than long-term use-case owners (Palantir adoption program; Palantir phase-three roles).

Operating ownership should then transfer progressively or continue under an explicit service model rather than remain ambiguous at go-live (Google SRE engagement model; Google Cloud operational-readiness guidance). Google requires production readiness before an SRE team accepts responsibility and transfers operations, change management, and access rights progressively while the development team remains available as backup (Google SRE engagement model). Palantir’s separate FDRE role demonstrates the continuing model: it goes on call for mission-critical customer workflows, prevents recurring failures, and routes support learning into product and tooling changes (Palantir FDRE role). Whichever model is chosen, critical-user-journey SLOs, actionable alerts, telemetry, runbooks, incident roles, escalation paths, support commitments, and a named pager owner should be agreed before the handoff date (Google incident-management guide; Google Cloud operational-readiness guidance).

Korea’s delivery market already contains both direct FDE capacity and end-to-end functional substitutes: OpenAI recruits a Seoul FDE for discovery through stable production, Samsung SDS packages Korean AX support from strategy through implementation, enterprise expansion, and operational improvement, and LG CNS offers agentic-AI evaluation, monitoring, and operation across cloud and on-premises environments (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Samsung SDS AX support; LG CNS AgenticWorks). This supports a layered local model in which a global vendor FDE owns model and product escalation, a Korean SI or MSP supplies integration and first-line operations, and the buyer’s business, risk, platform, and operations teams retain approval and durable ownership (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; LG CNS cloud operations; NIST AI RMF Core). The defining transition is therefore not “consulting to software” but “provider-led uncertainty reduction to jointly accepted production, then to an explicitly funded and capable steady-state owner,” with field learning continuously returned to the reusable product and deployment system (OpenAI enterprise deployment role; OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Palantir FDRE role).

2.1 Account selection, engagement lifecycle, and joint governance

Forward-deployed engineering should be managed as a sequence of joint investment decisions, not as an open-ended promise to place engineers beside a customer (OpenAI, AWS). OpenAI now prioritizes enterprise deployment engagements according to customer need, strategic value, technical complexity, and the potential for repeatable impact, while Scale assigns its forward-deployed product layer to the highest-impact enterprise accounts (OpenAI, Scale AI). This makes account selection a portfolio-allocation problem: a provider must judge both the prospective customer outcome and whether scarce field capacity will generate a reusable pattern, rather than treating contract size or brand prestige as sufficient evidence of fit (OpenAI).

The operating span is broader than a pilot or implementation project (OpenAI, Scale AI). OpenAI describes an enterprise journey from initial exploration through production applications and wider organizational adoption, and its deployment manager is accountable across solution design, implementation, production readiness, adoption, and expansion (OpenAI). Scale likewise expects its forward-deployed product role to move work from contract to production, coordinate with FDEs as one account team, serve as a senior escalation point, and ground expansion in demonstrated value (Scale AI). The evidence therefore supports a lifecycle of qualification, discovery, contract and mobilization, bounded pilot, production approval, build and stabilization, adoption and value validation, expansion or productization, and finally handoff or exit (OpenAI, OpenAI, AWS, AWS, AWS).

Which accounts merit scarce FDE capacity?

A practical qualification model should test six dimensions before assigning a deployment pod or individual FDE (OpenAI, OpenAI, Databricks).

  1. Material customer need. The proposed workflow should connect to a named business or operational outcome because OpenAI’s deployment leaders are expected to connect technical decisions to measurable business and operational results, and Scale makes its account product owner accountable for both delivery and customer value (OpenAI, Scale AI).

  2. Technical need for embedded production engineering. The account should present enough technical complexity and deployment uncertainty to warrant scarce field capacity, because technical complexity is an explicit OpenAI prioritization dimension and Scale requires contract-to-production ownership across real integration, security, and change-management constraints (OpenAI, Scale AI).

  3. Customer readiness and authority. The customer must make appropriate technical, business, and domain experts available, provide access to data and environments, approve models or services, and designate the internal personnel who will own the relevant products after the engagement (Databricks). These conditions mean provider ability to build is not enough; the buyer must be able to supply domain truth, access, approvals, and a future operating owner (Databricks).

  4. Decision-oriented pilotability. OpenAI’s current pilot model selects a small set of high-impact use cases capable of producing credible signal in 30–45 days and aligns cohort, scope, and success criteria to a clear commercial decision (OpenAI). AWS similarly treats a generative-AI proof of concept as a validation of business value, data readiness, technical feasibility, and risk mitigation rather than as a demonstration designed only to impress (AWS).

  5. Feasible path to governed production. The account should have credible customer owners for risk, policy, architecture, business acceptance, and operations because Microsoft assigns ongoing cloud-risk, policy, and governance-reporting accountability to a customer governance team, while AWS assigns significant issues, scope changes, plans, and final approval to the customer executive sponsor in its example governance model (Microsoft, AWS).

  6. Repeatable learning or justified expansion. OpenAI asks deployment leaders to distinguish scalable market patterns from bespoke requests and to measure the impact of reusable work, while Scale requires expansion to rest on demonstrated customer value (OpenAI, Scale AI).

These criteria imply clear disqualifiers (OpenAI, OpenAI, Databricks). A demo with no downstream decision, an unavailable sponsor or operating owner, blocked data or security access, and work that cannot produce either material customer value or repeatable learning are weak candidates for scarce FDE allocation because current provider models explicitly tie engagement selection to need, readiness, commercial decision, production outcome, and reusable impact (OpenAI, OpenAI, Databricks). The reviewed provider materials supply these selection dimensions but do not disclose a universal account score, minimum contract value, staffing formula, or expected probability of reaching production (OpenAI, Scale AI).

Contract design is part of that control system because AWS recommends joint SOW review, explicit deliverables, continuous recording of new scope and tradeoffs, and either formal change control or backlog prioritization (AWS).

2.2 Technical execution

Technical execution is the point at which forward deployment becomes distinguishable from advisory work: the team must make a customer-specific system work in the customer’s real environment and carry it from prototype to stable production (OpenAI). OpenAI’s Seoul FDE owns technical scoping, system design, build, and production rollout with customer engineering and domain teams, and the role is explicitly expected to write and review production-grade frontend and backend code (OpenAI). The role therefore cannot stop at an architecture presentation, API demonstration, or backlog; it must leave working code, integrations, test evidence, and a controlled path into production (OpenAI, Google Cloud).

The buyer’s technical problem is usually located at the interfaces among a vendor product or model, proprietary data, existing applications, identity and network controls, users, and production operations (OpenAI, Google Cloud). An FDE creates value by shortening the loop between diagnosing those interfaces and implementing the missing architecture, code, data flow, or deployment mechanism, while Google Cloud’s current production guidance reinforces small changes, rapid feedback, maintained architecture documentation, and independently operable components (OpenAI, Google Cloud). This analysis accordingly treats the mandate as broader than “custom coding” but narrower than owning every downstream control: the FDE owns technical truth and remediation, while the customer retains control of its environment and permissions (OpenAI, Palantir AI FDE).

What the FDE must do directly

A production-capable FDE should directly perform, or be the accountable technical driver for, six linked activities (OpenAI, Google Cloud).

  1. Establish the technical baseline. The joint team should inventory applications, data sources, schemas, APIs, identity boundaries, networks, deployment environments, non-functional requirements, and known failure modes, then produce an as-is view, a target architecture, and an explicit decision record because maintained architecture documentation creates a shared language and supports later design decisions (Google Cloud).

  2. Choose the minimum viable production architecture. The design should define component boundaries, data and interface contracts, model placement, tenancy, environment topology, and build-versus-use choices while resisting unnecessary complexity because Google recommends starting simply, iterating through small changes, and decoupling components so upgrades, controls, reliability targets, and monitoring can be applied independently (Google Cloud).

  3. Build where customer or product gaps block delivery. The expected implementation surface can include frontend and backend services, adapters, data pipelines, model calls, tool wrappers, event flows, and infrastructure or configuration as code because OpenAI’s Seoul role requires production-grade full-stack coding and ownership from prototype through stable production (OpenAI).

  4. Create a reproducible delivery path. Source control, review, automated builds, environment-specific configuration, release promotion, rollback, and deployment records should make the system repeatable rather than dependent on undocumented field-engineer actions; Palantir Apollo provides a concrete pattern through product and environment constraints plus release-channel promotion across cloud, on-premises, disconnected, and air-gapped environments (Palantir Apollo).

  5. Generate acceptance evidence, not merely a convincing demo. Conventional systems need layered code, interface, integration, migration, performance, and user-acceptance tests, while agent systems also need multi-turn traces and tests of the actual resulting environment state because Anthropic distinguishes an agent’s asserted answer from whether the intended state change really occurred (Anthropic).

  6. Prepare the handoff-ready technical package. The evidence supports current architecture documentation, interface and schema contracts, versioned code and configuration, build and deployment definitions, test and evaluation results, release and rollback instructions, dependency and model inventories, known limitations, and links to operational ownership because Google treats documentation and change history as inputs to future decisions and Palantir makes constraints, promotion, permissions, previews, and CI validation explicit parts of deployment (Google Cloud, Palantir Apollo, Palantir AI FDE).

Three technical execution modes

The engineering envelope changes materially across conventional software, agentic AI, and OT-integrated systems (Google Cloud, OpenAI, NIST).

Mode Core architecture and build work Additional testing and production evidence
Conventional enterprise software The FDE integrates deterministic services through APIs, data pipelines, schemas, identity controls, frontend/backend code, and environment configuration, with components decoupled where independent upgrades, controls, reliability targets, and monitoring are needed (OpenAI, Google Cloud). The production package should demonstrate reproducible builds, small controlled releases, interface and integration behavior, rollback, and maintained architecture and change records because Google links these practices to fast feedback and manageable evolution (Google Cloud).
Agentic AI The deployed system includes a model, tools, and instructions, with a run loop and either single-agent or coordinated multi-agent execution; OpenAI recommends maximizing a single agent before adding orchestration complexity (OpenAI). Tools should have standardized, documented, tested, and reusable definitions, and their access to external systems should be governed by ordinary authentication, authorization, and access control in addition to model guardrails (OpenAI). OT-integrated or physical AI The FDE must connect models and software to devices and physical processes under OT-specific performance, reliability, and safety requirements, and OPC UA offers a platform-independent basis for information modeling, secure communication, and interoperability among diverse industrial systems (NIST, OPC Foundation). Edge execution can package the model, runtime, and inference code separately and run cloud-trained models over locally generated device data (AWS). The production package should add asset and network topology, protocol and semantic mappings, edge model/runtime/inference versions, hardware and firmware compatibility, connectivity assumptions, plant change windows, rollback, and commissioning evidence because OT directly interacts with the physical environment and deployments may be disconnected or air-gapped (NIST, Palantir Apollo).

The key difference is not simply which programming language is used, but what the deployed system can do and how its behavior must be verified (Anthropic, NIST). Conventional testing can largely assert deterministic inputs, interfaces, and outputs, whereas an agent can take multiple tool-mediated paths, modify state over many turns, and return plausible text even when the intended external outcome did not occur (Anthropic). OT raises the consequence further because the controlled object is a physical process and NIST explicitly requires security work to respect performance, reliability, and safety (NIST).

Agentic-AI production standards

An agent deployment should begin with an explicit architecture of model, tools, and instructions, then add complexity only where tests show that one agent cannot reliably follow the logic or select the right tools (OpenAI). This incremental rule matters economically as well as technically because each additional agent creates more handoffs, state transitions, tool permissions, and paths that must be evaluated (OpenAI, Anthropic).

The minimum agent evidence set should include representative tasks with defined success criteria, repeated trials, code-, model-, or human-based graders, complete traces, and checks of the final environment state because Anthropic defines a trace as the record of outputs, tool calls, reasoning, intermediate results, and other interactions and treats the outcome as distinct from the transcript (Anthropic). It should also include versioned tools and instructions, tool-permission tests, retry and stop conditions, and human escalation paths because OpenAI requires ordinary access controls alongside guardrails and recommends human intervention when failures exceed thresholds or actions are high-risk (OpenAI).

Palantir’s AI FDE demonstrates how some of this work is becoming automatable: it can perform data transformations, manage code repositories and ontology, execute under existing user permissions, and validate changes through previews and CI checks (Palantir AI FDE). The same design preserves user permissions, review branches, previews, and CI evidence, so current product evidence supports automation of bounded build and validation steps more strongly than autonomous ownership of architecture or customer risk (Palantir AI FDE).

OT and edge production standards

OT work is not merely an ordinary cloud application installed in a factory because it interacts with the physical environment under distinct performance, reliability, and safety requirements (NIST). NIST defines OT as programmable systems and devices that interact with the physical environment and makes performance, reliability, and safety explicit requirements, while OPC UA supplies an industrial information model and secure interoperability framework across heterogeneous devices and systems (NIST, OPC Foundation). This means the technical baseline must include physical assets, network zones, equipment protocols, information semantics, timing, connectivity, device resources, and the plant’s allowable change and fallback conditions (NIST, OPC Foundation, Palantir Apollo).

Edge inference changes the deployment unit from an API integration into a versioned set of model, runtime, and inference-code components installed on a device, and AWS documents this separation explicitly in Greengrass (AWS). Palantir Apollo shows why the release system must also understand environment-specific constraints and intermittent or absent connectivity when promoting software across on-premises, disconnected, or air-gapped targets (Palantir Apollo). For physical AI, the FDE should therefore provide simulation and hardware-in-the-loop results, compatibility evidence, safety interlocks, manual override and fail-safe behavior, a plant-approved rollback plan, and joint commissioning records in addition to ordinary software tests because the deployed system can directly change devices, processes, or events (NIST).

Expected artifacts and technical scorecard

The following artifact set is a practical synthesis of the production disciplines in the evidence (Google Cloud, Anthropic, NIST).

  • Architecture and interfaces: an as-is and target architecture, data-flow and trust-boundary views, architecture decision records, schema and API contracts, component ownership, and environment topology because current cloud guidance emphasizes maintained documentation, common standards, and separable components (Google Cloud).

  • Build and release: production source code, dependency manifests, configuration or infrastructure definitions, environment constraints, automated build and review evidence, release-channel or equivalent promotion rules, rollback instructions, and deployment records because OpenAI requires production-grade code and Palantir demonstrates constraint-aware promotion across heterogeneous environments (OpenAI, Palantir Apollo).

  • Data and model integration: source inventories, schema mappings, pipeline logic, data-quality checks, model and runtime versions, tool definitions, context sources, and permission boundaries because OpenAI treats tools as standardized integrations with external systems and AWS separates edge models, runtimes, and inference code into deployable components (OpenAI, AWS).

  • Test and evaluation: conventional unit and integration evidence plus agent task datasets, repeated trials, traces, graders, outcome checks, regression results, and escalation cases because Anthropic identifies the trace and resulting environment state as separate objects of evaluation (Anthropic).

  • OT commissioning, where applicable: asset and network topology, protocol and semantic mapping, device and firmware compatibility, edge packages, connectivity and timing assumptions, physical-safety controls, rollback, and commissioning sign-off because OT systems interact with the physical environment under reliability and safety constraints (NIST, OPC Foundation).

A technical scorecard should measure time from approved scope to a deployable increment, automated quality-gate pass rate, escaped defects and rollbacks, integration and data-quality failures, agent evaluation and regression performance, latency and cost per completed task, tool-error rate, recovery indicators, reproducibility across target environments, and reuse of connectors, tests, and deployment components; these measures follow from OpenAI’s prototype-to-production ownership, Google’s small-change discipline, Anthropic’s evaluation model, and the componentized deployment patterns documented by AWS and Palantir (OpenAI, Google Cloud, Anthropic, AWS, Palantir Apollo). Code volume and architecture complexity should not be treated as success because the evidence instead emphasizes stable production, controlled change, verified outcomes, and reusable components (OpenAI, Google Cloud, Anthropic).

Korea-specific execution and market implications

OpenAI’s live Seoul position is direct evidence that global frontier-model providers are localizing hands-on technical deployment in Korea: the role is based in Seoul, expects substantial customer travel, and owns end-to-end production work with customer engineering and domain teams (OpenAI). This is narrow-market FDE capacity rather than a generic sales presence because the job requires full-stack production code and technical ownership from prototype to stable production (OpenAI).

Korea also has functional substitutes with hybrid and physical-AI execution capability (LG CNS). LG CNS states that AgenticWorks supports design, build, operations, and management across both on-premises and cloud environments, and its 2025 physical-AI work included simulation-generated synthetic data, training, autonomous mobile robots, and humanoid robotics (LG CNS). The combination indicates that Korean competition for technical deployment work will include local teams able to integrate enterprise agents in constrained environments and teams able to connect AI to logistics and factory systems, even when they do not use the FDE title (LG CNS, NIST).

For Korean manufacturers, an FDE or substitute team should be prepared for multi-vendor industrial semantics, edge inference, segmented or disconnected environments, and plant-specific reliability and safety conditions because OPC UA addresses cross-system interoperability, AWS supports local edge inference, and Palantir supports on-premises and air-gapped deployment constraints (OPC Foundation, AWS, Palantir Apollo). For regulated knowledge-work buyers, the technical design should support constrained deployment options, enterprise-system integration, traceable tool permissions, and evaluation of real workflow outcomes because LG CNS advertises both on-premises and cloud agent deployments and the agent guidance from OpenAI and Anthropic requires access controls, traces, and outcome-based evaluation (LG CNS, OpenAI, Anthropic).

The recent trajectory is toward convergence rather than one standardized FDE stack (OpenAI, Anthropic, Palantir AI FDE, LG CNS). OpenAI’s Seoul role combines software, LLM, customer, and production delivery skills; Anthropic’s January 2026 guidance makes multi-turn traces and outcome evaluation central to agent engineering; Palantir’s AI FDE automates portions of data, repository, and validation work; and LG CNS is developing both hybrid agentic-AI and physical-AI deployment capabilities in Korea (OpenAI, Anthropic, Palantir AI FDE, LG CNS). Automation should absorb more bounded transformation, integration, and validation work, but heterogeneous customer environments and OT safety constraints preserve the need for engineers who can make architecture, permission, evaluation, and rollout trade-offs (Palantir AI FDE, NIST, Palantir Apollo).

Tensions:

  • Prototype speed versus production discipline. OpenAI expects prototype-to-stable-production ownership, while Google emphasizes small controlled changes, maintained documentation, and independently manageable components; the synthesis is that a successful demo is evidence for further engineering, not proof of production readiness (OpenAI, Google Cloud).

  • Customization versus product leverage. Direct code and environment integration remove customer blockers, but the production guidance favors decoupled components, standardized tools, and reusable deployment mechanisms rather than unmanaged customer forks (Google Cloud, OpenAI, AWS).

  • Single-agent simplicity versus multi-agent specialization. OpenAI recommends maximizing one agent first, although complex logic or unreliable tool selection can justify coordinated agents; the trade-off is greater specialization at the cost of more orchestration and evaluation surface (OpenAI, Anthropic).

  • Agent autonomy versus control. Tool-enabled agents can change external state, but OpenAI requires layered guardrails and conventional access controls, while Palantir’s AI FDE remains bounded by user permissions, branches, previews, and CI checks (OpenAI, Palantir AI FDE).

  • Transcript quality versus real outcome. Anthropic’s evaluation model distinguishes a complete execution trace from the actual state produced, so production acceptance must test both how the agent acted and whether the intended external result exists (Anthropic).

  • Cloud iteration versus OT constraints. Cloud engineering favors frequent small releases, while OT and disconnected environments impose performance, reliability, safety, connectivity, accreditation, and local change constraints that can require edge execution and controlled release windows (Google Cloud, NIST, Palantir Apollo).

  • Central training versus local inference. AWS supports cloud-trained models deployed for local inference, which reduces dependence on round trips but adds model, runtime, inference-code, device, and fleet-version obligations (AWS).

  • Automating FDE work versus retaining engineering judgment. Palantir’s AI FDE can automate transformations, repository operations, and validation, but its permission and review controls indicate augmentation of bounded implementation steps rather than autonomous ownership of architecture or customer risk (Palantir AI FDE).

2.3 Domain translation and workflow redesign

Domain translation is the bridge between an important business problem and a system that can be built, tested, accepted, and operated: OpenAI’s Seoul FDE owns discovery, technical scoping, system design, build, and production rollout while working directly with customer engineering and domain teams (OpenAI). Its success measures include production adoption, measurable workflow impact, and evaluation-driven feedback, making translation part of outcome ownership rather than an upstream requirements handoff (OpenAI). Scale’s Forward Deployed Product Manager makes the interpretive requirement even more explicit: the role turns messy operational reality into platform input and must distinguish a customer’s stated request from the actual problem and the appropriate platform response (Scale AI).

This means an FDE should not behave as a message relay or implement every requested feature literally; the role must interpret the workflow, expose constraints and tradeoffs, and decide whether the blocker belongs to the product, data, integration, execution, or organizational change (Scale AI). Simply adding generative AI to an unchanged process is argued to produce only incremental impact, so discovery should be followed by deliberate workflow reconfiguration rather than direct automation of the status quo (McKinsey). ⚠️ McKinsey’s 2025 survey reinforces the strategic importance of that work: among 25 tested attributes, workflow redesign had the strongest relationship with reported EBIT impact from generative AI, although only 21% of respondents at AI-using organizations said at least some workflows had been fundamentally redesigned (McKinsey).

Required discovery and redesign practice

  1. Frame the outcome and accountable owner. The team should begin with the business or service outcome, affected users, baseline process KPI, scope boundary, constraints, and the decision that the deployment must support, because OpenAI evaluates FDE work through workflow impact and production adoption rather than prototype completion alone (OpenAI).

  2. Establish current-state truth. Process discovery should combine stakeholder and frontline input with operational evidence; Microsoft process mining uses system-of-record event data to visualize how processes actually execute and supports root-cause analysis and KPI monitoring (Microsoft). Event data can reveal variants, delay, and bottlenecks, while customer domain teams remain necessary to interpret why those patterns exist and which unlogged rules or exceptions matter (Microsoft, OpenAI).

  3. Model the as-is workflow and diagnose the real constraint. The FDE or paired product lead should capture tasks, roles, systems, decision points, handoffs, exceptions, controls, and outcome measures, then determine whether the binding constraint is the platform, integration, data, execution, or organizational change rather than accepting the first stated request (Scale AI).

  4. Design the to-be workflow from the outcome backward. Current McKinsey guidance finds a stronger value relationship for reimagining workflows end to end than for inserting AI tools into existing processes, so the target design should reconsider the full sequence of work and not merely automate isolated steps (McKinsey). One proposed method decomposes existing roles into tasks before reconstructing roles around the new workflow, but this specific design prescription rests on medium-confidence secondary evidence (McKinsey). ⚠️

  5. Define human and AI decision boundaries. Business and technology teams should jointly define the new way of working, with the business side responsible for expected business results and the technology side responsible for architectural feasibility (McKinsey). The redesigned workflow should therefore specify where AI proposes, decides, executes, or escalates and which customer authority approves exceptions or high-impact actions (McKinsey, OpenAI).

  6. Translate the workflow into a technical roadmap. The roadmap should connect each workflow capability and constraint to system design, data and integration work, evaluation, and rollout, because the Seoul FDE mandate spans technical scoping through production rather than ending at a requirements document (OpenAI). Scale’s role design further requires the translation to produce high-signal platform input rather than an unfiltered list of customer feature requests (Scale AI).

  7. Turn requirements into layered acceptance criteria before implementation. IIBA defines acceptance criteria as conditions used to determine whether the solution reaches the desired post-implementation state and explicitly links those criteria to approval points (IIBA). Efficient acceptance criteria depend on verified and validated requirements, so the FDE should preserve traceability from the business outcome through the workflow requirement and technical component to the approving test (IIBA).

  8. Encode domain judgment in evaluations. AWS recommends that domain experts create canonical examples covering core functionality and critical business requirements and that the resulting evaluation dataset be diverse and representative of real-world use (AWS evaluation guidance). For an AI deployment, business acceptance should therefore be supplemented by domain cases, edge cases, representative failures, and measurable system-quality thresholds rather than relying on a generic model score (AWS evaluation guidance, IIBA).

  9. Iterate and close the product loop. OpenAI expects evaluation-driven field feedback to influence product and model roadmaps, while Scale expects operational reality to be converted into platform input, so account learning should end in either a customer-specific backlog or a reusable platform signal with the relevant tradeoffs made explicit (OpenAI, Scale AI).

Division of domain expertise

The evidence does not support making the FDE the sole domain expert: AWS recommends a cross-functional team of data engineers, AI specialists, and domain experts and assigns that team responsibility for validating data quality and alignment between model behavior and business requirements (AWS data strategy). Domain experts should own the canonical cases and critical business requirements that define what good looks like, while the FDE should acquire enough fluency to challenge assumptions and convert that knowledge into system behavior and evaluations (AWS evaluation guidance, OpenAI).

The customer business or process owner should retain authority over the target outcome and approval, while business and technology teams jointly define the new workflow and the technology side ensures architectural feasibility (McKinsey, IIBA). The FDE or FDPM/FDE pair should own synthesis across stakeholders, technical feasibility, roadmap coherence, evaluation implementation, and routing of product versus execution blockers (OpenAI, Scale AI). Core product teams should receive the reusable signal, while customer SMEs continue to provide the field-specific judgment that cannot safely be inferred by the vendor (Scale AI, AWS evaluation guidance).

What companies should expect and measure

An effective FDE should be able to structure an ambiguous problem, interpret customer reality rather than relay it, move between business outcomes and production system design, and work directly with both engineering and domain stakeholders (OpenAI, Scale AI). Companies should assess whether the role produces validated workflow impact, production adoption, representative domain evaluations, and actionable product feedback rather than measuring only workshops, documentation, prototypes, or feature volume (OpenAI, AWS evaluation guidance). Useful leading measures include time to a validated workflow and bounded roadmap, coverage of critical variants and exceptions, traceability from outcome to acceptance test, and the proportion of evaluation cases authored from real domain evidence (Microsoft, IIBA, AWS evaluation guidance). Useful outcome measures include production adoption, process-KPI movement, acceptance performance on critical scenarios, and the quality of reusable platform input (OpenAI, Microsoft, Scale AI).

Korea applicability

OpenAI’s current Seoul opening provides direct evidence that a global model provider expects local FDE capacity to own discovery and work with customer domain and engineering teams in Korea (OpenAI). The competitive substitute is already broader than titled FDE roles: Samsung SDS announced an AI-agent banking program for Woori Bank covering more than 175 agents and 29 core tasks, with the scope selected from field feedback and the existing business process planned for redesign around AI agents (Samsung SDS). The announcement demonstrates a Korean SI-led model in which process redesign, agent implementation, and existing-system integration are delivered together, but it is evidence of planned scope rather than independent proof of realized efficiency or durable adoption (Samsung SDS).

For Korean deployments, the operating implication is that the team must preserve customer ownership of domain truth and approval while coordinating model-platform expertise, local system integration, and business-process knowledge (OpenAI, AWS data strategy, IIBA). A credible Korea pod can therefore combine a technically strong FDE, customer SMEs and process owners, a workflow or product lead, and local integration capability rather than requiring one engineer to hold every form of industry and system expertise (OpenAI, AWS data strategy, Samsung SDS).

Tensions:

  • As-is evidence versus zero-based design: process mining exposes real execution and root causes, but the stronger value relationship is associated with end-to-end reimagination rather than inserting AI into the current workflow unchanged (Microsoft, McKinsey).

  • Provider outcome ownership versus customer authority: OpenAI expects the FDE to own workflow impact, while IIBA ties acceptance to validated requirements and stakeholder approval, so technical accountability cannot replace the customer’s business decision rights (OpenAI, IIBA).

  • Generalist speed versus specialist accuracy: an FDE must translate ambiguity quickly, yet AWS places domain experts inside the cross-functional validation team and makes them responsible for canonical evaluation cases (Scale AI, AWS data strategy, AWS evaluation guidance).

  • Rapid iteration versus stable acceptance: evaluation-driven product learning requires iteration, while acceptance criteria require verified requirements and clear approval points, so criteria must remain stable enough to prevent post hoc success definitions while allowing genuine domain learning to be incorporated explicitly (OpenAI, IIBA).

  • Account fidelity versus product leverage: Scale expects detailed customer reality to change platform decisions, but the role must distinguish the actual problem from the requested feature so local exceptions do not automatically become core product scope (Scale AI).

  • Korean ambition versus outcome proof: Samsung SDS documents a large, field-informed, agent-centered redesign for Woori Bank, but the evidence set contains no independent post-deployment result demonstrating realized speed, quality, risk performance, or sustained use (Samsung SDS).

Unresolved questions:

  • The reviewed FDE and FDPM role descriptions do not establish the optimal split of workflow, product, engineering, and industry expertise among an individual FDE, a paired deployment pod, consultants, and customer SMEs by sector or engagement size (OpenAI, Scale AI).

  • The reviewed employer and delivery guidance does not disclose standard discovery effort, customer-SME allocation, translation staffing ratios, billing treatment, or the margin effect of workflow-redesign work (OpenAI, Scale AI, AWS data strategy).

  • As of 2026-07-19, the Woori Bank evidence describes a planned, phased program and does not yet answer whether the deployment will realize its projected efficiency, sustain adoption, manage risk, or create reusable product leverage (Samsung SDS).

2.4 Adoption, change management, and capability transfer

Forward deployment should be judged by sustained use in a production workflow, not by license assignment, training attendance, or a successful demonstration. OpenAI’s Seoul FDE role makes this boundary explicit by measuring the engineer on “production adoption” and “measurable workflow impact,” while Microsoft’s adoption model defines AI use as a habit and measures it over a rolling 28-day period (OpenAI; Microsoft). Capability transfer is the corresponding exit condition: the customer must be able to govern, support, train for, and improve the deployed capability without depending on the original FDE for routine operation, which is consistent with Palantir’s definition of customer autonomy across administration, technology management, program management, training, and use-case development (Palantir).

The evidence supports a real but bounded adoption mandate for the FDE. OpenAI expects its Seoul FDE to guide adoption of what the engineer builds, measure production adoption and workflow impact, and codify successful patterns into reusable tools and playbooks (OpenAI). Databricks places similar responsibilities in its embedded Delivery Solutions Architect service: aligning people, processes, and technology; executing a tailored enablement plan; building champions and centers of excellence; and tracking adoption, progress, and risk across teams (Databricks). These role definitions make adoption part of technical delivery rather than a post-launch customer-success afterthought (OpenAI; Databricks).

They do not make the FDE the customer’s permanent change office. OpenAI’s 2026 Deployment Company model describes operating-transformation and change-management expertise as complementary to technical, product, and frontier-AI deployment expertise, and says the combined model is intended to drive team adoption and durable operating change (OpenAI). Palantir likewise recommends clear owners and decision makers for each program process and ongoing feedback loops between the customer program team, domain leads, and users (Palantir). The practical division of responsibility is therefore:

  • The FDE owns the technical adoption loop: workflow and usability barriers, product and integration changes, adoption instrumentation, technical documentation, administrator and champion enablement, go-live support, and the path to stable production (OpenAI; Databricks).

  • The customer’s executive sponsor and business-process owner should own the case for change, target workflow, staff time, policy and role decisions, acceptance, incentives, outcome targets, and the named long-term owner; Palantir’s autonomy model assigns the customer program team responsibility for use-case intake, user cultivation and training, and end-to-end use-case development (Palantir).

  • A dedicated change, learning, customer-success, or transformation team should own stakeholder mapping, sponsor and manager activation, broad communications, learning logistics, resistance management, champion communities, and reinforcement when the rollout crosses functions or materially changes work (OpenAI; BCG).

  • The customer’s platform, support, or operations team should own routine administration, support, governance, and improvement after transfer, while the contract should state which exceptional problems continue to escalate to the provider (Palantir; Palantir).

This separation matters because the main adoption barriers are organizational as well as technical. McKinsey reports that 48% of US employees would use generative AI more often if they received formal training and 45% would use it more frequently if it were integrated into daily workflows (McKinsey). BCG’s 2026 survey found that only 36% of respondents felt adequately upskilled, only one-third of frontline employees considered leadership communication about AI clear, and only 28% saw strong alignment between what leaders said and what the organization did (BCG). McKinsey also recommends inviting employees to create agents and identify where AI should enter their workflows, but this is prescriptive guidance rather than strong comparative outcome evidence (McKinsey). ⚠️

An effective operating cadence should start with an adoption charter naming the affected groups, executive sponsor, process owner, change lead, technical owner, steady-state operator, baseline, target behavior, value measure, risk owner, and exit model. That recommendation follows Palantir’s emphasis on explicit process ownership and feedback loops and Databricks’ requirement for coordinated enablement and adoption tracking (Palantir; Databricks). Frontline users should participate during workflow design and pilot testing, while training should be role-specific, use realistic work and failure cases, and continue through office hours, champions, manager reinforcement, and updated material after releases; OECD–Korea Labor Institute research reports better worker outcomes when employees are consulted about workplace AI and receive training (OECD/Korea Labor Institute). The same study recommends informing and consulting workers and their representatives during AI adoption and involving social partners in AI-literacy training for workers and management (OECD/Korea Labor Institute).

Adoption measurement should connect reach, behavior, capability, and business outcomes. Reach measures can include eligible, enabled, and trained users; behavioral measures can include first-value completion, weekly active share, repeat-use cohorts, successful task completion, abandonment, overrides, support demand, and time to proficiency; capability-transfer measures can include customer-led releases, customer-resolved tickets, independently executed evaluations, trained operators and champions, and declining dependence on the FDE. Microsoft’s current implementation provides one concrete behavioral benchmark: its AI adoption score uses three active Copilot days per week because users crossing that threshold have a high likelihood of consistent long-term engagement, and it uses feature-level insights to identify areas that may need more training (Microsoft). No usage-frequency threshold proves business value by itself, so teams should pair behavioral data with workflow outcomes such as cycle time, quality, error and rework, risk events, user sentiment, and financial value relative to baseline. Microsoft’s documentation itself pairs usage reporting with sentiment questions about work quality, effort, speed, and productivity (Microsoft).

A handoff should be treated as an operating-readiness gate. Palantir’s autonomy model requires customer ownership across data management, technology management, program management, training, and use-case development, and its later-stage model states that embedded center-of-excellence personnel are temporary and cannot be responsible for long-term use-case ownership (Palantir; Palantir). It also recommends explicitly deciding what support will be developed internally and what support Palantir will continue to provide (Palantir). A defensible exit gate should therefore require a named and funded owner, stable production, current architecture and operating documentation, trained administrators and support staff, a working intake and escalation path, customer-led execution of routine changes and evaluations, adoption and outcome dashboards, explicit acceptance of residual risk, and a post-handoff review. Continued vendor operation can be a valid managed-service choice, but it should be contracted and measured as continuing service rather than described as completed capability transfer.

Korea already exhibits both the direct-FDE and bundled-substitute models. OpenAI’s Seoul role explicitly combines customer embedding, adoption guidance, production adoption, workflow impact, and substantial travel (OpenAI). Samsung SDS says its 2026 Korean AX offer integrates consulting, development and operations, cloud, and security from initial strategy through deployment, enterprise-wide expansion, and operational improvement (Samsung SDS). Samsung SDS also advertises a pre-adoption bootcamp consisting of a one-month trial and three days of dedicated support (Samsung SDS). Its Icraft announcement links strategic consulting to departmental use-case discovery and enterprise-wide diffusion, but the statement is provider-authored intent rather than independently verified adoption evidence (Samsung SDS). ⚠️

For Korean buyers, this means competition is partly about who can supply the adoption system around the technology. A global model provider may supply the FDE and platform knowledge; a Korean SI or consultancy may supply local security, language, account access, training, and organizational rollout; and the buyer’s internal AX, IT, HR, labor-relations, risk, and business teams must supply authority and durable ownership. OECD–Korea Labor Institute evidence makes consultation and training especially important for workplace AI, while Samsung SDS’s offer demonstrates that local providers are already packaging pre-adoption support and enterprise expansion into commercial delivery (OECD/Korea Labor Institute; Samsung SDS). A three-party delivery pod should assign one owner for business adoption, one for technical delivery, and one for steady-state operation to prevent provider, integrator, and customer responsibilities from becoming interchangeable.

Tensions:

  • Adoption ownership versus organizational authority. The FDE must observe usage, remove technical barriers, and guide adoption, but customer leaders or authorized change teams must own policy, incentives, communications, and long-term process accountability (OpenAI; OpenAI).

  • Usage versus value. Repeat use is more meaningful than licenses or attendance, but even frequent use can be low-value or mandatory; Microsoft’s habit metric should therefore be read as a leading indicator rather than proof of outcome (Microsoft).

  • Reusable enablement versus local specificity. Provider playbooks and champion models improve scale, while Korean adoption still requires role-specific workflows, language, policy, and realistic local cases (OpenAI; Databricks; OECD/Korea Labor Institute).

  • Speed versus participation. Embedded teams are designed to move quickly, while worker consultation, manager alignment, and role-based training require time; the Korean labor-market evidence associates consultation and training with better worker outcomes (OpenAI; OECD/Korea Labor Institute).

  • Customer autonomy versus managed service. Palantir’s model treats embedded experts as temporary and long-term ownership as a customer responsibility, while also allowing the support split between customer and provider to be designed explicitly (Palantir).

  • Adoption telemetry versus employee trust. Usage and sentiment data can direct training and product changes, but it should be aggregated, purpose-limited, and separated from individual performance assessment; Microsoft’s implementation combines organizational usage insights with sentiment measures and privacy controls (Microsoft).

  • Korean provider ambition versus outcome evidence. Samsung SDS documents bootcamps, end-to-end support, and enterprise-spread objectives, but the cited material does not report independently verified post-handoff retention, internal support performance, or reduced provider dependence (Samsung SDS).

Unresolved questions:

  • What staffing ratio and responsibility split among FDEs, customer-success teams, change specialists, and customer personnel works best by engagement size and industry?

  • What combination of adoption frequency, proficiency, operating readiness, and reduced vendor dependence is sufficient to authorize handoff for different products and workflows?

  • Which Korean deployments can demonstrate independently verified post-handoff retention, workflow outcomes, internal support performance, and customer autonomy?

2.5 Trust, security, privacy, compliance, and model governance

Trust and assurance are part of forward-deployed engineering because the production system is defined not only by the base model, but also by the customer’s data, connected tools, permissions, workflow, users, and consequences; NIST therefore treats AI risk management as continuous work across the full system lifecycle and explicitly includes third-party software, data, and supply-chain risk in governance. (NIST AI RMF Core) OpenAI’s Seoul FDE role reflects this operating reality: the engineer owns the production rollout, works directly with GRC and Security, and is expected to identify risks early without unnecessarily slowing delivery. (OpenAI Seoul FDE) The FDE is consequently an assurance engineer for the system it builds, but not the sole compliance or risk-acceptance authority; NIST assigns responsibility for AI development and deployment risk decisions to executive leadership and requires risk roles and communication lines to be documented and clear. (NIST AI RMF Core)

The practical division of duties is therefore separation with collaboration. The model or platform provider should supply accurate model, service, security, privacy, change, and incident evidence; the FDE should translate the deployment context into controls, tests, documentation, and remediation; independent validators should challenge the resulting system in proportion to its risk; customer legal, privacy, security, model-risk, business, and operational owners should approve their respective domains; and the accountable customer executive should accept residual production risk. (NIST AI RMF Core, NIST Generative AI Profile) This design prevents two common category errors: treating a provider’s platform-level certification as approval of the customer’s application, and treating an external test report as a transfer of the customer’s operational accountability. (NIST AI RMF Core, NIST Generative AI Profile)

Assurance-by-design across the deployment lifecycle

The first task is to establish applicability, scope, and owners before implementation hardens. A deployment inventory should identify the model, application, agents, tools, data sources, users, affected individuals, environments, suppliers, intended uses, prohibited uses, and named owners for control design, evidence production, review, remediation, approval, monitoring, and incident response because NIST requires clear role assignment and lifecycle governance. (NIST AI RMF Core) In Korea, this initial classification should determine whether the service is high-impact AI, generative AI, or a fully automated decision that significantly affects a person’s rights or obligations, because each classification can trigger different notice, explanation, supervision, documentation, or recourse requirements. (Korea AI Basic Act, Personal Information Protection Act Article 37-2)

The second task is to create the risk and data baseline. The evidence pack should contain current system and data-flow diagrams, processing purposes and legal bases, data source and provenance records, quality and sensitivity classifications, residency and retention rules, a third-party component inventory, a threat model, an impact assessment where applicable, and a risk register linking each risk to an owner, control, test, and residual-risk treatment. (NIST AI RMF Core, PIPC generative-AI privacy guide) Korea’s high-impact-AI impact-assessment rules require identification of affected individuals or groups and call for assessment content covering affected rights, socioeconomic effects, use patterns, quantitative or qualitative metrics, result-calculation methods, and prevention, mitigation, and recovery. (Korea AI Basic Act Enforcement Decree) Korean state bodies must also prefer high-impact-AI products or services that have undergone an impact assessment, making the assessment commercially relevant in public-sector procurement as well as useful for internal governance. (Korea AI Basic Act)

The third task is to implement controls in the deployed system. The FDE should convert the risk baseline into identity integration, least-privilege service and tool permissions, environment and tenant separation, secrets and key management, encryption, approved retrieval sources, filtering and data-loss prevention, protected audit trails, retention and deletion workflows, user notice, human confirmation for consequential actions, override and appeal paths, and safe fallback or deactivation mechanisms because the applicable Korean rules require risk management, user protection, human supervision, documentation, and automated-decision recourse. (Korea AI Basic Act, Personal Information Protection Act Article 37-2) PIPC’s 2025 guidance reinforces this lifecycle approach by organizing minimum privacy safeguards across purpose setting, strategy, training and development, and application and management, while recommending CPO-centered governance. (PIPC generative-AI privacy guide)

The fourth task is context-specific evaluation and independent challenge. NIST recommends iterative and documented test, evaluation, validation, and verification before deployment, but warns that existing generative-AI tests can be inadequate or mismatched to the actual deployment context. (NIST Generative AI Profile) A Korean production test program should therefore cover real task quality, Korean-language and subgroup performance, unsupported claims, tool and permission boundaries, privacy leakage, jailbreak and prompt injection, harmful content, bias, misuse, cyber and supply-chain scenarios, human-oversight effectiveness, rollback, and incident response, with acceptance thresholds tied to the severity of business harm rather than a generic benchmark. (NIST Generative AI Profile) Higher-risk systems should receive independent evaluation or red-team work whose type and rigor are proportional to the identified risk. (NIST Generative AI Profile) Korea’s financial sector has already applied this principle operationally: 2024 blind exercises targeted a pool of 83 organizations, directly checked 12, included generative-AI robustness, and prompted immediate remediation where important weaknesses were found. (Financial Security Institute)

The fifth task is to build a production-approval dossier rather than a loose collection of questionnaires. At minimum, the dossier should preserve the approved purpose and scope, classification decision, architecture and data flows, model/data/component/prompt/tool versions, supplier evidence, risk and impact assessments, control and permission matrices, evaluation datasets and thresholds, raw results and limitations, red-team and penetration-test findings, remediation evidence, user notices and explanation materials, named human supervision, incident and rollback plans, monitoring and change-control plans, recourse and decommission procedures, unresolved risks, independent reports, and signed decisions because NIST calls for documented lifecycle governance, evaluation history, incident evidence, and third-party risk management. (NIST AI RMF Core, NIST Generative AI Profile) The Korean decree requires high-impact-AI implementation evidence to be retained for five years and requires publication of specified governance information, including the human supervisor’s name and contact details. (Korea AI Basic Act Enforcement Decree Article 27)

The Korean provider–customer responsibility model

Korea’s 2026 regime gives deployment teams a concrete rule for allocating upstream and downstream duties. An AI utilization business may be treated as having performed specified high-impact-AI measures already performed upstream when it has not materially changed the system’s original purpose or use. (Korea AI Basic Act Enforcement Decree Article 27) The utilization business may request the evidence needed to perform its duties, and the AI developer is expected to cooperate. (Korea AI Basic Act Enforcement Decree Article 27) This rule makes integration architecture consequential: a deployment team should not assume that upstream assurance remains sufficient after changes to purpose, data, retrieval, fine-tuning, tools, permissions, autonomy, user population, or operating environment. (Korea AI Basic Act Enforcement Decree Article 27)

The Personal Information Protection Act adds a separate controller-side responsibility for qualifying automated decisions. Korean data subjects can reject fully automated decisions that significantly affect their rights or obligations, subject to statutory exceptions; controllers must provide human reprocessing or explanation for valid requests absent just cause and must publish the decision criteria, procedure, and personal-data processing method. (Personal Information Protection Act Article 37-2) An FDE can build the logging, explanation, case-management, replay, and human-review mechanisms needed to exercise those rights, but the customer controller remains responsible for operating the process and making the consequential decision. (Personal Information Protection Act Article 37-2, NIST AI RMF Core)

The AI Basic Act itself took effect on January 22, 2026, while the government announced at least a one-year regulatory grace period and opened a support desk providing legal and technical implementation advice. (Korea Policy Briefing) The grace period changes near-term enforcement pressure, but the underlying Act still specifies high-impact-AI risk management, explanations, user protection, human supervision, and documentation, so the period is best treated as a readiness window rather than an exemption from assurance work. (Korea AI Basic Act, Korea Policy Briefing)

What the company should expect from the FDE

An assurance-capable FDE should identify trust and compliance requirements during discovery, translate them into engineering tasks, produce evidence that non-engineering control owners can evaluate, close technical findings, and escalate risks that cannot be reduced within the agreed tolerance. (OpenAI Seoul FDE, NIST AI RMF Core) The role requires secure architecture and coding, identity and access management, privacy engineering, data governance, threat modeling, model and agent evaluation, adversarial testing, auditability, incident design, documentation discipline, and the judgment to separate a remediable engineering defect from a risk-acceptance decision. (NIST AI RMF Core, NIST Generative AI Profile)

The operating scorecard should emphasize evidence and decision quality: systems inventoried and risk-tiered, assurance requirements with named owners, critical findings closed before launch, permission tests passed, representative and adversarial evaluation coverage, residual risks explicitly accepted, supplier obligations mapped, and re-evaluation completed after material changes. (NIST AI RMF Core, NIST Generative AI Profile) No public cross-company evidence establishes universal FDE thresholds for evaluation pass rates, control-closure time, red-team case counts, or assurance staffing ratios. (OpenAI Seoul FDE, NIST Generative AI Profile) ⚠️ Reusable control libraries, evaluation harnesses, permission tests, evidence templates, and responsibility matrices can increase delivery leverage, but executive risk acceptance and independent challenge cannot be reduced to automated box-checking because NIST assigns deployment-risk decisions to leadership and recommends risk-proportionate independent assessment. (NIST AI RMF Core, NIST Generative AI Profile)

Tensions:

  • Speed versus assurance depth. OpenAI expects Seoul FDEs to spot risk early without slowing delivery, while NIST and Korean rules require continuous risk work, documented evaluation, and retained evidence; the practical resolution is to begin assurance during discovery rather than attach it after the prototype. (OpenAI Seoul FDE, NIST AI RMF Core, Korea AI Basic Act Enforcement Decree Article 27)

  • Vendor evidence versus customer accountability. Korea permits limited reliance on upstream high-impact-AI measures when the downstream business has not materially changed purpose or use, but the customer still needs to reassess the allocation when integration changes data, tools, permissions, autonomy, or consequences. (Korea AI Basic Act Enforcement Decree Article 27)

  • Builder ownership versus approval independence. The FDE is positioned to implement and remediate controls, but NIST recommends independent evaluation proportionate to risk and assigns final risk decisions to executive leadership. (NIST AI RMF Core, NIST Generative AI Profile)

  • Generic benchmarks versus contextual evidence. NIST warns that pre-deployment tests can be mismatched to context, so Korean-language, sector, subgroup, tool, and workflow evaluations are more decision-relevant even though they are less standardized. (NIST Generative AI Profile)

  • Human oversight versus ceremonial review. Korean law requires human supervision and gives data subjects automated-decision recourse, but an effective implementation also needs a named owner, accessible evidence, authority to intervene, and a tested replay or override path. (Korea AI Basic Act, Personal Information Protection Act Article 37-2, Korea AI Basic Act Enforcement Decree Article 27)

  • Law in force versus enforcement grace. Korea’s AI Basic Act is operative, but the announced grace period can encourage either orderly capability building or delayed readiness. (Korea AI Basic Act, Korea Policy Briefing)

Unresolved questions:

  • How will Korean authorities apply the material-functional-change rule to RAG, fine-tuning, agent tools, and permission changes?
  • Which approval body and independent-assurance standard will each Korean sector require in practice?
  • What evaluation pass thresholds, control-closure service levels, and assurance staffing ratios will become credible FDE benchmarks?
  • How will enforcement develop after the announced grace period?

2.6 Reliability, observability, support, and operating ownership

Forward-deployed engineering does not end cleanly at go-live: OpenAI’s Seoul FDE role owns technical delivery through “stable production” and is evaluated through production adoption and measurable workflow impact, not simply completion of implementation (OpenAI Seoul FDE role). At the same time, Palantir has a distinct Forward Deployed Reliability Engineer (FDRE) role that owns the stability of mission-critical customer workflows, participates in on-call, prevents recurring field failures, and turns operational learning into product and tooling changes (Palantir FDRE role). Taken together, these role designs support a useful distinction: the FDE should own the reliability consequences of getting its deployment to stable production, while indefinite operational ownership may transfer to a customer team or continue under a dedicated reliability or managed-service function (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Palantir FDRE role).

What production ownership means

Production ownership should be defined as responsibility for a named service and its user-facing outcomes, including monitoring, emergency response, change management, capacity, availability, latency, and efficiency (Google SRE engagement model). Google treats a Production Readiness Review as a prerequisite before an SRE team accepts that responsibility, which makes ownership an explicit operational decision rather than an informal consequence of having helped build the system (Google SRE engagement model). Google also transfers operations, access rights, and change-management responsibilities progressively and keeps the development team available to advise and back up the new operator during the transition (Google SRE engagement model).

Four ownership patterns follow from this evidence: build-and-handoff to the customer, a time-bounded hypercare period, co-managed operations across vendor and customer boundaries, or continuing ownership by an FDRE, SRE, or managed-service provider (Google SRE engagement model; Palantir FDRE role). A build-and-handoff model is appropriate only after the receiving team is operationally ready, while persistent vendor ownership is appropriate when the contract and staffing model explicitly fund ongoing reliability work (Google SRE engagement model; Google Cloud operational-readiness guidance). Because Google does not give every service full SRE support and instead offers documentation or consultation in lower-touch cases, forward-deployed reliability capacity should likewise be tiered rather than assumed for every customer (Google SRE engagement model).

Reliability and observability after launch

For AI systems, infrastructure uptime is necessary but insufficient because post-deployment monitoring must also detect unreliable real-world behavior, unforeseen outputs caused by nondeterminism or dynamic inputs, and unexpected consequences in the deployment context (NIST AI 800-4). NIST finds that validated methods, best practices, and common terminology for this monitoring remain nascent, so the monitoring design must be tailored to the workflow and its risk rather than copied from a universal template (NIST AI 800-4).

Observability means being able to infer a system’s internal state from its outputs, and an observable application must emit traces, metrics, or logs that an operator can inspect (OpenTelemetry). For a forward-deployed AI system, the practical monitoring path should therefore connect infrastructure, application, data, retrieval, model, agent, tool, cost, and user-workflow signals rather than stop at a healthy API (OpenTelemetry; NIST AI 800-4).

The operating team should define service-level indicators for availability, success rate, latency, throughput, saturation, freshness, and recovery performance, while AI workflows additionally require task completion, tool-call success, human handoff, evaluation regression, drift, cost, and correction or override signals (Google Cloud operational-readiness guidance; NIST AI 800-4). Alerts should be tied to symptoms and end-to-end customer experience, be actionable for the on-caller, and be supported by current debugging and mitigation playbooks (Google incident-management guide).

A strong telemetry design should correlate the release, environment, tenant, session, request, model or prompt version, retrieval source, tool invocation, resulting state, cost, and user outcome so an operator can trace a failure across organizational boundaries (OpenTelemetry; NIST AI 800-4). Because observability data can include customer content and non-provider systems for which the customer retains responsibility, the contract and access model should define what may be collected and who may inspect it (OpenAI Services Agreement).

Incident response and the field-to-product loop

Operational readiness requires a reliable alerting mechanism, a defined on-call process, and up-to-date playbooks before the first serious incident occurs (Google incident-management guide). During a major incident, Google separates the Incident Commander, Communications Lead, and Operations Lead so coordination, stakeholder updates, and technical mitigation do not compete for the same person’s attention (Google incident-management guide). A forward-deployed team should adopt the same separation when an incident crosses the model provider, application, data, infrastructure, and customer business process (Google incident-management guide).

The FDE or FDRE should not only restore service but also diagnose recurring field problems, automate or streamline their resolution, document the resulting practice, and send structural defects into product and internal-tooling roadmaps (Palantir FDRE role). OpenAI similarly expects its Seoul FDE to codify successful deployment patterns into reusable tools and playbooks, reinforcing that operational learning should reduce future deployment effort rather than remain private account knowledge (OpenAI Seoul FDE role).

An appropriate post-incident cycle is therefore to stabilize the workflow, communicate user impact, preserve diagnostic evidence, restore or degrade safely, document the incident, assign corrective actions, regression-test the fix, and update monitoring and runbooks (Google incident-management guide; Palantir FDRE role). The company should evaluate this work through SLO attainment, detection and recovery time, repeat-incident rate, user impact, action closure, manual toil removed, reusable fixes, and support cost per deployment rather than ticket volume alone (Google SRE engagement model; Palantir FDRE role).

Support obligations and handoff criteria

Google Cloud recommends SLOs and SLAs for critical workloads and states that an SLA should specify availability, performance, support, provider and customer responsibilities, and remedies for noncompliance (Google Cloud operational-readiness guidance). An FDE engagement should consequently define supported components and environments, coverage hours and languages, severity levels, acknowledgement and update targets, restoration or resolution objectives, escalation paths, maintenance windows, exclusions, customer prerequisites, change policies, diagnostic access, postmortem delivery, service remedies, and exit assistance (Google Cloud operational-readiness guidance).

The contractual boundary is especially important for model deployments because OpenAI’s business agreement makes the customer responsible for evaluating output accuracy and appropriateness and disclaims responsibility for customer content and non-OpenAI services (OpenAI Services Agreement). This means that an FDE’s success metric of workflow impact does not by itself transfer legal or operational accountability for the customer’s full application stack to the model provider (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; OpenAI Services Agreement). Any broader obligation must therefore be made explicit in the order form, statement of work, managed-service agreement, or SLA (Google Cloud operational-readiness guidance; OpenAI Services Agreement).

A defensible handoff should occur only after the receiving operator has accepted the SLOs, telemetry, runbooks, architecture, access rights, escalation contacts, recovery procedures, known risks, and support backlog, and has demonstrated operational readiness through training or exercises (Google SRE engagement model; Google incident-management guide). The transfer should be progressive, with the FDE or development team remaining available as backup until the new operator can manage production independently (Google SRE engagement model).

Korea-specific operating model

Korea already shows both localized FDE launch ownership and local managed-operations capacity: OpenAI’s Seoul FDE role owns delivery through stable production, while LG CNS offers AI and cloud services that extend into monitoring and operations (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; LG CNS AgenticWorks; LG CNS cloud operations). LG CNS says AgenticWorks covers planning through operation, includes evaluation and monitoring, and supports both cloud and on-premises deployment (LG CNS AgenticWorks). Its cloud practice separately advertises integrated monitoring and tuning from infrastructure and applications through databases, a dedicated Technical Account Manager for proactive operations, and integrated management of applications and security solutions (LG CNS cloud operations).

This evidence supports a layered Korean delivery model in which a global FDE retains product and architecture escalation while a Korean MSP, SI, or in-house team owns first-line operations and local infrastructure (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; LG CNS cloud operations). Cloud and on-premises support in the same platform makes explicit allocation of local access, monitoring, pager, and vendor-escalation responsibility especially important (LG CNS AgenticWorks; Google Cloud operational-readiness guidance).

Market direction

The emerging role family separates deployment build from durable reliability while keeping the feedback loop intact: OpenAI’s Seoul FDE owns the path to stable production, and Palantir’s FDRE institutionalizes on-call, prevention, documentation, and product learning after launch (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Palantir FDRE role). NIST’s 2026 work indicates that AI reliability will expand beyond conventional uptime into ongoing observation of nondeterministic outputs and deployment-context consequences, even though validated practices remain immature (NIST AI 800-4). The most scalable likely structure is automated telemetry and evaluation as a baseline, customer or partner first-line operations, dedicated reliability capacity for critical systems, and FDE or product escalation for structural defects (OpenTelemetry; Google SRE engagement model; Palantir FDRE role).

Tensions: The role evidence creates a real divide between production-impact accountability and legal or contractual accountability: an FDE can be measured on adoption while the customer remains responsible for output evaluation and non-provider systems (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; OpenAI Services Agreement). High-context FDE support can shorten diagnosis, but indefinite bespoke on-call consumes scarce reliability capacity and weakens software leverage, which is why lower-touch documentation, consultation, partner support, and explicit support tiers remain necessary (Palantir FDRE role; Google SRE engagement model). Rich traces improve diagnosis, yet customer content and non-provider components remain subject to customer responsibility and must be governed accordingly (OpenTelemetry; OpenAI Services Agreement). Finally, strict production-readiness gates reduce avoidable incidents, while NIST finds that nondeterminism and dynamic inputs make some failure modes visible only after deployment (Google SRE engagement model; NIST AI 800-4).

Unresolved questions: What universal boundary, if any, should separate FDE launch responsibility from the long-term pager and SLA? What are the cross-company staffing ratios, support margins, pages per engineer, and incident benchmarks for FDE or FDRE teams? What Korean-language support targets, handoff checklists, and pricing models are becoming standard in Korea? Which AI post-deployment monitoring methods will become validated common practice? How will long-run operating ownership divide among global-vendor FDEs, Korean MSP or SI providers, and buyer-owned SRE or AI-platform teams?

3. Company expectations and talent system

Define the competencies, performance system, organizational placement, and career conditions companies use to build and manage FDE capacity.

The FDE talent system is best designed around a common delivery spine plus product- and industry-specific specialization: current employers converge on production engineering, customer-problem translation, end-to-end ownership, communication across technical and business audiences, and judgment under ambiguity, while differing materially in the depth of LLM, data-platform, SaaS-integration, and industrial-AI expertise they require (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Palantir Korea FDSE, Notion Korea FDE, MakinaRocks LLM FDE). Companies should therefore hire against observable evidence that a candidate personally built, integrated, deployed, and improved a real system rather than relying on the FDE title, employer prestige, tenure, or technical presentation alone (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Notion Korea FDE, MakinaRocks LLM FDE). Structured, job-related interviews and bounded work samples are the most defensible selection approach because OPM reports that interview structure improves validity and rater agreement, while a 2022 meta-analysis found that earlier selection-validity estimates were often overstated even though structured interviews ranked highest among the reviewed methods (OPM structured interviews, Sackett et al.).

Korea adds a constrained intersection of skills rather than a separate competency model: current Seoul roles combine production or customer-delivery depth with Korean-English communication and substantial mobility, including 50% expected travel at OpenAI and 25–50% preferred travel at Palantir (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Palantir Korea FDSE, Notion Korea FDE). The local supply strategy should combine experienced anchors from software, applied AI, cloud, professional services, SI, and in-house platform teams with junior pipelines and multidisciplinary pods, because current Korea postings range from one-year junior entry to five-plus-year senior requirements (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Reflection Applied AI, MakinaRocks junior ML/DS FDE).

Performance should be evaluated as a balanced portfolio of durable customer outcome, production adoption, delivery quality, reliability, reusable leverage, field-to-product learning, capability transfer, and commercial contribution rather than as one activity or revenue quota (OpenAI Seoul FDE, OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, Scale FDE Director). Every attractive metric needs a guardrail: speed should be paired with failure and rework, usage with task and workflow value, expansion with realized customer outcome and support load, and reuse with evidence that another deployment actually adopted the artifact (DORA software-delivery metrics, DORA customer feedback, OpenAI Platform FDE Manager). DORA explicitly warns that turning performance metrics directly into goals increases gaming risk, reinforcing the need for multiple measures with productive tension and contextual review (DORA software-delivery metrics).

Career progression should broaden the radius of technical and organizational influence rather than merely increase account size or travel: published expectations move from account-level execution toward hardest-case ownership, cross-account patterns, reference architecture, mentoring, standards, product direction, and portfolio judgment (Okta Principal FDE, Scale FDE Director). A parallel management track should own allocation, hiring quality, coaching, capacity, cross-functional coordination, and the durability of the delivery model, while rotations into Product, Platform, Research Engineering, Solutions, or domain leadership should remain legitimate career moves (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director).

Organizationally, the evidence supports a primary technical home in Engineering, Model Deployment, Technical Success, or an equivalent deployment function, with formal account governance shared with Sales or GTM and mandatory interfaces to Product, Research, Security/GRC, Partnerships, and customer operators (OpenAI Seoul FDE, OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director). Scaling can use a direct core team, centralized deployment unit, controlled subsidiary built partly through acquisition, local joint venture, or ring-fenced partner practice, as shown respectively by OpenAI and Scale, AWS and MakinaRocks, the OpenAI Deployment Company, Palantir’s HD Hyundai entity, and AWS’s partner-led model (OpenAI Deployment Company, AWS Partner-Led FDE, Palantir 2024 Form 10-K, MakinaRocks hiring announcement). The practical default is a persistent engineering home plus temporary account pods, with specialists added by risk and provider staffing reduced as the partner or customer gains operating capability (AWS Partner-Led FDE, Microsoft–EY initiative).

Sustainability must be an operating metric rather than an HR afterthought because current roles combine ambiguity, pressure, concurrent deployments, and material travel, while WHO identifies excessive workload, long or inflexible hours, limited support, unclear roles, and weak career investment as workplace mental-health risks (OpenAI Seoul FDE, OpenAI FDE Manager, WHO mental health at work). A four-wave study found that greater business travel was associated with both higher career satisfaction and higher turnover intentions, so travel can be developmentally valuable without being uniformly sustainable (“Another trip?” study). Leaders should protect capacity for incidents, productization, learning, mentoring, and recovery; make travel predictable; rotate people out of prolonged high-pressure accounts; and reward clean handoff and prevention rather than repeated heroics (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director, WHO mental health at work).

3.1 Competency profile, hiring signals, and talent sources

The FDE hiring profile is best understood as a common delivery spine plus a product- and industry-specific specialization, because current Seoul postings agree on production engineering, customer translation, ownership, and communication while differing sharply on AI, data, SaaS, and industrial depth (OpenAI, Palantir, Notion, Reflection, MakinaRocks). This means companies should define the role through observable work—what the candidate has built, deployed, learned, and changed—rather than through the FDE title, a prestigious employer, or a single educational pedigree (OpenAI, Notion, MakinaRocks).

The non-negotiable competency spine

Production engineering is the first gate: OpenAI requires production-grade frontend and backend code, Palantir expects engineers to solve technical problems across data, infrastructure, and applications, Notion requires production-quality code plus APIs and data integration, and MakinaRocks treats development, deployment, and operation of a usable LLM/VLM service as a preferred signal (OpenAI, Palantir, Notion, MakinaRocks). Architecture slides, notebooks, proofs of concept, and polished demonstrations are therefore insufficient unless the candidate can also show personal responsibility for production code, integration, testing, deployment, or operations (OpenAI, Reflection, MakinaRocks).

Problem translation is the second gate: the candidate must turn an incomplete customer need into a technically coherent scope, explain it to both business and engineering stakeholders, and connect the delivered system to customer value (OpenAI, Notion, Palantir). MakinaRocks similarly asks its LLM FDE candidates to analyze complex problems structurally and lead the derivation and execution of technical solutions, showing that this translation requirement also appears in a Korean industrial-AI employer (MakinaRocks).

Ownership under ambiguity is the third gate: OpenAI asks for evidence of delivering complex systems in fast-moving or ambiguous environments, Palantir values independent learning and decision-making with minimal supervision, and Reflection explicitly selects for high agency where playbooks are still being written (OpenAI, Palantir, Reflection). The relevant signal is not confidence alone but evidence that the candidate made sound scope, quality, risk, and sequencing decisions while remaining accountable for the result (OpenAI, Palantir).

Communication and collaboration are the fourth gate: OpenAI expects clear communication across engineers, product teams, and customers, Palantir expects collaboration with technical and nontechnical people, and Notion requires effective engagement with both technical and business audiences (OpenAI, Palantir, Notion). This capability should be tested as accurate translation, expectation setting, conflict handling, and decision clarity rather than as generic charisma (Notion, Palantir).

Product and commercial judgment form a fifth, near-core layer because current roles connect customer implementation to reusable methods, platform readiness, and measurable customer value rather than treating each engagement as isolated custom work (OpenAI, Notion). The appropriate commercial signal is value framing, qualification, estimation, and stakeholder judgment; quota-carrying sales history is relevant only where the actual FDE role owns that motion (Notion, OpenAI).

Context-specific technical and domain profiles

The technical specialization should follow the deployment surface rather than a universal FDE checklist (Notion, Reflection, MakinaRocks). OpenAI’s Seoul profile requires previous LLM or generative-model delivery as well as full-stack engineering, while Reflection asks for RAG, vector databases, agent orchestration, evaluations, fine-tuning, cloud or hybrid deployment, and modern DevOps practices (OpenAI, Reflection). Notion’s Korea role centers production integrations, migrations, APIs, data pipelines, permissions, governance, and agentic workflows, but explicitly says the candidate need not already be an AI expert if they are curious and willing to adopt AI tools (Notion). MakinaRocks divides the profile further: its LLM FDE emphasizes Python, LLM/VLM application, evaluation and monitoring, data pipelines, and agents, while its junior ML/DS FDE emphasizes manufacturing and logistics prediction, anomaly detection, optimization, simulation, model performance, and operational stability (MakinaRocks LLM FDE, MakinaRocks junior ML/DS FDE).

The current Korea evidence therefore supports at least six recruiting pools: customer-facing production software engineers; applied-AI or ML engineers who have shipped enterprise systems; data and MLOps/platform engineers who can broaden into discovery and applications; hands-on solutions architects or SaaS professional-services engineers; technically deep consultants or SI engineers with personal production ownership; and industrial data scientists who can strengthen their software and customer-delivery practice (OpenAI, Notion, Reflection, MakinaRocks). Research experience is a recognized entry signal for MakinaRocks’s LLM FDE, but that same posting also favors deployed and operated systems, so research pedigree should not substitute for production evidence (MakinaRocks).

Experience thresholds also differ enough to rule out a single seniority template: OpenAI asks for at least five years, Notion for at least four, Reflection for at least three years of AI-enterprise delivery, MakinaRocks’s LLM role for at least two years of project or research experience, and MakinaRocks’s junior ML/DS FDE for at least one year (OpenAI, Notion, Reflection, MakinaRocks LLM FDE, MakinaRocks junior ML/DS FDE). A sustainable talent system should consequently combine experienced anchors with junior pipelines, internal rotations, and multidisciplinary pods instead of relying only on a scarce, fully formed hybrid profile (MakinaRocks junior ML/DS FDE, OpenAI).

Korea-specific filters

Language and deployment mobility are genuine job requirements in the current Seoul market: Palantir requires fluent Korean and English and prefers 25-50% travel, OpenAI expects 50% travel, and Notion requires native-level Korean with business-level spoken English (Palantir, OpenAI, Notion). These filters imply that the immediately deployable Korean pool is narrower than the overall software or AI workforce because candidates must combine technical depth with bilingual stakeholder access and sustained customer-site availability (Palantir, OpenAI, Notion).

Korean hiring exercises should therefore reproduce the real interface: candidates should interpret Korean-language business and operational material, clarify requirements with a Korean stakeholder, and explain architecture or risk in English to a global product or research team when both languages are actually required by the job (Palantir, Notion). The case should also reflect enterprise constraints such as existing systems, data boundaries, integration, permissions, governance, and operational stability because those are explicit parts of the current Korea role set (Notion, Reflection, MakinaRocks junior ML/DS FDE).

What to assess and how

The strongest résumé or portfolio evidence is a concise project chain that identifies the customer or user problem, the candidate’s personal technical decisions, the system actually shipped, the constraints encountered, the measured result, the failures or revisions, and the reusable product or process learning (OpenAI, Notion, MakinaRocks). Brand-name employment, years of service, degree pedigree, algorithm performance, or polished customer presence are incomplete when they are not accompanied by personal production ownership and evidence of translating a real problem into an operating system (Palantir, OpenAI, MakinaRocks).

A defensible selection sequence should contain four distinct samples of the job (Palantir hiring guidance, OPM). First, a technical stage should test coding, debugging, integration, efficiency, and reasoning in an unfamiliar system; Palantir publicly expects every engineering candidate to work through a coding question and frames its process around coding, efficiency, open-ended questions, technical problem solving, and existing systems (Palantir phone interview, Palantir hiring guidance). Second, a bounded FDE case should require the candidate to clarify an incomplete customer brief, decompose the workflow, choose an architecture, build or repair a small production-relevant slice, identify tests and risks, and explain trade-offs to both technical and business stakeholders (OpenAI, Notion, Palantir). Third, a structured behavioral interview should probe past ambiguity, deployment failure, stakeholder conflict, security or reliability trade-offs, rapid domain learning, and a decision not to build an inappropriate request (OPM, OpenAI). Fourth, senior-hire references should verify the candidate’s personal scope, ownership, and delivery behavior, while companies should treat their incremental predictive value as unproven until validated against internal outcomes (OPM, Sackett et al.).

MakinaRocks already uses a pre-assignment followed by technical and fit interviews for its junior ML/DS FDE role, providing a current Korean example of a work-sample-like stage in the selection process (MakinaRocks). The broader personnel-selection evidence favors discipline over intuition: OPM reports that greater interview structure improves validity, rater reliability, rater agreement, and adverse-impact performance, and that questions derived from job analysis and critical competencies perform better (OPM). OPM also reports that behavioral-description interviews have higher validity for highly complex professional and managerial work, which closely matches the ambiguity and cross-functional character employers assign to FDEs (OPM, OpenAI, Palantir).

A 2022 meta-analysis adds an important caution: it concluded that earlier estimates of selection-procedure validity were commonly overstated by 0.10-0.20 points, although structured interviews still ranked highest among the procedures reviewed (Sackett et al.). Employers should consequently use standardized job-related questions, anchored rating scales, independent scoring before panel discussion, and minimum gates for engineering, decomposition, translation, ownership, and communication rather than allowing charisma or one excellent technical score to compensate for a critical deficiency (OPM). The assessment itself should then be validated against probation performance, deployment quality, adoption, reliability, reusable product learning, customer and manager feedback, and retention because the available selection research supports structured assessment in general but does not validate a named company’s FDE loop (OPM, Sackett et al.).

Tensions:

  • A common generalist spine is visible across employers, but frontier-model, SaaS-integration, data-platform, and industrial-AI roles require different technical evidence (OpenAI, Notion, Reflection, MakinaRocks).
  • OpenAI, Notion, and Reflection favor three to five-plus years of experience, while MakinaRocks exposes a one-year junior route, creating a real choice between buying ready-made hybrids and building the pipeline (OpenAI, Notion, Reflection, MakinaRocks).
  • Direct generative-AI depth is mandatory in some roles but explicitly learnable in others, so copying one employer’s AI checklist can exclude suitable candidates for a different deployment surface (OpenAI, Reflection, Notion).
  • Coding tests protect the production-engineering bar, but coding alone does not sample customer discovery, domain translation, delivery judgment, or adoption work that current FDE descriptions also require (Palantir phone interview, OpenAI, Notion).
  • Customer-facing, consulting, and professional-services backgrounds enlarge the pool, but communication polish cannot replace demonstrated production engineering (Notion, OpenAI).
  • Bilingual fluency and 25-50% or greater travel support direct Korean customer access, but they also narrow the pool and may create sustainability and retention risk (Palantir, OpenAI, Notion).
  • Hybrid roles invite personalized evaluation, while the personnel-selection evidence favors job analysis, standardized questions, rating anchors, and constrained interviewer discretion (OPM, Sackett et al.).
  • Employer postings reveal current expectations, while the available general selection research does not establish that these employers’ listed traits or interview methods causally predict successful FDE deployments (OpenAI, Palantir, Sackett et al.).

Unresolved questions: The reviewed personnel-selection evidence is general rather than FDE-specific, leaving the relationship between résumé signals, interview dimensions, work samples, and later FDE outcomes unresolved (OPM, Sackett et al.). The reviewed Korea job postings specify requirements but do not report applicant volumes, acceptance rates, sourcing-channel yield, time-to-hire, bilingual or travel-related attrition, compensation premiums, or validated cut scores (OpenAI, Palantir, Notion, Reflection, MakinaRocks). The detailed interview content and criterion-validity evidence for most Korea FDE employers remain unavailable in the reviewed sources, so the recommended process is an evidence-based synthesis rather than proof of a universally optimal hiring loop (Palantir hiring guidance, MakinaRocks, Sackett et al.).

3.2 Goals, KPIs, evaluation, and promotion

Current employer materials define FDE success much more broadly than “software shipped”: OpenAI’s Seoul role names production adoption, measurable workflow impact, and evaluation-driven product or model feedback, while Scale adds shipping consistency, deployment durability, and the quality of field signal reaching the roadmap (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Scale FDE Director). OpenAI’s enterprise deployment-management role broadens the portfolio scorecard again to include production deployments, usage, adoption, customer outcomes, delivery quality, team capacity, and reusable-work impact (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager). These sources establish stated expectations, but they do not disclose the actual weights, quotas, compensation links, or calibration rules used in employee reviews (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, Scale FDE Director).

The resulting design principle is a balanced scorecard: judge the account outcome and the engineer’s contribution to it, then add explicit measures of production quality, reuse, customer independence, and product learning so that a superficially successful account does not hide fragile or permanently bespoke delivery (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, OpenAI Platform FDE Manager). DORA independently cautions that turning a metric directly into a goal increases gaming risk and recommends multiple measures with healthy tension rather than a single dominant number (DORA software-delivery metrics).

What an FDE scorecard should measure

  1. Production delivery and speed. Measure qualified-problem-to-prototype time, prototype-to-production time, milestone predictability, blocker removal, and service-level delivery flow, but pair speed with failure, rework, adoption, and outcome evidence (OpenAI Seoul FDE, DORA software-delivery metrics). DORA treats software-delivery throughput as a multi-factor construct rather than a single speed metric, which makes it a useful engineering input but not a complete FDE evaluation (DORA software-delivery metrics).

  2. Customer value. Establish a pre-engagement baseline and compare the production workflow against it using the customer’s relevant cycle-time, quality, cost, revenue, risk, or mission outcome, with the customer owner validating the interpretation (OpenAI Seoul FDE, DORA customer feedback). DORA says product-success measures should derive from how customers actually interact with the product and identifies delivery-to-specification as the wrong success test when the customer problem remains unresolved (DORA customer feedback).

  3. Adoption and durable use. Track production usage, repeat use, successful task completion, retention in the intended workflow, spread across the intended teams, and time to first useful result rather than licenses, demos, or one-time activity alone (OpenAI Seoul FDE, OpenAI Startup Deployment Manager). OpenAI’s startup deployment role lists tokens, requests, and production workloads as usage signals, while the Seoul role anchors those signals in workflow impact and adoption (OpenAI Startup Deployment Manager, OpenAI Seoul FDE).

  4. Reliability and production quality. Use workflow-level service objectives, evaluation results, severe incidents, recovery time, deployment rework, cost-to-serve, and closure of corrective actions to prevent “live in production” from being treated as sufficient success (DORA software-delivery metrics, Google SRE error-budget policy). Google’s example error-budget policy freezes ordinary releases after an error-budget breach and requires a postmortem when one incident consumes more than 20% of the four-week budget, illustrating how reliability evidence can change work priorities without becoming punishment for incident reporting (Google SRE error-budget policy).

  5. Commercial contribution. Record production usage, value-backed expansion, referenceability, retention-risk reduction, and long-term account growth as shared outcomes with Sales and Customer Success rather than as sole FDE credit (OpenAI Startup Deployment Manager). OpenAI explicitly expects startup deployment leaders to use technical engagement to accelerate adoption, production usage, and long-term account growth, showing that commercial influence belongs in the role even though it is not fully controlled by the engineer (OpenAI Startup Deployment Manager).

  6. Reuse and reduced account dependence. Count a playbook, reference architecture, tool, or building block only when another deployment adopts it and it improves speed, quality, or supportability (OpenAI Seoul FDE, OpenAI Startup Deployment Manager). OpenAI’s enterprise leadership role explicitly requires distinguishing scalable market patterns from bespoke requests, while its Platform FDE role requires a clear boundary between what generalizes and what remains customer-specific (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, OpenAI Platform FDE Manager).

  7. Product and research learning. Evaluate field feedback by its specificity, supporting evidence, recurrence across accounts, and whether it receives a documented Product or Research disposition rather than by ticket volume alone (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Scale FDE Director). Scale treats the clarity with which field signal sharpens the product roadmap as a leadership success measure, and OpenAI expects evaluation-driven field feedback to change product and model roadmaps (Scale FDE Director, OpenAI Seoul FDE).

  8. Team and operating leverage. At senior levels, assess technical standards, mentoring, decision quality under pressure, cross-functional trust, onboarding, career development, and the ability to make other FDEs more effective (Scale FDE Director, Okta Principal FDE). Scale assigns its FDE director responsibility for onboarding and career frameworks, while Okta expects a Principal FDE to set technical direction across multiple teams or accounts (Scale FDE Director, Okta Principal FDE).

Evaluation mechanics and anti-customization guardrails

A defensible review begins with jointly defined success criteria and continues through delivery, stabilization, and a portfolio-level talent review, because the public role definitions span prototype, stable production, adoption, customer outcome, reusable work, and product signal rather than a single endpoint (OpenAI Seoul FDE, OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager). The evidence should distinguish the gross account result from the FDE’s controllable contribution and from outcomes controlled by Sales, Product, Research, customer teams, or delivery partners (OpenAI Startup Deployment Manager, OpenAI Platform FDE Manager).

Each attractive metric needs a counter-metric: deployment count with adoption and rework; speed with safety and reliability; tokens or requests with task success and value; expansion with customer outcome and support load; utilization with protected reuse and learning time; and handoff with evidence that the receiving team can operate the system (DORA software-delivery metrics, DORA customer feedback, Google SRE error-budget policy). Bespoke work should be classified at intake as customer-specific, potentially reusable, or core-product candidate, because OpenAI’s current management model explicitly asks leaders to distinguish scalable patterns from bespoke requests and make disciplined scale-versus-pause decisions (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, OpenAI Platform FDE Manager). A rising share of one-account exceptions, recurring manual support, or reuse artifacts that no other deployment adopts should therefore lower the evaluation of portfolio leverage even when the focal customer is satisfied (OpenAI Platform FDE Manager, DORA customer feedback).

Promotion paths and senior expectations

The clearest published IC ladder moves from owning execution and outcomes inside an account to owning patterns, standards, and leverage across accounts (Okta Principal FDE). Okta states that a Senior FDE owns the outcome in one account, whereas a Principal FDE owns patterns inherited across accounts and FDEs, leads the hardest deployments, shapes reference architecture and roadmap direction, mentors others, and defines shared evaluation and observability standards (Okta Principal FDE).

The management path adds portfolio allocation, team development, technical escalation, operating mechanisms, cross-functional coordination, and responsibility for a scalable delivery model (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, Scale FDE Director). OpenAI’s Platform FDE manager is expected to coach through direct feedback and clear expectations while making productization and handoff decisions, and Scale’s director must set the technical bar and build onboarding and career frameworks (OpenAI Platform FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director).

Not every company presents progression as a rigid ladder: Palantir says it emphasizes strengths, interests, and multiple pathways rather than relying on traditional career ladders, while still expecting impact, end-to-end ownership, autonomy, and independent judgment (Palantir FDSE). The common seniority signal across explicit and flexible models is broader scope of influence: harder cases, stronger technical judgment, reusable standards, mentoring, portfolio trade-offs, executive credibility, and product influence rather than merely a larger account or more travel (Okta Principal FDE, Scale FDE Director, Palantir FDSE).

Korea application

The strongest direct Korea evidence is OpenAI’s Seoul FDE posting, which expects ownership across multiple deployments through stable production and measures the role on production adoption, workflow impact, reuse, and product or research feedback (OpenAI Seoul FDE). A Korea scorecard should retain those global categories while documenting customer-controlled approval delays, local risk and governance gates, Korean-language workflow quality, partner handoffs, and the split of accountability among vendor, local delivery provider, and buyer team so that raw speed or revenue comparisons do not erase delivery context (OpenAI Seoul FDE, DORA software-delivery metrics). Public evidence does not establish whether Korean employers outside global vendors operate formal FDE-specific ladders, promotion committees, or calibrated scorecards (OpenAI Seoul FDE).

Tensions:

  • Outcome accountability versus controllability: FDEs are expected to own durable customer impact, but Product, Sales, customer teams, and governance owners control parts of the result, so reviews should separate gross outcome from attributable contribution (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, OpenAI Startup Deployment Manager).
  • Revenue influence versus trusted judgment: Account growth is an explicit deployment objective, but a pure individual revenue quota can conflict with evidence-driven scope and anti-gaming discipline (OpenAI Startup Deployment Manager, DORA software-delivery metrics).
  • Customer depth versus software leverage: Deep account work produces adoption and insight, but recurring one-account exceptions create dependence unless teams generalize, transfer, reprice, partner, or stop (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, OpenAI Platform FDE Manager).
  • Speed versus quality: Time-to-production matters, but speed without change-failure, rework, reliability, and outcome measures can reward fragile delivery (DORA software-delivery metrics, Google SRE error-budget policy).
  • Usage versus value: Tokens, requests, and production workloads are visible leading indicators, but workflow impact and customer-derived success measures are needed to show value (OpenAI Startup Deployment Manager, OpenAI Seoul FDE, DORA customer feedback).
  • Reuse volume versus reuse impact: Producing playbooks is not the same as creating leverage; reuse matters when another deployment adopts the artifact and the next delivery improves (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Scale FDE Director).
  • Explicit ladder versus flexible pathway: Okta publishes an account-to-portfolio Senior/Principal distinction, while Palantir favors multiple pathways over traditional career ladders (Okta Principal FDE, Palantir FDSE).
  • Reliability accountability versus transparency: Treating reliability policy as punishment can discourage incident reporting, whereas Google frames error budgets as a mechanism for changing work priorities and protecting customers (Google SRE error-budget policy).

Unresolved questions:

  • No reviewed primary source disclosed a validated cross-company FDE scorecard weighting, quota, promotion threshold, calibration method, or compensation linkage (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, Scale FDE Director, Okta Principal FDE).
  • Employer postings state expectations but do not reveal whether those criteria are applied consistently in actual performance reviews or promotions (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, Scale FDE Director).
  • No reviewed primary evidence established Korea-specific leveling, promotion rates, performance distributions, or calibrated comparisons across global vendors, Korean SIs, consultancies, and in-house teams (OpenAI Seoul FDE).
  • The causal allocation of business outcomes and account growth among FDE, Sales, Product, customer teams, and delivery partners remains engagement-specific (OpenAI Startup Deployment Manager, OpenAI Platform FDE Manager).
  • The optimal balance among customer outcome, reliability, commercial contribution, reusable work, and product learning remains undisclosed and likely varies by company maturity and business model (OpenAI Enterprise Deployment Manager, Scale FDE Director).

3.3 Organization design, staffing, careers, and sustainability

The strongest current role definitions place Forward Deployed Engineering between customer delivery and core platform development, with direct working interfaces to Product, Research, Partnerships, security and governance, and GTM rather than inside a single conventional function (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Scale FDE Director). OpenAI assigns its Seoul FDE to Model Deployment for Business, while Scale lists its FDE director in Engineering, showing that the exact reporting label varies even when the customer-to-product bridge is similar (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Scale FDE Director).

The evidence-supported design recommendation is therefore a primary technical home—Engineering, Model Deployment, Technical Success, or an equivalent deployment organization—with formal account governance shared with Sales or GTM and explicit interfaces to Product, Research, Security/GRC, Partnerships, and customer operators (OpenAI Seoul FDE, OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director). This structure gives the FDE manager authority over engineering quality, staffing, workload, feedback, and career development while letting the account lead coordinate customer sequencing and commercial context (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director). Product and Research should own disposition of reusable field signals, Sales and Partnerships should own qualification and commercial coordination, security functions should retain production-control authority, and the customer should own workflow value, adoption, and steady-state operating decisions (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Microsoft–EY initiative).

Five organization models

  1. Core FDE team. OpenAI and Scale directly employ FDEs who embed alongside strategic customers while remaining connected to core product or platform development (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Scale FDE Director). This model best preserves the product-feedback loop, but it also requires leaders to prevent strategic accounts from permanently absorbing scarce engineers (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director).

  2. Centralized deployment organization. AWS announced a dedicated FDE organization backed by $1 billion and intended to embed thousands of engineers with customers, while MakinaRocks says it established its dedicated FDE organization in 2024 (AWS Partner-Led FDE, MakinaRocks hiring announcement). A centralized unit creates a durable home for hiring, standards, staffing, learning, and reusable assets, although it can become a remote delivery queue if account context does not circulate back into the unit (AWS Partner-Led FDE, OpenAI FDE Manager).

  3. Controlled deployment company and acquisition. OpenAI launched its Deployment Company as a standalone business unit with a distinct operating model, pace, and customer focus while retaining majority ownership and control (OpenAI Deployment Company). OpenAI said the unit would launch with more than $4 billion of initial investment and announced an agreement to acquire Tomoro, which would contribute approximately 150 experienced FDEs and deployment specialists (OpenAI Deployment Company). This structure can add experienced capacity and services-like operating speed quickly, but the cited announcement does not yet establish post-acquisition retention, integration quality, or delivery outcomes (OpenAI Deployment Company).

  4. Local joint venture. Palantir created a jointly owned South Korean entity with HD Hyundai in December 2022 and reported that it held a controlling interest (Palantir 2024 Form 10-K). Palantir says the structure provides strategic operational advantages in Korea while also limiting some ability to sell independently, demonstrating that local access and alignment can come at the cost of unilateral commercial freedom (Palantir 2024 Form 10-K).

  5. Partner-led practice. AWS requires ring-fenced, AWS-credentialed partner engineering teams and embeds AWS engineers deeply in early engagements before shifting support toward practice scaling as reusable patterns accumulate (AWS Partner-Led FDE). Microsoft and EY use an integrated, industry-aligned team in which Microsoft contributes FDE engineering depth and EY contributes business, industry, and change-management capabilities (Microsoft–EY initiative). Partner-led structures expand geographic, industry, and change capacity, but they require common technical bars, explicit accountability, and a planned transfer of capability to avoid variable quality and permanent provider dependence (AWS Partner-Led FDE, Microsoft–EY initiative).

These models solve different scaling constraints: the core team protects product learning, the centralized unit institutionalizes scarce capability, the controlled company separates deployment cadence, acquisition adds experienced capacity, the joint venture supplies local alignment, and the partner practice expands delivery reach (OpenAI Deployment Company, AWS Partner-Led FDE, Palantir 2024 Form 10-K). No reviewed evidence establishes that one model is universally superior on margin, retention, product learning, or customer outcome (OpenAI Deployment Company, AWS Partner-Led FDE).

Staffing and operating practice

A scalable default is a persistent technical home combined with temporary account pods, because current FDE organizations expect both deep customer embedding and reusable product or platform learning (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Scale FDE Director). The persistent home should own hiring, leveling, code and architecture review, reusable assets, staffing, learning, and team health, while the account pod should own discovery through stabilization with a named technical lead and explicit customer counterparts (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director). Domain, security, reliability, data, integration, and change specialists should join according to account risk rather than being treated as universal skills that every FDE must supply alone (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Microsoft–EY initiative).

AWS provides the clearest published capability-transfer pattern: partner engineers must meet an AWS-defined production bar, AWS engineers embed during early builds, reusable delivery knowledge stays with the partner, and AWS support shifts toward helping the partner practice scale (AWS Partner-Led FDE). That pattern implies that direct-provider staffing should decline as the partner or customer becomes capable, unless ongoing operations are explicitly contracted as a separate service (AWS Partner-Led FDE).

Portfolio staffing reviews should consider account phase, risk, expected value, dependency burden, travel, incidents, and handoff readiness, because OpenAI explicitly expects FDE managers to define support models that scale without added complexity and judges them on delivery-model durability (OpenAI FDE Manager). Capacity should also be reserved for production incidents, productization, learning, mentoring, and recovery rather than driving customer utilization toward 100%, because reuse, growth, and sustainable delivery are explicit organizational expectations (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director). Rotations should occur at delivery milestones with paired handoff, preserved technical records, and a narrow escalation path so that career mobility does not destroy account continuity (OpenAI Seoul FDE, AWS Partner-Led FDE).

Careers, incentives, and retention

Scale requires its FDE director to build onboarding and career frameworks, while OpenAI expects FDE managers to support individual growth through direct, actionable feedback (Scale FDE Director, OpenAI FDE Manager). A sustainable talent system should therefore offer both an IC path—from bounded deployment execution to hardest-case ownership, cross-account standards, product influence, and mentoring—and a management path—from one pod to portfolio allocation, hiring systems, partner governance, and operating-model economics (Scale FDE Director, OpenAI FDE Manager). Rotations into Product, Platform, Research Engineering, Technical Success, Solutions, or domain leadership should be treated as legitimate career mobility because the organization depends on sustained exchange between field delivery and core development (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Scale FDE Director).

Incentives should combine durable customer outcome, production quality, reusable leverage, field-signal quality, capability transfer, mentoring, and team health because OpenAI and Scale define leadership success across shipping, deployment durability, roadmap signal, career development, and delivery-model sustainability (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director). Individual revenue, raw utilization, travel volume, deployment count, and repeated heroic incident recovery should not dominate compensation because they can reward overscoping, account dependence, and understaffing rather than reusable technical value (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director). MakinaRocks advertised a KRW 5 million signing bonus in its July 2026 campaign, but a recruitment bonus does not establish long-term retention or career sustainability (MakinaRocks hiring announcement).

Workload, travel, and burnout risk

Travel is a material design variable rather than a minor job condition: OpenAI’s Seoul FDE posting expects 50% travel, while its Munich FDE manager posting requires up to 25% (OpenAI Seoul FDE, OpenAI FDE Manager). A four-wave study of 676 employees found that greater business travel was associated with both higher career satisfaction and higher turnover intentions, indicating that travel can provide developmental value while simultaneously increasing retention risk (“Another trip?” study).

WHO identifies excessive workload, long or inflexible hours, limited support, unclear roles, and weak investment in career development as workplace mental-health risks and recommends organizational interventions that change working conditions rather than relying only on individual coping (WHO mental health at work). FDE sustainability should therefore be managed through capacity buffers, predictable travel schedules, workload control, manager support, recovery after launches, rotation out of prolonged high-pressure accounts, role clarity, and visible career investment (WHO mental health at work, OpenAI FDE Manager). Travel expectations should be disclosed before hiring and measured at portfolio level because the burden can otherwise concentrate on bilingual or locally scarce staff (OpenAI Seoul FDE, “Another trip?” study).

Korea application

Korea already contains three observable organization structures: OpenAI directly employs a Seoul FDE in Model Deployment for Business, MakinaRocks operates a dedicated local FDE organization established in 2024, and Palantir uses a controlled joint entity with HD Hyundai (OpenAI Seoul FDE, MakinaRocks hiring announcement, Palantir 2024 Form 10-K). MakinaRocks made FDE its most strategic hiring priority in July 2026 and announced hiring across more than 30 roles, showing that its FDE expansion is embedded in a broader multi-function organization rather than an isolated engineer title (MakinaRocks hiring announcement).

The most defensible Korea design is a bilingual core-plus-ecosystem model in which a direct vendor core retains platform architecture and field-signal accountability, Korean partners supply local integration and change capacity, and buyer teams own workflow decisions and steady-state operations (OpenAI Seoul FDE, AWS Partner-Led FDE, Microsoft–EY initiative). Korea-facing employers should not make bilingual or local-domain FDEs absorb every integration, governance, and stakeholder task, because the observed partner and integrated-team models allocate engineering, industry, and change capabilities across multiple roles (AWS Partner-Led FDE, Microsoft–EY initiative).

Tensions:

  • Customer embedding versus engineering home: Close embedding improves context, but permanent account isolation can weaken platform standards, peer learning, and career visibility, so embedded pods need a durable technical home (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Scale FDE Director).
  • Technical accountability versus commercial control: Sales and GTM must shape qualification and account coordination, but engineering managers need authority over quality, staffing, workload, and escalation (OpenAI Seoul FDE, OpenAI FDE Manager).
  • Direct capacity versus partner leverage: Direct teams preserve product connection, while partner-led teams expand industry and geographic reach but add quality and accountability interfaces (AWS Partner-Led FDE, Microsoft–EY initiative).
  • Standalone speed versus core connection: A separate deployment company can create a distinct delivery pace, but it must remain connected to the research, product, and in-house deployment teams that shape the platform (OpenAI Deployment Company).
  • Acquisition speed versus integration risk: Buying an experienced delivery firm can add capacity quickly, but the cited announcement provides no evidence yet on retention, tooling convergence, or post-close operating performance (OpenAI Deployment Company).
  • Local access versus strategic restriction: Palantir reports both operational advantages and limits on independent selling through its Korean joint entity (Palantir 2024 Form 10-K).
  • Utilization versus compounding leverage: Maximum account utilization conflicts with the protected productization, mentoring, learning, and recovery time needed to scale the organization (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director).
  • Travel as opportunity versus retention risk: Business travel can increase career satisfaction while also increasing turnover intentions, so a single high-travel policy will not be equally sustainable for all employees (“Another trip?” study).
  • Heroic ownership versus durable delivery: The role requires judgment under pressure, but a model that repeatedly depends on individual heroics risks normalizing excessive workload and understaffing (OpenAI FDE Manager, WHO mental health at work).

Unresolved questions:

  • No reviewed source establishes an optimal FDE-to-account ratio, manager span, specialist leverage ratio, bench level, or rotation period (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director).
  • No reviewed evidence compares customer outcome, product learning, margin, or retention across internal teams, standalone deployment companies, acquisitions, joint ventures, and partner-led practices (OpenAI Deployment Company, AWS Partner-Led FDE, Palantir 2024 Form 10-K).
  • OpenAI’s cited Tomoro acquisition announcement does not disclose post-close integration or retention outcomes (OpenAI Deployment Company).
  • No reviewed source discloses Korea-specific FDE compensation, promotion rates, tenure, attrition, burnout prevalence, travel distribution, gender representation, or return-to-product mobility (OpenAI Seoul FDE, MakinaRocks hiring announcement).
  • Employer pages state intended career and sustainability practices but do not establish how consistently those practices are implemented in reviews, staffing decisions, or day-to-day workload management (OpenAI FDE Manager, Scale FDE Director).

4. Commercial and scaling engine

Evaluate how companies package, price, fund, and scale forward-deployed work without losing product leverage.

Forward-deployed engineering has no single commercial package: current offers range from provider-funded pilots and separately paid professional services to bounded capacity subscriptions, platform-linked support, bundled software-and-services relationships, and partner or reseller co-delivery (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K, Databricks DSA service description, OpenAI Services Agreement, Google Cloud Marketplace terms). Databricks, for example, sells annual access to up to 8, 16, or 32 advisory hours per week, forfeits unused weekly capacity, and excludes production deployment, user-acceptance testing, customer change management, and non-Databricks integration (Databricks DSA service description). Those exclusions illustrate the essential contracting principle: product entitlement, discovery, build and integration, recurring technical capacity, production support, travel, and third-party costs should be priced and owned separately even when the buyer experiences them as one transformation program (Databricks DSA service description).

The economic dividing line is whether delivery remains proportional to staffed labor or becomes leverage for recurring product use, account expansion, reusable patterns, and partner throughput. Palantir reported an 82% consolidated gross margin in 2025 while also recording cloud, subcontractor, field-service, and payroll costs, and its average revenue from the top twenty customers rose 45% to USD 93.9 million (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). Those consolidated figures show that field-intensive delivery can coexist with software-like company economics, but they do not disclose an FDE-only margin or prove that every deployment is profitable (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). Accenture provides the contrasting labor benchmark: it reported 92% utilization and a 14.7% operating margin for fiscal 2025, with compensation its largest operating-cost component (Accenture 2025 Form 10-K). FDE economics should therefore be managed by account or deployment cohort—recurring product and separately billed service revenue minus model, cloud, direct labor, partner, travel, support, and pilot costs—rather than by billable utilization alone.

Productization is the mechanism that converts expensive field learning into lower marginal delivery effort. OpenAI’s Seoul FDE role requires one engineer to own multiple deployments, codify working patterns into reusable tools, playbooks, or building blocks, and share field feedback with Research and Product (OpenAI Seoul FDE role). Google Cloud’s Delivery Navigator similarly packages consulting knowledge as standardized plans, technical blueprints, and implementation instructions that project teams can tailor (Google Cloud Delivery Navigator). A durable boundary separates four layers: recurring, supportable capabilities enter core product; parameterized patterns enter a governed accelerator registry; proprietary workflows, data mappings, and legacy adapters remain customer-specific extensions; and local operating decisions and runbooks remain customer-owned knowledge. Google’s implementation agreement formalizes a related IP distinction by retaining generally applicable service technology at Google while licensing specific deliverables to the customer, and OpenAI’s enterprise agreement states that the customer retains input rights, owns output, and must explicitly agree before customer content is used to improve services (Google Cloud Implementation Services Agreement, OpenAI Services Agreement).

Partners and automation increase throughput only when they inherit a common engineering bar and explicit accountability. AWS’s Partner-Led FDE model requires each engagement to create a reusable partner-owned harness containing assets such as domain ontologies, evaluation frameworks, MCP servers, agent-operations tooling, and context graphs, while AWS-credentialed partner engineers must pass an AWS-defined production standard (AWS Partner-Led FDE). AWS also says agents accelerate every delivery phase while human engineers verify and guide, and Palantir’s AI FDE applies the user’s permissions, approval controls, branches, and audit logging rather than acting as an unconstrained production operator (AWS FDE announcement, Palantir AI FDE security and governance). Palantir explicitly instructs users to verify generated resources before production and combine AI-generated foundations with manual refinement (Palantir AI FDE best practices). Automation output should therefore be treated as a governed change, not an accountability transfer, and one named delivery lead should remain responsible across vendor, partner, and customer contributions.

Korea is already developing a layered vendor-plus-local-delivery stack: OpenAI recruits an FDE in Seoul, while Samsung SDS has described an OpenAI go-to-market strategy, joint-account planning, and an AI-platform approach spanning FabriX and external platforms such as AWS (OpenAI Seoul FDE role, Samsung SDS Q3 2025 earnings script). The likely operating model is a scarce platform-vendor FDE core for strategic and product-edge problems, surrounded by local SI, consulting, security, cloud, and managed-service capacity for repeatable delivery; this remains an inference from the announced roles and partnerships rather than a disclosed staffing formula (OpenAI Seoul FDE role, Samsung SDS Q3 2025 earnings script). ⚠️ Buyers should demand price decomposition and an IP-and-accountability schedule covering product defects, customer-specific code, reusable accelerators, localization, security evidence, usage overruns, and post-launch operations, because partner leverage otherwise risks stacked margins and blurred ownership (OpenAI Services Agreement, Samsung SDS Q3 2025 earnings script).

4.1 Offer packaging, pricing, contracting, and unit economics

Forward-deployed work is not sold through one standard price architecture. Public evidence shows four recurring forms: provider-funded pilots used as customer-acquisition investment; separately paid professional services; subscriptions that reserve a bounded share of technical capacity; and deployment effort bundled with, or justified by, recurring software, support, license, consumption, or minimum-commitment revenue (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K, Databricks DSA service description, OpenAI Services Agreement). Partner-led delivery adds a fifth route: the buyer may pay a reseller, consultancy, SI, or marketplace provider for implementation while the platform company supplies its technology, enablement, escalation, or selective FDE involvement (OpenAI Partner Network, Google Cloud Marketplace terms).

The commercial distinction is therefore not whether an invoice contains the title “FDE,” but whether deployment labor remains proportional to staffed hours or becomes leverage for recurring product use, expansion, and reusable delivery patterns (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K, OpenAI Seoul FDE role). No authoritative source reviewed disclosed a standard FDE list price, day rate, or outcome-based fee schedule; commercial price is usually left to an order form, service order, private offer, or statement of work (Databricks DSA service description, OpenAI Services Agreement, Google Cloud Marketplace terms).

Packaging and pricing archetypes

Model Who pays and how Economic logic and boundary
Provider-funded pilot or bootcamp The provider absorbs the initial delivery cost; Palantir says it conducts some pilots and bootcamps at its own expense without guaranteed future returns (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). The pilot is an acquisition investment whose return depends on later conversion, product use, and account expansion rather than pilot revenue (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).
Paid professional services The buyer pays under an order, SOW, service credits, fixed service package, or usage-based time-and-materials charge; Google Cloud Marketplace explicitly permits professional services billed by time spent or materials incurred (Databricks DSA service description, Google Cloud Marketplace terms). Revenue and cost scale closely with delivered labor unless the work is standardized or accelerates recurring product revenue (Accenture 2025 Form 10-K, Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).
Capacity subscription The buyer prepays for recurring access to a bounded fraction of a technical advisor; Databricks packages up to 8, 16, or 32 hours per week and forfeits unused weekly hours (Databricks DSA service description). The provider protects staffing capacity and gives the buyer continuity, but the buyer bears the risk that blocked prerequisites or unused hours destroy prepaid value (Databricks DSA service description).
Platform-linked support The buyer pays a minimum fee or a charge tied to platform consumption; AWS prices Enterprise Support as the greater of a minimum monthly fee or a percentage of monthly cloud charges (AWS Support pricing). Technical coverage grows with platform scale, while exceptional events can be priced separately; AWS lists designated launch or migration support at USD 10,000 per project per month (AWS Support pricing).
Bundled software, O&M, and deployment The buyer commits to recurring software, license, O&M, usage, or volume while deployment and professional-services effort is attached or separately identified; Palantir sells cloud or on-premises software with O&M and on-demand professional services (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). This model can reduce procurement friction and support expansion, but it can also conceal bespoke labor and deployment-level losses inside consolidated software economics (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).
Partner or reseller co-delivery The buyer may pay the platform vendor or its reseller for product access and contract separately with a partner for deployment; OpenAI’s agreement permits payment to OpenAI or a reseller, and its partner network supports co-selling, deploying, building, and customer connectivity (OpenAI Services Agreement, OpenAI Partner Network). The model expands delivery coverage but can create stacked software, resale, integration, cloud, security, and managed-service charges unless each layer is priced and owned explicitly (OpenAI Services Agreement, Samsung SDS FY2025 earnings call).

Databricks illustrates how a vendor can productize scarce advisory capacity without promising end-to-end implementation. Its DSA subscription excludes production deployment, user-acceptance testing, customer change management, upstream data cleansing, and non-Databricks integration, and a service order can take the form of an order, Success Credit redemption, SOW, or similar document (Databricks DSA service description). These exclusions turn the offer into paid, bounded technical guidance rather than a blank cheque for full implementation (Databricks DSA service description).

OpenAI’s enterprise agreement shows a scalable product-contract pattern: usage can be charged from OpenAI’s measurement; additional licenses, quantities, or volumes can be added for the remainder of the current term; minimum commitments may be non-cancellable; and fees may be paid to OpenAI or a reseller (OpenAI Services Agreement). These mechanics allow expansion inside an existing term while keeping price, payment, and renewal in the commercial contract rather than leaving them to the deployment engineer (OpenAI Services Agreement).

Contract design and commercial ownership

A robust commercial design should separate platform entitlement or consumption, discovery or pilot work, build and integration, recurring advisory capacity, production support or managed operations, and travel or third-party costs, because the reviewed offers price and constrain these elements differently (Databricks DSA service description, AWS Support pricing, OpenAI Services Agreement). Each layer should specify its price basis, capacity or deliverables, prerequisites, acceptance or consumption trigger, term, renewal, change control, expenses, support boundary, liability, and exit condition; the need for this decomposition is visible in Databricks’ explicit caps and exclusions and OpenAI’s order-form and minimum-commitment mechanics (Databricks DSA service description, OpenAI Services Agreement).

Commercial authority should be split rather than concentrated in the FDE. Sales or the account executive should own price, discount, order form, forecast, renewal, and expansion; deployment leadership should own qualification, effort estimation, staffing, delivery risk, and whether the work is product-relevant; and the customer should own access, business acceptance, and operating readiness, because vendor contracts explicitly rely on customer commitments and separate product use from professional-services scope (Databricks DSA service description, OpenAI Services Agreement). The FDE should provide the technical and value evidence for expansion, but commission-bearing commercial staff should close it so engineering incentives do not reward unbounded customization (OpenAI Seoul FDE role, Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).

Unit economics: software leverage or consultancy labor

Palantir provides the strongest public evidence that a field-intensive model can coexist with software-like consolidated economics. It reported an 82% consolidated gross margin in 2025 while its cost-of-revenue increase included cloud hosting, subcontractors, field-service representatives, and payroll (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). Its average revenue from the top twenty customers rose 45% to USD 93.9 million, and existing customers contributed USD 425.2 million of the increase in commercial revenue, supporting a land-and-expand mechanism in which expensive initial deployment can be repaid by deeper product adoption (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). Those figures do not establish an FDE margin, because Palantir does not disclose FDE revenue or direct FDE cost separately (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).

Accenture provides the contrasting labor-service benchmark. It reported 92% utilization and a 14.7% operating margin for fiscal 2025, and it identifies compensation as its largest operating-cost component (Accenture 2025 Form 10-K). In that model, margin depends heavily on matching skills to demand, maintaining utilization, pricing work appropriately, and improving delivery efficiency and workforce mix (Accenture 2025 Form 10-K).

An FDE model approaches software economics when recurring platform or consumption revenue and account expansion grow faster than dedicated delivery cost, one engineer or enabled partner can cover multiple deployments, and working patterns become reusable assets (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K, OpenAI Seoul FDE role). It approaches consultancy economics when revenue scales mainly with staffed hours, utilization and rates drive profit, customer-specific work remains non-reusable, and continuing operations require dedicated labor (Accenture 2025 Form 10-K, Google Cloud Marketplace terms). OpenAI’s Seoul role explicitly expects one FDE to own multiple deployments and codify working patterns into tools, playbooks, or building blocks, making concurrency and reuse observable design goals rather than incidental benefits (OpenAI Seoul FDE role).

The appropriate management unit is therefore the account or deployment cohort rather than the isolated engineer-hour. A useful cohort contribution view is recurring product or consumption revenue plus separately billed deployment and support revenue, minus model or cloud cost, direct FDE and specialist labor, partner or subcontractor cost, travel, support burden, and provider-funded pilot cost; each component is represented in the reviewed contracts or financial disclosures (OpenAI Services Agreement, Databricks DSA service description, Palantir 2025 Form 10-K). Leading indicators should include pilot-to-paid conversion, time to stable production, activated product use, weekly FDE capacity consumed, concurrent deployments, scope changes, hypercare duration, reusable-asset adoption, partner-delivered share, and customer self-sufficiency; lagging indicators should include renewal, expansion, gross margin after delivery, payback, support burden, and concentration risk (Databricks DSA service description, OpenAI Seoul FDE role, Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).

Korea-specific commercial stack

Korea already shows a hybrid vendor-plus-local-delivery structure. OpenAI has a Seoul FDE role responsible for multiple deployments, while Samsung SDS said that, after becoming a ChatGPT Enterprise reseller, it was conducting consulting engagements and proofs of concept with multiple companies (OpenAI Seoul FDE role, Samsung SDS FY2025 earnings call). Samsung SDS also stated that it planned to bundle security consulting by default across its cloud services as an acquisition tactic (Samsung SDS FY2025 earnings call) ⚠️.

This evidence supports a four-layer Korean account structure: enterprise licenses or model consumption; vendor- or reseller-supported pilot work; local consulting, integration, and localization; and recurring cloud, security, or managed operations (OpenAI Services Agreement, Samsung SDS FY2025 earnings call). OpenAI’s Partner Network supports co-selling, deployment, building, and connectivity, and it is piloting a Forward Deployed Experts program intended to align partner practitioners with OpenAI FDE teams (OpenAI Partner Network) ⚠️. The likely operating implication is a small platform-vendor FDE layer focused on strategic and product-edge problems, surrounded by local partner capacity for repeatable integration, change, and operations; this is an inference from the announced partner and Korea delivery models rather than a disclosed staffing formula (OpenAI Partner Network, Samsung SDS FY2025 earnings call).

Korean buyers should therefore demand price decomposition by layer and a joint ownership matrix for product defects, customer-specific code, local integration, security evidence, usage overruns, and post-launch operations, because the reseller-plus-platform-plus-services structure otherwise permits overlapping charges without a single outcome owner (OpenAI Services Agreement, Samsung SDS FY2025 earnings call). No authoritative public source reviewed disclosed Korean FDE day rates, reseller discounts, pilot prices, minimum commitments, consumption pass-through terms, or vendor-partner margin splits (OpenAI Services Agreement, Samsung SDS FY2025 earnings call).

Tensions:

  • Provider-funded pilots can reach difficult, high-value accounts, but Palantir explicitly says those pilots may produce no future return, making qualification and cohort payback essential (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).
  • Bundling deployment with recurring software can lower transaction friction, but consolidated gross margin does not reveal whether a particular deployment is profitable or subsidized (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).
  • Weekly caps and forfeited hours protect provider capacity, but transfer schedule and readiness risk to the buyer (Databricks DSA service description).
  • Consulting-style utilization protects near-term billability, while the FDE role also requires time for reusable tooling and product feedback that is not naturally captured as client hours (Accenture 2025 Form 10-K, OpenAI Seoul FDE role).
  • Existing-account expansion can create software leverage, but it also increases renewal and customer-concentration exposure (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).
  • Partner delivery expands local capacity, but can stack reseller, implementation, infrastructure, security, and managed-service margins unless contracting makes each layer visible (OpenAI Services Agreement, Samsung SDS FY2025 earnings call).

Unresolved questions:

  • What are current FDE list prices, day rates, or outcome-based fee schedules across vendors? The reviewed public materials leave pricing to private order forms, service orders, and SOWs (Databricks DSA service description, OpenAI Services Agreement).
  • What are FDE-only gross margin, contribution margin, and fully loaded deployment cost? Palantir reports consolidated economics but does not isolate the function (Palantir 2025 Form 10-K).
  • What utilization, concurrent-deployment, pilot-conversion, and payback benchmarks should an FDE organization use? The reviewed disclosures provide capacity tiers and company-level utilization but no cross-company FDE standard (Databricks DSA service description, Accenture 2025 Form 10-K).
  • How are Korean reseller discounts, pilot fees, minimum commitments, consumption charges, and vendor-SI margins divided? The reviewed Korea and contract sources do not disclose those commercial terms (OpenAI Services Agreement, Samsung SDS FY2025 earnings call).

4.2 Productization, reusable assets, learning loops, and automation

The scaling problem is not simply to make each FDE faster; it is to convert repeated field work into supported product capability, governed delivery assets, and customer-owned operating knowledge. OpenAI makes this dual mandate explicit in Seoul: the FDE must both deliver production systems and codify working patterns into reusable tools, playbooks, or building blocks, while sending field feedback to Research and Product (OpenAI Seoul FDE role).

A practical boundary has four layers:

  1. Core product should contain recurring, cross-customer capabilities that warrant a stable interface, roadmap owner, release process, security model, telemetry, documentation, and support obligation. OpenAI describes its FDE loop as moving from a business problem through deployment to research and back, making repeated field evidence an input to product and model evolution (OpenAI Frontier).
  2. Reusable delivery assets should contain parameterized reference architectures, connectors, schemas, evaluation harnesses, deployment templates, runbooks, code generators, playbooks, and training. Google Cloud has productized this layer in Delivery Navigator, which packages consulting knowledge as standardized project plans, technical blueprints, and implementation instructions that teams can tailor to their own projects (Google Cloud Delivery Navigator).
  3. Customer-specific extensions should contain proprietary workflow rules, local data mappings, domain ontology, legacy adapters, configuration, UI, and acceptance tests that encode one buyer’s context. Google’s implementation terms distinguish generally applicable tools, code, modules, materials, and know-how retained as Google Technology from deliverables licensed to the customer, illustrating why the boundary must be stated contractually (Google Cloud Implementation Services Agreement).
  4. Customer operating knowledge should remain maintainable inside the customer’s environment: decisions, ownership, exceptions, runbooks, and local procedures. AWS says its FDE model is designed to leave customers self-sufficient at the end of an engagement, although this is a provider-stated design objective rather than an independently verified result (AWS FDE announcement). ⚠️

The decision to move something into core product should pass five tests: recurrence across unrelated customers, strategic fit with the product, generalizability without importing confidential context, the provider’s ability to operate and support it across versions, and lower lifecycle cost than repeated field implementation. A repeated but fast-changing or domain-specific need belongs first in a governed accelerator; a unique workflow, confidential rule set, local data model, or legacy integration should normally remain a modular customer extension. This separation is also a data-governance requirement: OpenAI’s enterprise agreement says the customer retains input rights and owns output, and that OpenAI does not use customer content to improve services without explicit customer agreement (OpenAI Services Agreement).

The field-to-product loop therefore needs an explicit abstraction pipeline. FDEs should record recurring integration friction, missing capabilities, evaluation failures, support burden, time spent, affected deployments, and business impact; a joint product-and-field review should then classify each signal as a product defect, roadmap candidate, reusable asset, partner-enablement need, documentation gap, or customer-specific work. OpenAI’s Seoul role directly assigns both reusable-pattern codification and field feedback to the FDE, while Google permits use of feedback voluntarily supplied under its implementation agreement (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Google Cloud Implementation Services Agreement).

Reusable assets need product-like governance even when they are not formal products. Each asset should have a named owner, semantic version, supported platform and model versions, license and provenance, security classification, localization status, automated tests, examples, permitted telemetry, and a deprecation path. Google Delivery Navigator demonstrates the operating principle: reusable methods provide a common baseline, but project teams can adjust deliverables, timelines, and tasks to fit their context (Google Cloud Delivery Navigator). OpenAI also argues that shared context and open standards can reduce repeated one-off integration work, but this is a vendor claim and is not quantified in the evidence reviewed (OpenAI Frontier). ⚠️

Partner leverage can expand capacity when it transfers both assets and an engineering standard. AWS’s Partner-Led FDE model requires every engagement to produce a reusable, partner-owned harness and describes domain ontologies, evaluation frameworks, MCP servers, agent-operations tooling, context graphs, and capability registries as components of that harness (AWS Partner-Led FDE). AWS assigns delivery IP to the partner while distinguishing it from the customer’s business outcome, and it requires partner engineers to meet an AWS-defined production bar before customer work (AWS Partner-Led FDE). The scalable model is therefore not an uncontrolled subcontracting chain: the platform vendor owns product defects, reference standards, enablement, and escalation; the partner owns staffing quality, its delivery harness, integration, and first-line assurance; the customer owns requirements, data rights, acceptance, and operating authority; and one named delivery lead remains accountable for the integrated result.

Automation can increase throughput, but it does not transfer accountability. AWS says its delivery lifecycle uses agents across phases while human engineers verify and guide their work (AWS FDE announcement). Palantir’s AI FDE applies the user’s existing permissions, governance controls, and audit logging, and it does not automatically approve operations that could affect production workflows (Palantir AI FDE security and governance). Palantir also instructs users to verify generated resources before production and recommends combining AI-generated foundations with manual development for production quality (Palantir AI FDE best practices). The appropriate control envelope is bounded work, least-privilege identity, approved tools, branch isolation, previews, tests and evaluations, human review before production mutation, audit logs, rollback, rate and cost limits, and clear escalation.

The operating scorecard should measure both customer delivery and compounding leverage. Delivery measures should include time to stable production, acceptance and evaluation pass rate, escaped defects, rollback and incident rates, adoption, workflow impact, and customer self-sufficiency. Leverage measures should include FDE hours per stable deployment, concurrent deployments per FDE, reuse of approved assets, customer-specific fork count, support burden per asset, partner-delivered share, and time from repeated field signal to product decision. Automation should be measured by human review time, accepted-change rate, rework, defect escape, evaluation regression, privileged-action attempts, cost per completed task, and rollback frequency—not generated code volume or agent task count.

For Korea, the likely model is a small product-vendor FDE core multiplied by local platform, SI, and MSP capacity. OpenAI already recruits an FDE in Seoul, and Samsung SDS has stated that it is developing an OpenAI go-to-market strategy for Korea while exploring AI-platform partnerships spanning FabriX and external platforms such as AWS (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Samsung SDS Q3 2025 earnings script). The reusable Korean layer should include Korean-language evaluation and red-team assets, domestic enterprise-system connectors, local security and approval documentation, hybrid or private-cloud reference architectures, and repeatable handoff packages; proprietary customer data, policies, process semantics, and regulated mappings should remain isolated. Procurement should separately identify vendor product, provider background IP, partner-owned accelerators, open-source components, customer materials, customer-specific deliverables, permitted learning use, and escalation rights.

Tensions:

  • Bespoke speed versus product leverage: immediate local code may prove value quickly, while a supported abstraction requires recurrence evidence, stable interfaces, tests, ownership, and lifecycle funding (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Google Cloud Delivery Navigator).
  • Learning versus confidentiality: FDE proximity improves product learning, while enterprise content and workflow details cannot be reused without contractual permission (OpenAI Services Agreement).
  • Partner scale versus accountability: AWS expects reusable partner-owned delivery IP and independent scaling, but still imposes a common production-engineering bar (AWS Partner-Led FDE).
  • Provider reuse versus customer control: Google’s terms retain generally applicable service technology at Google, whereas AWS describes partner-owned delivery IP and customer-owned business outcomes, so “customer ownership” must be decomposed by asset class (Google Cloud Implementation Services Agreement; AWS Partner-Led FDE).
  • Automation versus production control: agentic execution can accelerate delivery, but Palantir’s own design retains permissions, approvals, branches, review, and human production refinement (Palantir AI FDE security and governance; Palantir AI FDE best practices).
  • Utilization versus compounding knowledge: maximizing staffed delivery can crowd out abstraction and maintenance, while excessive accelerator-building can delay customer outcomes and create unused assets (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Google Cloud Delivery Navigator).
  • Global reuse versus Korean fit: reusable global patterns still require localization for Korean language, systems, deployment environments, governance, and handoff responsibilities (OpenAI Seoul FDE role; Samsung SDS Q3 2025 earnings script).

Unresolved questions: What reuse-rate and FDE-hour-reduction benchmarks distinguish genuine software leverage from asset accumulation? What IP, telemetry, reuse, and escalation schedules will govern OpenAI-Samsung SDS co-delivery in Korea? What are the long-run defect, maintenance, and incident economics of AI-generated deployment assets?

5. Demand, fit, and outcomes in Korea

Determine which Korean buyers and use cases fit FDE, which local constraints shape deployment, and how outcomes and sourcing choices should be evaluated.

Korean demand for forward-deployed engineering is strongest where adoption ambition exceeds the buyer’s ability to integrate, govern, and operate AI: large Korean firms adopt AI materially more often than mid-sized firms, employee skills are the leading generative-AI barrier among non-adopting Korean SMEs, and only 26.2% of AI-using firms in a 2024 Korea Labor Institute survey had internal AI-use guidelines (OECD/KLI). Public and regulated demand is also moving from exploration toward controlled deployment, with NIA cataloguing 110 public-sector pilots, the financial regulator receiving 141 generative-AI or SaaS exception requests from 74 financial companies, and OpenAI recruiting a Seoul FDE to work with strategic customers from discovery through production (NIA; FSC; OpenAI). These patterns make FDE a particularly good fit for buyers with a valuable but poorly standardized workflow, strong executive urgency, scarce engineering or domain-translation capacity, and enough internal ownership to make decisions and absorb capability transfer (OECD/KLI; OpenAI).

The opportunity divides into digital knowledge-work systems and physical or OT-integrated systems, with different delivery burdens. Samsung SDS reported FabriX use by about 70 public, financial, and manufacturing customers and more than 130,000 users in June 2025, while Woori Bank’s agent programme includes integration with existing banking systems and an operating data-management layer, illustrating that digital-agent value depends on workflow and system integration rather than a standalone model interface (Samsung SDS FabriX; Samsung SDS–Woori). Physical-AI demand is concentrated in Korea’s industrial base: the government’s autonomous-manufacturing strategy targets 200 anchor projects, its 2026 industrial-complex programme convened about 500 industry, academic, and research organizations, and Hyundai and NVIDIA shifted from platform adoption to co-development for mobility, smart factories, and on-device semiconductors (MOTIE autonomous manufacturing; MOTIE industrial-complex AX; Hyundai). OT deployments require more site-specific process analysis, heterogeneous sensing and control integration, trustworthy operation, and safety engineering than ordinary knowledge-work tools, while HD Hyundai’s shipyard programme explicitly plans to validate robot, vision, and AI solutions in actual work settings before standardizing the process model (MOTIE autonomous manufacturing; NIST smart-manufacturing roadmap; HD Hyundai; ISO 10218-1:2025).

Institutional gates can determine the feasible architecture and provider coalition before model performance does. Korea’s AI Basic Act and Enforcement Decree entered into force on January 22, 2026, requiring advance notice for high-impact or generative-AI services and recognizable labeling of generative outputs, while PIPC’s lifecycle guidance covers legal basis, architecture-specific risk reduction, data-subject rights, and CPO-led governance (MSIT; PIPC generative-AI guidance). PIPA does not impose universal domestic storage, but public-sector catalogue eligibility requires independently operable services on high-grade government-cloud infrastructure with high-grade national security verification, and financial regulation separates sandboxed commercial-cloud use from internally installed open-source AI for internal information (PIPC cross-border transfers; PPS; FSC). Healthcare adds independent clinical-performance testing and, for diagnostic or predictive digital medical devices, Korean test data unless racial irrelevance can be demonstrated (MFDS).

Outcome evidence remains more cautious than adoption evidence. The Bank of Korea estimated that generative-AI use reduced working time by 3.8% but increased potential productivity by only 1.0%, the IMF found no significant average productivity effect across Korean firms and stronger benefits in larger, mature firms, and OECD/KLI found that 95.5% of surveyed Korean firms reported no department- or team-level workforce change after AI adoption (Bank of Korea; IMF; OECD/KLI). Durable success therefore requires production adoption, end-to-end workflow improvement, post-deployment monitoring, safe recovery, and whole-life value rather than a successful demonstration, benchmark, or seat count (NIST deployed-AI monitoring; NBER; UK DDaT Playbook). RAND’s practitioner interviews identify problem misunderstanding and poor workflow fit as leading failure causes, while McKinsey’s 2025 survey found enterprise-level EBIT impact in only 39% of respondents and associated its highest-performing group with workflow redesign (RAND; McKinsey).

The practical Korean sourcing default is consequently a staged hybrid rather than complete outsourcing. A product vendor’s FDE should lead where proprietary platform knowledge and first-of-kind co-development dominate; a consultancy should address problem selection, operating-model change, governance, and benefits realization; a Korean SI or MSP should lead complex legacy integration, regulated rollout, local operations, or OT work; a specialist should own a bounded technical or sector bottleneck; and the buyer’s internal team should retain programme, domain, data, risk, and long-term product ownership (RAND; OECD/KLI; UK DDaT Playbook). The UK DDaT bridge model formalizes this path by using external capability until the internal organization can own and run the requirement, while U.S. AI-acquisition guidance requires proposal testing and contract terms for data and IP, privacy, lock-in, ongoing monitoring, vendor performance, and feature-change notice (UK DDaT Playbook; GAO). This allocation preserves scarce vendor expertise for the hardest deployment phase without turning the FDE into a permanent manual integration layer, and it gives Korean buyers a path from first production through partner industrialization to internal or managed steady-state ownership (UK DDaT Playbook; NIST deployed-AI monitoring).

5.1 Buyer archetypes and adoption triggers

Korean demand is best understood as two related markets: scarce, direct FDE capacity for strategic and technically novel deployments, and FDE-like delivery through SIs, cloud/MSPs, government programs, catalogues, and productized pods for buyers that cannot justify a dedicated frontier-provider team (OpenAI Seoul FDE, NIPA AI Voucher, PPS catalogue tender). A strong direct-FDE buyer has a strategically material but still ambiguous workflow, proprietary systems or data, and internal business, engineering, domain, security, and operating counterparts able to co-own delivery from discovery through production (OpenAI Seoul FDE).

Regulated enterprises. Financial institutions show the clearest observed trigger: after Korea broadened permitted generative-AI and SaaS use, 74 firms submitted 141 requests for network-separation exceptions, and the first approvals covered ten services from nine banks, securities firms, insurers, and card companies (Financial Services Commission). This is high-need but high-friction demand: the rule change creates urgency, while production still requires business, IT, risk, legal, and security coordination (Financial Services Commission). These buyers benefit from embedded engineering when approval evidence and workflow-specific controls must be developed alongside the system, but the buyer must retain statutory decisions and post-launch ownership (Financial Services Commission, OpenAI Seoul FDE).

Public organizations. NIA’s 2025 material documented 110 Korean public-AI pilots—54 for social problems, 37 for citizen services, and 19 for internal work—and supplied an adoption procedure plus performance-management guidance for ministries, local governments, and public institutions (NIA public-AI guidance). The Public Procurement Service subsequently opened a catalogue tender for generative-AI services on public work networks and limited eligibility to independently operable services meeting specified high-level cloud and national-security checks (PPS catalogue tender). Public demand therefore favors a Korean prime contractor or consortium when procurement, certification, documentation, formal acceptance, training, and capability transfer dominate the buying motion; genuinely ambiguous workflows still require room for iterative discovery in the contract (NIA public-AI guidance, PPS catalogue tender).

Chaebol groups and large enterprises. These buyers have the strongest economics for direct FDE when a deployment is a group-level priority, a lighthouse factory or knowledge-work system, or a potential standard reusable across affiliates (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Samsung SDS). Samsung SDS reported more than ten customers across public, finance, manufacturing, distribution, and services by March 2026, using an integrated delivery team spanning consulting, development and operations, cloud, and security (Samsung SDS). Korea Zinc’s stated objective—internalizing enterprise AI capability while protecting and accumulating proprietary smelting knowledge—illustrates why large industrial buyers seek acceleration without surrendering data, domain IP, architecture, or long-term control (Samsung SDS). The preferred model is therefore a joint pod: the buyer’s platform or captive-IT team owns enterprise architecture and retained capability, while the provider contributes product authority, frontier-model expertise, and deployment velocity (Samsung SDS, OpenAI Seoul FDE).

Mid-market firms and SMEs. Need is large but purchasing capacity is constrained: AI adoption was 27.4% among Korean firms with 50–249 employees versus 63.3% among firms above 250 employees, and 53% of non-adopting Korean SMEs cited employee skills as their greatest barrier (OECD/Korea Labor Institute). The 2025 AI Voucher program addressed this buying constraint by offering up to KRW 200 million per project, requiring private matching, and requiring eligible SMEs, mid-sized firms, medical institutions, or small merchants to apply with a domestic AI-solution supplier (NIPA AI Voucher). For this segment, a narrow workflow, fixed milestones, reusable architecture, and a time-boxed transfer plan are generally more viable than an open-ended dedicated FDE; subsidy availability, labor shortages, and a short-payback use case are the practical adoption triggers (OECD/Korea Labor Institute, NIPA AI Voucher).

AI-native firms and digital platforms. These buyers usually need less basic workflow translation but more technical co-development around model access, compute, localization, evaluation, reliability, and speed to market (MSS LLM Challenge, Kakao–OpenAI collaboration). The Ministry of SMEs and Startups created its 2025 LLM Challenge after repeated field requests for access to large-company LLM infrastructure and real-world collaboration; KT, Naver Cloud, and Oracle opened 19 projects to 20 selected startups with up to KRW 100 million per participant (MSS LLM Challenge). Kakao’s combination of proprietary models, external OpenAI APIs, ChatGPT Enterprise, technical collaboration, and joint product development shows the high-control, multi-model buying motion of a technically capable platform (Kakao–OpenAI collaboration).

Across archetypes, providers should qualify accounts on outcome value, deployment novelty, buyer readiness, strategic learning, and access to an appropriate funding or procurement channel (OpenAI Seoul FDE, NIPA AI Voucher, PPS catalogue tender). Minimum readiness signals are an empowered sponsor, a named workflow and KPI, accessible data and systems, internal technical and security counterparts, a viable approval path, and a named post-launch owner (NIA public-AI guidance, OpenAI Seoul FDE). Useful funnel measures are time to data access, regulatory or procurement cycle time, prototype-to-production conversion, user adoption, business-KPI movement, capability transfer, and custom engineering effort per account (NIA public-AI guidance, OpenAI Seoul FDE).

Tensions:

  • Need versus purchasing capacity: SMEs have the widest adoption and skills gap, while large enterprises are better able to fund dedicated FDE capacity (OECD/Korea Labor Institute, OpenAI Seoul FDE).
  • Urgency versus control: financial reform released a substantial proposal queue, but regulated buyers still face security and approval duties that lengthen the buying motion (Financial Services Commission).
  • Innovation versus procurement standardization: NIA documents diverse public pilots, while the PPS catalogue emphasizes predefined secure services that may fit routine adoption better than discovery-led engineering (NIA public-AI guidance, PPS catalogue tender).
  • Internal capability versus external acceleration: technically strong chaebol and AI-native buyers have less need to outsource ownership but more ability to benefit from temporary co-development with a frontier provider (Kakao–OpenAI collaboration, OpenAI Seoul FDE).
  • Observed demand versus demonstrated outcomes: application counts, program participation, hiring, and provider contract announcements establish market activity but do not independently prove durable adoption, productivity gains, or the causal return from FDE (Financial Services Commission, Samsung SDS, OpenAI Seoul FDE).

Unresolved questions: The reviewed Korean hiring, program, and provider evidence does not disclose direct-FDE contract values, utilization, conversion rates, or willingness-to-pay thresholds (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Samsung SDS, NIPA AI Voucher). The reviewed evidence also does not isolate FDE’s incremental return relative to a capable internal team, SI, cloud/MSP, or consultancy, while comparable adoption and procurement data remain thinner for healthcare, energy, defense, and other regulated sectors than for finance and public administration (OECD/Korea Labor Institute, Financial Services Commission, NIA public-AI guidance).

5.2 Use-case and industry archetypes

Korea’s forward-deployment opportunity divides into two related archetypes: digital agents that work through documents, enterprise applications, databases, and approval flows; and physical-AI systems that sense, decide, or act through factories, robots, vehicles, equipment, and industrial networks (Samsung SDS, NIST). Manufacturing knowledge agents remain on the digital side while they advise humans, but they become OT-integrated when their outputs change production schedules, inspection dispositions, material movement, machine settings, or robot behavior (Samsung Electronics).

The practical threshold for embedded engineering is unresolved coupling to customer-specific workflows, data, systems, permissions, physical processes, or safety constraints (Samsung SDS, NIST). Standard implementation can serve isolated copilots, common connectors, repeatable automation, or packaged robots within a previously validated operating envelope, whereas an FDE-style team becomes valuable when an agent touches core transactions, coordinates many systems, or affects quality, uptime, safety, or regulated decisions (Samsung SDS, ISO). Physical AI has the stronger default case for embedded work because ISO treats an industrial robot as partly completed machinery and separates robot-level requirements from integration into a complete application (ISO).

Digital and knowledge-work agents

Korean demand for digital agents is most visible in finance, public administration, and enterprise-wide knowledge work (Samsung SDS). Samsung SDS reported in June 2025 that FabriX served about 70 public, financial, and manufacturing customers and more than 130,000 users, indicating that a common platform can spread across industries once enterprise access and data layers are in place (Samsung SDS).

Woori Bank’s announced program shows the more deeply embedded archetype: more than 175 agents across 29 workflows in customer relationship management and corporate lending, wealth management, internal control, customer service, and automation (Samsung SDS). The supplier is expected to connect the agents to existing banking systems, establish a data-management layer, and stage delivery from roughly 90 initial agents by December 2026 to the broader portfolio by August 2027 (Samsung SDS). The bank expects about a 30% processing-speed improvement, but this is a forecast rather than a realized outcome (Samsung SDS) ⚠️.

The value mechanism is not simply better language-model output; it is the redesign of a high-volume workflow around trusted enterprise context, system tools, permissions, exception handling, human approvals, and measurable operating outcomes (Samsung SDS). This work favors FDEs with software and data engineering, API integration, agent evaluation, identity and security, product judgment, Korean-language and domain fluency, and the ability to coordinate business, architecture, risk, and change teams (Samsung SDS).

Digital-agent economics can improve as a provider reuses its platform, connector library, model gateway, policy layer, and evaluation harness across users and accounts (Samsung SDS). Samsung SDS reported a finance code-conversion proof of concept with a 98.8% conversion rate and roughly 68% lower development cost than manual conversion, and it reported completing 1,500 finance-expense tasks in five hours rather than 24 (Samsung SDS) ⚠️. Both figures are provider-reported PoC or internal results, so they should guide hypothesis formation rather than serve as independent evidence of general market ROI (Samsung SDS) ⚠️.

Physical AI and OT-integrated deployment

Physical-AI demand is unusually important in Korea because national programs explicitly prioritize machinery, robotics, shipbuilding, secondary batteries, and semiconductors (MOTIE). MOTIE’s autonomous-manufacturing strategy targeted 200 anchor projects, an increase in diffusion from 9% to 30% by 2030, and a 20% productivity increase, while also calling for 13,000 trained professionals and more than 250 specialist solution firms (MOTIE). The same program calls for detailed process analysis before deploying software, robots, and systems, which directly supports an embedded rather than catalogue-only delivery model (MOTIE).

Hyundai Motor Group and NVIDIA moved in 2025 from adopting platforms to jointly developing physical-AI capabilities for mobility, smart factories, and on-device semiconductors (Hyundai Motor Group). Their announced architecture links training, validation, and deployment on 50,000 Blackwell GPUs, while the wider Korean collaboration includes an approximately USD 3 billion investment and physical-AI application, technology, and data centers (Hyundai Motor Group). Samsung Electronics separately announced a 2030 plan to integrate AI from inbound logistics through production, inspection, and shipment using digital twins and specialized quality, production, and logistics agents (Samsung Electronics). Samsung’s plan extends into EHS and commits to embedding safety mechanisms at the initial design stage as autonomy expands (Samsung Electronics).

Shipbuilding demonstrates why physical deployment remains site-specific even when the strategic platform is centralized (HD Hyundai). HD Hyundai Heavy Industries and HD Hyundai Robotics plan to co-develop shipyard-specific robot, vision, and AI solutions, validate them progressively in actual work environments, and only then standardize an optimal process model (HD Hyundai). Their stated targets include production stability, efficiency, productivity, shorter process duration, and quality competitiveness, but these remain program targets rather than independently demonstrated results (HD Hyundai) ⚠️.

Aerospace maintenance shows a more productized physical system that still required extensive field learning (Hyundai Motor Group). Korean Air became the first customer for Hyundai and Kia’s X-ble Shoulder wearable robot and planned deployment across multiple aerospace assembly and maintenance operations (Hyundai Motor Group). More than 300 on-site workers had participated in prototype trials that informed the product’s technology and ergonomics (Hyundai Motor Group). Hyundai reported reductions of up to 60% in shoulder-joint load and up to 30% in deltoid muscle activity, but those figures require independent validation before they are generalized (Hyundai Motor Group) ⚠️.

Physical-AI FDE teams therefore require software, data, and model skills plus industrial sensing, controls and OT architecture, robotics or vision, simulation and digital twins, systems safety, reliability, commissioning, and sustained work with operators and maintenance teams (NIST, ISO). NIST identifies industrial-data complexity, heterogeneous sensing and control, and trustworthy, explainable, reliable operation as critical smart-manufacturing deployment challenges (NIST). The lifecycle must consequently include process baselining, instrumentation, simulation where useful, hazard analysis, representative on-site testing, constrained commissioning, manual override, monitoring, and change control (HD Hyundai, ISO).

The economic profile is more capital- and labor-intensive than digital deployment because physical systems add hardware, on-site data collection, simulation, safety engineering, production trials, commissioning, maintenance, field support, and sometimes dedicated networks or compute (NIST, MOTIE). Scale is still possible through standardized cells, foundation models, digital-twin assets, and sector templates, but site variance limits engineer leverage until interfaces, validation protocols, and operating envelopes become repeatable (MOTIE, HD Hyundai).

Korean industry pattern and role implications

Korea’s digital-agent market should concentrate scarce embedded talent on high-volume or high-consequence workflows in finance, public administration, telecom, commerce, software operations, and group-wide enterprise functions (Samsung SDS, Samsung SDS). Its physical-AI market should concentrate on high-value production and asset environments in semiconductors, batteries, automotive, shipbuilding, heavy industry, logistics, and aerospace (MOTIE, Hyundai Motor Group).

The delivery ecosystem is becoming cluster- and consortium-shaped rather than vendor-only (MOTIE). Korea’s 2026 industrial-complex initiative involved about 500 industry, academic, and research organizations, called for shared AI models around core processes and tacit manufacturing knowledge, and planned private-5G pilots for real-time, low-latency manufacturing data (MOTIE). This structure favors multidisciplinary deployment pods joining anchor manufacturers, captive IT teams, robot and equipment makers, global model or compute vendors, Korean AI specialists, institutes, and regional infrastructure providers (MOTIE, Hyundai Motor Group).

Employers should treat a digital FDE as a workflow-and-platform integrator and a physical-AI FDE as a systems integrator and outcome owner inside a multidisciplinary pod, rather than expect one generalist to replace domain, controls, safety, and maintenance specialists (NIST, ISO). Digital KPIs should emphasize completed workflows, exception and human-review rates, policy compliance, latency, cost, adoption, throughput, and component reuse, while physical KPIs should add yield, scrap, uptime, cycle time, unsafe events, commissioning time, performance drift, operator workload, and recovery time (Samsung SDS, NIST).

Tensions:

  • Digital agents promise software scale, but production value depends on customer-specific data, permissions, system integration, and exception handling that can push delivery back toward services economics (Samsung SDS).
  • Group-wide AI factories and platforms promise standardization, while shipyard and wearable-robot evidence shows that physical performance still depends on actual-environment validation and operator feedback (HD Hyundai, Hyundai Motor Group).
  • Both archetypes require governance, but physical-AI failure adds injury, equipment damage, defective output, and production interruption to the information, transaction, and compliance harms associated with digital agents (ISO, NIST).
  • Korean policy frames autonomy as a productivity and workforce response, while current deployment practice still depends on operators’ tacit knowledge and participation in design and validation (MOTIE, Hyundai Motor Group).
  • Announced investment and deployment volume demonstrate market activity, but most quantified benefits remain forecasts or provider-reported PoCs rather than independently verified production outcomes (Samsung SDS, Samsung SDS, HD Hyundai) ⚠️.

Unresolved questions:

  • How do realized ROI, failure rates, and time-to-production compare between Korean digital-agent and physical-AI deployments?
  • What are the staffing ratios, project prices, commissioning costs, and provider margins for each archetype?
  • How large is deployable Korean physical-AI demand in healthcare, energy, construction, and defense relative to manufacturing, mobility, shipbuilding, and aerospace?

5.3 Sovereignty, localization, regulation, and procurement gates

Korea is not a single, blanket data-localization market: PIPA permits safeguarded cross-border transfers to jurisdictions with equivalent protection or certain certified companies, while allowing the regulator to suspend unlawful transfers (PIPC). In practice, “sovereignty” is a layered control problem—data movement, infrastructure and administrator control, model and corpus choice, Korean-language behavior, documentation, and regulatory inspectability—whose required depth depends on the buyer and use case (PIPC generative-AI guidance, PPS, FSC, MFDS). Local hosting is therefore one architecture option or buyer requirement, not by itself proof that a deployment satisfies Korean privacy, security, sector, and procurement rules (PIPC, KISA CSAP).

The horizontal AI regime became operational when the AI Basic Act and its Enforcement Decree took effect on January 22, 2026 (MSIT). Providers using generative or high-impact AI must support advance notice, while generative outputs require recognizable labeling; high-impact analysis covers energy, drinking water, healthcare, nuclear, criminal investigation, recruitment, credit assessment, transportation, public services, and education (MSIT). MSIT granted an implementation grace period of at least one year, but that does not suspend PIPA, public-security qualification, financial controls, medical-device review, or contractual duties (MSIT, PIPC, KISA CSAP, FSC, MFDS). PIPC’s 2025 guidance further expects purpose and legal-basis analysis, architecture-specific risk reduction, layered controls for data poisoning, jailbreak and agent risks, preservation of data-subject rights, and CPO-led governance across service APIs, adapted open-source models, and self-developed models (PIPC generative-AI guidance).

Public-sector entry is the most explicit infrastructure and procurement gate (PPS, KISA CSAP). The 2025 generative-AI catalogue for government work networks accepted only independently operable services built on high-grade government-cloud IaaS and passing high-grade NIS security verification (PPS). CSAP assessment for public-workload cloud providers includes preliminary review, on-site and vulnerability testing, remediation verification, certification-committee review, and certificate issuance (KISA CSAP). NIS has nevertheless proposed moving public networks from uniform separation toward multi-level security based on data importance, indicating a shift from categorical isolation to differentiated access and stronger evidence of control (NIS). As of the July 19, 2026 research cutoff, an amendment scheduled for July 21 would require public buyers to give priority consideration to AI products verified through KOSA and TTA review or separately designated by MSIT, but its operational tender impact was not yet observable (MSIT public-procurement amendment).

Financial services use a two-track architecture rather than one permitted model (FSC). FSC opened commercial cloud AI through regulatory sandboxing while supporting open-source models installed inside financial-company networks (FSC). The regulator positioned external commercial AI mainly for work not using personal credit information, while internal models can support tasks using proprietary information (FSC). This makes data classification, network placement, compensating controls, security inspection, and human accountability part of system design rather than post-build approval work (FSC). Korean adaptation is also substantive: FSC found that major English-centric open models lacked Korean and finance-domain capability and initiated a finance-specific Korean corpus for training and evaluation (FSC).

Medical AI adds a different localization burden (MFDS). MFDS’s 2025 guidance calls for objective performance and clinical validation using test data independent from training data, and diagnostic or predictive digital medical devices should use Korean test data unless the applicant establishes that racial factors do not affect performance (MFDS). A deployer must consequently preserve training-data provenance, cloud configuration, test independence, Korean-population applicability, clinical metrics, software validation, and cybersecurity evidence as a traceable approval package (MFDS).

The local supplier opportunity follows these infrastructure, evidence, and procurement gates (KISA CSAP, PPS). NAVER Cloud describes a sovereign-AI stack spanning services, data, AI backbone, supercomputing, cloud, and data centers, and cites Korea Hydro & Nuclear Power and the Bank of Korea as security-critical implementations; these provider-authored claims demonstrate offering scope and reported activity, not independent comparative performance or outcomes (NAVER Cloud) ⚠️. The broader competitive implication is that a global model vendor may need a Korean cloud, SI, security assessor, or accountable operator around its model when local infrastructure, documentation, certification, or incident response determines eligibility (KISA CSAP, PPS, FSC).

For an FDE team, the correct first artifact is a gate matrix covering buyer type, data classes and legal bases, cross-border processors, generative or high-impact status, sector regulator, network zone, required certification or review, procurement route, and evidence owner (MSIT, PIPC generative-AI guidance, PPS). The team should map prompts, retrieval stores, embeddings, telemetry, training use, support access, retention, deletion, and incident response, then choose among public cloud, Korean-region cloud, sovereign or private cloud, on-premises, and isolated operation according to those gates (PIPC generative-AI guidance, FSC, PPS). The FDE owns traceability from customer and regulator decisions into architecture, tests, documentation, and remediation, while final legal and risk decisions remain with the customer’s CPO, CISO, legal, compliance, and sector authority (PIPC generative-AI guidance, KISA CSAP, MFDS).

Companies should therefore expect Korea-facing FDEs to combine software and cloud engineering with Korean-language evaluation, bilingual evidence writing, security architecture, and regulator- and procurement-facing coordination (FSC, KISA CSAP, MFDS). Leading indicators should include classification lead time, documented legal bases and processors, control-test pass rate, unresolved security findings, Korean-domain performance, approval-evidence completeness, and prototype-to-production approval time; outcome indicators should include unauthorized egress, incidents, user-right performance, approval on the intended network, and reuse of controls across customers (PIPC generative-AI guidance, KISA CSAP, FSC). Private or isolated infrastructure, Korean datasets, certification, and regulator-facing documentation add fixed cost, while reusable certified architectures, domain corpora, and catalogue eligibility can amortize that cost across deployments (KISA CSAP, FSC, PPS).

Tensions:

  • Cross-border legality versus buyer sovereignty: PIPA permits safeguarded transfers, while public and regulated procurement can still make local, private, or isolated operation a practical eligibility condition (PIPC, PPS, FSC).
  • Separation versus adaptive security: NIS and FSC identified blanket separation as an obstacle to AI and cloud use, but risk-tiered relaxation requires stronger assessment, monitoring, and accountability (NIS, FSC).
  • Grace period versus immediate obligations: the AI Basic Act’s grace period coexists with active privacy, sector, security, procurement, and contractual requirements (MSIT, PIPC, MFDS).
  • Global capability versus Korean control: frontier services may maximize general capability, while Korean or private deployments can improve local-language, domain-data, administrator, and infrastructure control; the reviewed evidence does not establish one dominant architecture for every use case (FSC, PIPC generative-AI guidance).
  • Standardization versus iteration: certification and catalogue routes can reduce repeat procurement friction, but changing models, tools, or service boundaries may reopen security and evidence work (KISA CSAP, PPS).

Unresolved questions: Public evidence does not provide comparable review lead times, rejection rates, or total compliance costs across CSAP, NIS review, financial sandboxing, MFDS review, and ordinary enterprise procurement (KISA CSAP, NIS, FSC, MFDS). Implementation practice for the July 21 public-AI preference was not observable at the July 19 cutoff, and no reviewed authority establishes one Korea-wide threshold for mandatory local hosting or indigenous models outside specific sector, security, or buyer contexts (MSIT public-procurement amendment, PIPC). Independent comparative evidence on Korean-language accuracy, safety, and cost across Korean sovereign models, global frontier models, and hybrid RAG or fine-tuned systems also remains limited in the reviewed record (FSC).

5.4 Outcomes, failure modes, and sourcing choice

An FDE engagement should count as durable only when the deployed system remains integrated into the real workflow, is repeatedly used, stays within quality and safety thresholds as conditions change, can be monitored and recovered, and produces value greater than its whole-life cost; NIST specifically treats real-world post-deployment monitoring as essential because AI behavior can vary unpredictably, while the UK DDaT Playbook requires sourcing analysis to include setup, operation, decommissioning, continuous improvement, and knowledge transfer (NIST; UK DDaT Playbook). A strong outcome chain therefore runs from technical quality and reliability, through workflow completion and exception rates, to active adoption, time or quality improvement, financial or mission impact, and full operating cost (NBER; UK DDaT Playbook). The NBER customer-support field study illustrates the required evidence standard: roughly 5,000 agents, gradual rollout and comparison data, task-throughput and resolution measures, and a customer-satisfaction check produced an estimated 13.8% increase in issues resolved per hour without a significant satisfaction decline (NBER).

Korean evidence warns against equating usage or time saved with enterprise value. The Bank of Korea estimated that generative-AI use reduced working time by 3.8% but increased potential productivity by only 1.0%, indicating that workflow bottlenecks and the use of released capacity determine how much saving becomes output (Bank of Korea). IMF analysis of Korean firm data found no significant average productivity effect from AI adoption, a positive profitability effect, and productivity benefits concentrated in larger, more mature firms (IMF). OECD/KLI evidence likewise found that 95.5% of surveyed Korean firms reported no department- or team-level workforce change after adoption, while its manufacturing case concluded that production-worker domain involvement remained crucial to better AI learning outcomes (OECD/KLI).

Failure usually begins before engineering. RAND interviews identified misunderstanding or miscommunication of the business problem and poor workflow fit as leading causes of AI-project failure, which makes problem definition and business ownership core FDE work rather than optional discovery (RAND). McKinsey’s 2025 survey found that only 39% of respondents reported enterprise-level EBIT impact, while its roughly 6% group of AI high performers reported redesigning workflows as part of their approach (McKinsey). Stanford’s selected sample of 51 successful deployments reported that 77% of the hardest challenges involved change management, data quality, or process redesign and that 61% had suffered a prior failure, but the sample was deliberately success-oriented and should not be read as a population success rate (Stanford Digital Economy Lab). ⚠️

The practical sourcing choice is therefore a division of responsibilities, not a single-provider beauty contest:

  • Use a product or model vendor’s FDE team when success depends on proprietary platform internals, first-of-kind co-development, rapid diagnosis of product behavior, or converting customer discoveries into the core product; retain an internal business owner because contract monitoring and workflow responsibility persist after vendor selection (GAO; UK DDaT Playbook).

  • Use a strategy or transformation consultancy when the primary gap is portfolio selection, business-case design, operating-model change, governance, executive alignment, or benefits realization; RAND’s failure evidence indicates that this problem-framing layer can be decisive, but it does not by itself prove production engineering or long-term operating capacity (RAND).

  • Use a Korean SI or MSP when the hard part is multi-system and legacy integration, Korean-language delivery, private or on-premises infrastructure, regulated documentation, scaled rollout, OT site work, or sustained local operations; Korean manufacturing evidence shows why domain workers and production context cannot be abstracted away from the implementation (OECD/KLI).

  • Use a specialist for a bounded, testable bottleneck—such as Korean-language evaluation, safety, security, data engineering, computer vision, robotics, or regulated validation—but assign an explicit prime integrator because federal AI-acquisition guidance requires system-level testing, monitoring, performance terms, and lock-in protection rather than isolated component acceptance (GAO).

  • Use an internal product or platform team when the workflow is strategically differentiating, continuously changing, highly sensitive, or inseparable from retained risk and process authority; IMF and OECD/KLI evidence suggests that complementary organizational assets and domain participation materially condition Korean outcomes (IMF; OECD/KLI).

  • Use a bridge or partner coalition when global product expertise must be combined with Korean infrastructure, assurance, integration, and operations; the UK DDaT Playbook defines this hybrid as external capability filling a gap until the internal organization can own and run the requirement (UK DDaT Playbook).

For consequential Korean deployments, the most defensible default is a bridge model: the buyer retains programme, domain, data, security, risk, and benefits ownership; the product vendor owns platform behavior and product defects; and a Korean SI, MSP, or specialist owns the agreed local integration, assurance, or operating layer (UK DDaT Playbook; GAO). The buyer should define the baseline and scale-or-stop thresholds before procurement, test proposed systems on representative buyer data and workflows, compare options on whole-life cost, and contract data and IP rights, portability, model-change notice, monitoring, performance, transfer, termination, and decommissioning (GAO; UK DDaT Playbook). The operating model should then migrate as the deployment matures: scarce vendor FDE capacity can lead first-of-kind stabilization, reusable work can pass to partners, and a trained internal or managed team can assume steady-state ownership (UK DDaT Playbook).

Economically, the warning signs are indefinite hypercare, rising manual-review cost, recurring account-specific customization, compliance rework, undocumented supplier knowledge, and labor-saving claims that cannot be captured because approvals or downstream work remain unchanged; the Bank of Korea’s gap between time saving and potential productivity and Stanford’s hidden-cost findings illustrate these risks (Bank of Korea; Stanford Digital Economy Lab). ⚠️ A provider should therefore be judged on stable production, outcome attainment, adoption, reliability, reusable improvement, and successful transfer rather than custom-code volume or billable utilization (NIST; UK DDaT Playbook).

Tensions: High-context FDE work can solve first-of-kind adoption failures but becomes uneconomic when it turns into permanent bespoke labor; vendor expertise accelerates diagnosis but does not replace buyer-owned domain judgment and risk acceptance; reusable global products lower cost but Korean language, legacy, regulated, and OT conditions preserve the need for local adaptation; and best-of-breed multi-party sourcing increases specialization while creating boundary failures unless one party owns end-to-end integration (RAND; OECD/KLI; GAO).

Unresolved questions:

  • How do Korean vendor FDEs, consultancies, SIs, specialists, partner coalitions, and internal teams compare on matched use cases using whole-life cost, reliability, adoption, and realized ROI?

  • What are Korea-specific FDE prices, staffing ratios, transfer success rates, renewal rates, and incident-performance benchmarks?

  • How much outcome variance is caused by the delivery model itself rather than buyer maturity, use-case selection, model quality, and complementary investment?

  • Which Korean agentic and physical-AI deployments have sustained independently measured outcomes beyond pilots?

6. Market development, size, and trajectory

Estimate the narrow titled-FDE market and the broader functional market using labor demand, deployment capacity, AI-services revenue, partnership capacity, and Korea-specific bottom-up proxies.

6 Market development, size, and trajectory

There is no defensible single-point estimate for Korea’s forward-deployed engineering market. The result below is a triangulation framework: it separates a narrow titled-role stock, a broader provider-side FDE-equivalent capacity stock, buyer-owned capacity, and attributable deployment revenue. These quantities must not be added together because headcount, internal capacity, supplier revenue, job vacancies, certifications, customers, and contracts measure different things.

The narrow category counts Korea-based people explicitly titled Forward Deployed Engineer, Forward Deployed Software Engineer, or Forward Deployed AI Engineer, plus only clearly documented vendor-side equivalents that combine customer proximity, production engineering, deployment accountability, and a feedback path into the core product. Current role evidence establishes that this category exists but does not reveal its incumbent headcount: OpenAI advertises a Seoul FDE role that owns discovery, technical scoping, system design, build, and production rollout; Palantir lists a Seoul Forward Deployed Software Engineer; and BCG X has advertised a South Korea Principal with the exact title Forward Deployed AI Engineer (OpenAI Seoul FDE role, Palantir careers, BCG X role). A vacancy is therefore evidence of hiring flow and employer presence, not evidence that one person—or only one person—is already employed.

The broader functional category counts the share of project managers, consultants, developers, architects, reliability and operations staff, SI engineers, and specialist pods actually assigned to embedded, customer-specific production deployment with outcome accountability. SPRi’s 2024 AI Industry Survey identified 2,517 Korean AI businesses and 54,039 AI workers as of June 2024, including 4,649 project managers, 2,741 consultants, 39,368 developers, and 1,987 system operations or management workers (SPRi 2024 AI Industry Survey). Those four occupational groups total 48,745 people and form a useful candidate pool, but only the fraction doing qualifying customer deployment work should be counted as FDE-equivalent capacity (SPRi 2024 AI Industry Survey). Product R&D, presales-only work, recommendation-only consulting, generic managed services, and staff augmentation without outcome ownership remain outside the functional count.

Demand-side triangulation

The addressable demand unit should be an active complex deployment, not every organization that reports using AI. The OECD/KLI survey frame covered 9,625 AI-using establishments with at least ten employees across manufacturing, information and communications, professional and scientific services, and healthcare, while published Korean firm-adoption estimates ranged from 6.35% to 30.3% across different surveys and populations (OECD/KLI). This denominator variation makes it unsafe to multiply one national adoption rate by a generic deployment fee.

Public procurement provides a harder annual flow measure. SPRi identified 6,975 Korean public-sector AI adoption contracts awarded to 1,803 suppliers, with an average contract value of KRW 1.68 billion, but that broad contract universe includes work outside the strict FDE boundary (SPRi public-sector AI procurement study). The narrower generative-AI flow grew from 24 contracts worth KRW 47.8 billion in 2023 to 42 contracts worth KRW 69.5 billion in 2024 (SPRi public-sector AI procurement study). The 75% increase in contract count and 45.4% increase in value are useful leading indicators, but the small base and procurement lag make them unsuitable as a stand-alone market-growth forecast (SPRi public-sector AI procurement study).

The SME channel is another distinct demand mechanism. Korea’s AI Voucher Programme had a government budget of approximately KRW 27.6 billion in 2025, and its registered provider pool grew from 553 in 2020 to more than 2,000 in 2023 (OECD AI Voucher Programme review). The program is oriented toward purchasing and applying pre-developed solutions, so only its customization-intensive projects should enter the broad FDE market rather than the full voucher budget or every registered supplier.

Avoiding double counting

A recurring market tracker should maintain six linked but separate ledgers:

  1. A role-posting ledger that deduplicates reposts and records employer, exact title, Korea location, lifecycle ownership, coding requirement, date, and vacancy status.
  2. An employer-team roster that records disclosed local incumbents or, when unavailable, a bounded and confidence-graded range.
  3. A canonical provider register that crosswalks SPRi AI businesses, voucher suppliers, public-contract winners, and model or cloud partner directories to one legal entity and ultimate parent.
  4. A deployment ledger keyed by customer, use case, and production environment, with one accountable prime plus separately attributed product-vendor and subcontractor roles.
  5. A revenue ledger split into software or model entitlement, customer-specific build and integration, adoption and change, and recurring operations.
  6. A buyer-owned capacity ledger that measures internal substitution and counterpart capability without adding it to provider revenue.

Partner capacity deserves special caution. OpenAI launched a Forward Deployed Experts pilot to align qualified partner practitioners with its FDE teams and set a global goal of enabling 300,000 certified consultants by the end of 2026 (OpenAI Partner Network). Anthropic reported more than 40,000 partner applicants and more than 10,000 certified consultants by June 2026, but its more decision-useful Select threshold combines at least ten active certified people with at least two joint customers deployed in production during the trailing year (Anthropic Services Track). Global training or certification counts should therefore be treated as an upper-bound capability signal; only Korea-based practitioners with recent production work belong in local deployable capacity.

Trajectory scenarios

Korea’s national strategy sets 2030 AI-adoption targets of 70% for industry and 95% for the public sector, but these are policy ambitions rather than market forecasts or counts of FDE-eligible customers (MSIT national AI strategy). Against that backdrop, three planning scenarios are useful:

  • Standardization: accountable deployment revenue grows roughly 8%-12% annually, while FDE-equivalent headcount grows 0%-5% as productization, automation, partners, and internal customer teams absorb repeat work.
  • Hybrid scaling: accountable deployment revenue grows roughly 15%-25% and FDE-equivalent capacity 10%-15%, with a small direct-vendor core orchestrating larger certified local delivery pods.
  • Sovereign and industrial surge: revenue grows roughly 25%-35% and capacity 15%-25% as regulated, defense, public, manufacturing, and OT deployments sustain demand for local integration, assurance, and on-site work.

These are unweighted scenarios rather than forecasts. The indicators that should determine which path is materializing are production contract value rather than pilot announcements, Korea-based partner practitioners with recent deployments, deployment revenue per FTE, repeat-deployment labor intensity, internal-team hiring, production conversion, and the share of work migrating from custom build to productized platform or managed operation.

Tensions: Title searches prove that a Korean FDE market exists but undercount incumbents and functional substitutes, whereas counting the full 54,039-person AI workforce would substantially overstate deployment capacity (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Palantir careers, BCG X role, SPRi survey). The KRW 1.866 trillion build/manage category is too broad to equal FDE revenue, yet deployment labor funded inside software subscriptions is not visible in that services line (SPRi survey). Global certifications indicate ecosystem formation but do not establish Korea location, availability, or production accountability (OpenAI Partner Network, Anthropic Services Track). Published adoption estimates range from 6.35% to 30.3% because the denominators and definitions differ, so blending them would manufacture precision rather than resolve it (OECD/KLI). Finally, stronger in-house teams can both displace repetitive outsourced work and enable more sophisticated co-development, so internal capacity is neither purely competitive nor additive to provider revenue.

Unresolved questions: No authoritative source reports incumbent Korea-based FDE headcount by employer. The strict-FDE share of Korean AI build/manage revenue remains unknown. Korea-based active partner practitioners and their recent production deployments are not publicly consolidated. Fully loaded FDE cost, bill rates, utilization, and revenue per FDE-equivalent are also unavailable, preventing a defensible labor-to-revenue conversion.

The program is oriented toward purchasing and applying pre-developed solutions, so only its customization-intensive projects should enter the broad FDE market rather than the full voucher budget or every registered supplier.

Avoiding double counting

A recurring market tracker should maintain six linked but separate ledgers:

  1. A role-posting ledger that deduplicates reposts and records employer, exact title, Korea location, lifecycle ownership, coding requirement, date, and vacancy status.
  2. An employer-team roster that records disclosed local incumbents or, when unavailable, a bounded and confidence-graded range.
  3. A canonical provider register that crosswalks SPRi AI businesses, voucher suppliers, public-contract winners, and model or cloud partner directories to one legal entity and ultimate parent.
  4. A deployment ledger keyed by customer, use case, and production environment, with one accountable prime plus separately attributed product-vendor and subcontractor roles.
  5. A revenue ledger split into software or model entitlement, customer-specific build and integration, adoption and change, and recurring operations.
  6. A buyer-owned capacity ledger that measures internal substitution and counterpart capability without adding it to provider revenue.

Partner capacity deserves special caution. OpenAI launched a Forward Deployed Experts pilot to align qualified partner practitioners with its FDE teams and set a global goal of enabling 300,000 certified consultants by the end of 2026 (OpenAI Partner Network). Anthropic reported more than 40,000 partner applicants and more than 10,000 certified consultants by June 2026, but its more decision-useful Select threshold combines at least ten active certified people with at least two joint customers deployed in production during the trailing year (Anthropic Services Track). Global training or certification counts should therefore be treated as an upper-bound capability signal; only Korea-based practitioners with recent production work belong in local deployable capacity.

Trajectory scenarios

Korea’s national strategy sets 2030 AI-adoption targets of 70% for industry and 95% for the public sector, but these are policy ambitions rather than market forecasts or counts of FDE-eligible customers (MSIT national AI strategy). Against that backdrop, three planning scenarios are useful:

  • Standardization: accountable deployment revenue grows roughly 8%-12% annually, while FDE-equivalent headcount grows 0%-5% as productization, automation, partners, and internal customer teams absorb repeat work.
  • Hybrid scaling: accountable deployment revenue grows roughly 15%-25% and FDE-equivalent capacity 10%-15%, with a small direct-vendor core orchestrating larger certified local delivery pods.
  • Sovereign and industrial surge: revenue grows roughly 25%-35% and capacity 15%-25% as regulated, defense, public, manufacturing, and OT deployments sustain demand for local integration, assurance, and on-site work.

These are unweighted scenarios rather than forecasts. The indicators that should determine which path is materializing are production contract value rather than pilot announcements, Korea-based partner practitioners with recent deployments, deployment revenue per FTE, repeat-deployment labor intensity, internal-team hiring, production conversion, and the share of work migrating from custom build to productized platform or managed operation.

Tensions: Title searches prove that a Korean FDE market exists but undercount incumbents and functional substitutes, whereas counting the full 54,039-person AI workforce would substantially overstate deployment capacity (OpenAI Seoul FDE, Palantir careers, BCG X role, SPRi survey). The KRW 1.866 trillion build/manage category is too broad to equal FDE revenue, yet deployment labor funded inside software subscriptions is not visible in that services line (SPRi survey). Global certifications indicate ecosystem formation but do not establish Korea location, availability, or production accountability (OpenAI Partner Network, Anthropic Services Track). Published adoption estimates range from 6.35% to 30.3% because the denominators and definitions differ, so blending them would manufacture precision rather than resolve it (OECD/KLI). Finally, stronger in-house teams can both displace repetitive outsourced work and enable more sophisticated co-development, so internal capacity is neither purely competitive nor additive to provider revenue.

Unresolved questions: No authoritative source reports incumbent Korea-based FDE headcount by employer. The strict-FDE share of Korean AI build/manage revenue remains unknown. Korea-based active partner practitioners and their recent production deployments are not publicly consolidated. Fully loaded FDE cost, bill rates, utilization, and revenue per FDE-equivalent are also unavailable, preventing a defensible labor-to-revenue conversion.

7. Korean competitive ecosystem

Map direct providers, functional substitutes, buyer-owned capabilities, and partnerships by actor business model while retaining origin, Korean presence, industry, role title, and lifecycle coverage as attributes.

Korea’s forward-deployed engineering ecosystem is broader than companies using the FDE title: it includes first-party model and platform teams, Korean product-plus-delivery specialists, SIs and MSPs, traditional consultancies with engineering capacity, flexible talent, and buyer-owned AI organizations that compete or cooperate at different stages of the same engagement (OpenAI, Palantir, LG CNS, BCG X, SK Telecom). The correct competitive unit is therefore phase ownership—who defines the workflow, writes and integrates the system, approves risk, operates production, captures reusable learning, and receives the capability at handoff—not the employer’s label (OpenAI, Databricks, SK Telecom). Databricks illustrates the boundary: its Delivery Solutions Architect subscription excludes production deployment, non-Databricks tooling, business-requirement validation, and customer change management, so a customer-facing delivery title does not by itself establish FDE-like accountability (Databricks).

The strongest verified first-party FDE signals are OpenAI’s Seoul role, which owns discovery through stable production and feeds deployment evidence into product and model roadmaps, and Palantir’s Seoul FDSE role, which embeds engineers directly with customers (OpenAI, Palantir). NAVER Cloud is the clearest domestic FDE-like platform provider: it explicitly describes forward-deployed engineers for rapid prototyping, validation, and operational support in defense, and its Bank of Korea engagement places a dedicated HyperCLOVA X platform inside the bank’s closed network (NAVER defense FDE, NAVER Cloud). Upstage adds a domestic model-led pattern in which it leads technical work and financial-data fine-tuning while K Bank owns use-case selection and implementation evaluation and KT/kt cloud supplies infrastructure (Upstage). Anthropic has opened a Seoul office and entered Korean public-sector safety cooperation, but the reviewed evidence does not establish a local team with the same disclosed production ownership as OpenAI or Palantir (Anthropic, OpenAI, Palantir).

Korean AI-native specialists compete most strongly where local domain fit, secure deployment, physical integration, or assurance matters more than direct frontier-model access (Upstage, MakinaRocks defense, Selectstar). MakinaRocks builds air-gapped defense environments on Runway and uses certified domain partners for industrial applications; Superb AI sells project design through model development, integration, deployment, operations, or capability transfer; Allganize integrates hybrid RAG and LLMOps into legacy financial workflows; and Selectstar supplies tailored evaluation, red-team testing, and analysis as a production gate (MakinaRocks defense, MakinaRocks partner program, Superb AI, Allganize, Selectstar). Nota shows the hybrid economics of this group: solution projects remained material in Q1 2026 while platform contracts dominated its reported order backlog, indicating simultaneous project execution and movement toward reusable licensing (Nota). These firms can substitute for a direct vendor FDE on bounded applications, complement global vendors through Korean data, hardware, security, or evaluation, or operate as subcontractors beneath an SI or consultancy prime (MakinaRocks, Upstage, Selectstar).

The largest functional substitutes are Korean IT-service and cloud-delivery firms (Samsung SDS, LG CNS, MegazoneCloud, Bespin Global). Samsung SDS resells OpenAI while providing technical support, consulting, security, API implementation, and operations; LG CNS’s Shinhan Bank work covers parsing, retrieval, unstructured-data management, and generative-AI security; SK AX applies an internally tested transformation model to group and external clients; and MegazoneCloud and Bespin Global combine cloud partnerships with consulting, build, evaluation, governance, and run services (Samsung SDS, LG CNS, SK AX, MegazoneCloud, Bespin Global). Hyundai AutoEver occupies the captive industrial variant, integrating AI-driven IT and OT with process improvement and operational services in software-defined factories (Hyundai AutoEver). These firms substitute most directly for FDEs in local architecture, integration, Korean security approval, rollout, and continuing operations, while remaining dependent on upstream vendors when product internals, model-level debugging, or roadmap escalation determine the outcome (Samsung SDS, MegazoneCloud–AWS).

Traditional consulting has also moved into build territory (BCG X, McKinsey, Deloitte Korea). BCG X advertised a South Korea Forward Deployed AI Engineer principal role, McKinsey is recruiting a Seoul QuantumBlack data engineer for production-ready agentic systems, and Deloitte Korea advertises lifecycle engineering across custom enterprise, AI/GenAI, and data-platform work (BCG X, McKinsey, Deloitte Korea). Deloitte Korea’s alliance with MindwareWorks assigns process transformation to the consultancy and workflow-platform technology and support to the Korean specialist, illustrating how a traditional firm may be prime, partner, or co-delivery integrator rather than a stand-alone advisor (Deloitte–MindwareWorks). Flexible channels such as Wanted Gigs supply freelancers, short-term contracts, dispatched workers, and matching support, but the platform’s documented role is intermediation rather than ownership of architecture or production reliability (Wanted Gigs).

Buyer-owned teams are simultaneously customers, substitutes, governance owners, and capability-transfer destinations (SK Telecom organization, SK Telecom governance, Woori Financial Group). LG operates a tiered internal talent system from literacy through applied leadership and graduate education; SK Telecom combines a CEO-led AI organization with separate lifecycle governance and Red-Team review; KB Kookmin Bank has recruited internal validation and red-team capability; and Woori Financial Group’s AI Strategy Center procured external governance design while retaining internal sponsorship (LG, SK Telecom organization, SK Telecom governance, KB Careers, Woori Financial Group). Hyundai Motor Group and NVIDIA moved from platform adoption toward joint physical-AI innovation through paired application and technology centers, while Kakao combines proprietary models with external APIs and joint product development with OpenAI (Hyundai Motor Group, Kakao). These cases show that strong internal capability replaces routine implementation and steady-state operation while increasing demand for frontier-product access, co-development, independent assurance, and difficult production escalation (Hyundai Motor Group, Woori Financial Group).

7.1 Model, platform, and FDE-native vendors

The narrow Korean FDE market should count a model or platform vendor only when its customer-facing engineers materially build or stabilize production systems; a local office, enterprise license, reseller agreement, or advisory role is not enough. Databricks provides a useful negative boundary: its Delivery Solutions Architect service expressly excludes production deployment, non-Databricks tooling, validation of customer requirements, and customer change management (Databricks). Against that test, OpenAI and Palantir have the strongest verified direct-FDE capacity in Korea, NAVER Cloud has credible domestic FDE-like capacity with an explicit defense FDE model, Upstage is a plausible but less transparent model-led contender, and Anthropic has Korean market presence without yet-disclosed local forward-deployment engineering capacity (OpenAI, Palantir, NAVER, Upstage, Anthropic).

OpenAI — verified direct FDE capacity. OpenAI is recruiting a Seoul-based FDE who owns discovery, technical scoping, system design, build, and production rollout, and remains responsible from the first prototype through stable production (OpenAI role). The role is evaluated on production adoption and measurable workflow impact, while field evaluations are expected to influence model and product roadmaps—an explicit product-learning loop that ordinary professional services usually lack (OpenAI role). The position expects 50% travel, reinforcing that this is embedded delivery rather than remote technical advice (OpenAI role). OpenAI also has meaningful Korean account reach: Samsung Electronics agreed to provide ChatGPT Enterprise and Codex to all employees in Korea and all worldwide DX-division employees, and OpenAI named a wider Korean user group including LG Electronics, LG Uplus, LG CNS, GS E&C, Samsung SDS, TVING, Krafton, Toss, MUSINSA, Korea Zinc, Nexen Tire, and HanaTour (OpenAI Samsung deployment). That announcement proves deployment scale, but it does not disclose which workflows the Seoul FDE team built, realized productivity, adoption rates, or contractual responsibility after launch (OpenAI Samsung deployment).

Palantir — verified direct capacity plus a Korean partner-scaling model. Palantir is recruiting a hybrid Seoul Forward Deployed Software Engineer to embed with customers and build solutions around difficult operational problems (Palantir role). The role requires fluent Korean and English and prefers 25–50% client-site travel, indicating locally accessible engineering rather than sales-only presence (Palantir role). Palantir is also transferring capability into KT: KT specialists were assigned to joint projects with Palantir global engineers, the parties ran an internal-process PoC on telecommunications data, and they agreed to an exclusive mutual sales arrangement for Korean finance with joint marketing elsewhere (Palantir–KT). This hybrid route can expand local delivery beyond Palantir’s own seats, but it also creates an accountability boundary between core-platform engineers and the Korean delivery partner (Palantir–KT). Reuters reported a January 2026 expansion of the HD Hyundai relationship worth hundreds of millions of dollars over several years, providing strong evidence of commercially material Korean production work (Reuters). The companies said Palantir’s work helped HD Hyundai manufacture ships about 30% faster, but that outcome was not independently validated. ⚠️ (Reuters). Palantir’s CEO also said the company would engage selectively outside the United States, implying that Korean direct capacity may remain concentrated on high-value strategic accounts (Reuters).

NAVER Cloud — strongest documented domestic FDE challenger. In June 2026, NAVER Cloud explicitly made forward deployment of on-site engineers a pillar of its sovereign defense-AI strategy (NAVER defense FDE). It described a defense FDE organization that performs rapid prototyping, validation, and close support in operational environments, connecting field engineering to its own cloud, HyperCLOVA X models, and services (NAVER defense FDE). Its Bank of Korea contract demonstrates customer-specific sovereign delivery: NAVER Cloud contracted to provide a dedicated NeuroCloud for HyperCLOVA X platform, train the system only inside a closed bank network, and support a stable service launch (NAVER Cloud). This combination of domestic infrastructure, model ownership, closed-network operation, and field delivery makes NAVER Cloud especially relevant where Korean language, data control, disconnected operation, or local accountability outweigh access to a foreign frontier model (NAVER defense FDE, NAVER Cloud). Public evidence does not disclose NAVER Cloud’s FDE headcount, staffing model, performance system, realized Bank of Korea adoption, or post-launch incident responsibility (NAVER defense FDE, NAVER Cloud).

Upstage — credible model-led contender, but direct ownership is unclear. Upstage’s enterprise stack joins its Solar model to document parsing, information extraction, agentic execution, and workflow automation (Upstage). The company stated that Samsung uses its models, which is evidence of Korean enterprise access but not by itself proof of a dedicated FDE organization or end-to-end deployment accountability. ⚠️ (Upstage). Its public evidence does not establish who owns discovery, customer application code, security acceptance, adoption, observability, incidents, or the formal feedback path into Solar (Upstage). Upstage should therefore be included as an FDE-like domestic model vendor, but not counted one-for-one with a verified OpenAI or Palantir FDE seat until its delivery roles and operating responsibilities are disclosed (Upstage, OpenAI role, Palantir role).

Anthropic — substantial Korea presence, local FDE unverified. Anthropic opened a Seoul office in June 2026 and separately agreed with Korea’s science ministry to cooperate on Korean-language model-safety evaluation and AI-enabled cyber threats (Anthropic). Anthropic identified NAVER’s organization-wide Claude Code rollout as a major enterprise adoption example, but this provider-supplied example does not establish a Seoul Applied AI or FDE team that builds and owns Korean customer production systems. ⚠️ (Anthropic). Anthropic is therefore a serious model competitor and possible future direct-FDE entrant, while current evidence supports classifying its Korean motion as office build-out, product adoption, ecosystem activity, and partnerships rather than verified local production co-engineering (Anthropic).

Tensions: Product authority and local delivery breadth do not reside in the same place: OpenAI and Palantir offer explicit field-to-core-product loops, while NAVER Cloud offers sovereign infrastructure, domestic model ownership, and closed-network field support (OpenAI role, Palantir role, NAVER defense FDE). Partner-led scale can broaden coverage, as in Palantir–KT, but can also blur responsibility for architecture, code quality, product feedback, and incidents (Palantir–KT). Titles are unreliable market measures because NAVER Cloud now uses the FDE label, Upstage may perform adjacent model deployment without a documented FDE role, and Databricks uses a delivery title while expressly excluding production responsibility (NAVER defense FDE, Upstage, Databricks).

Unresolved questions: Public sources do not disclose Korea-specific FDE headcount, active-account load, utilization, pricing, gross margin, staffing ratios, or contract attach rates for any provider (OpenAI role, Palantir role, NAVER defense FDE, Upstage, Anthropic). Contractual ownership of incidents, service levels, customer-specific code, IP, security approval, and post-launch operation is also undisclosed for the named Korean engagements (OpenAI Samsung deployment, Reuters, NAVER Cloud). It remains unknown whether Anthropic already has Seoul-based deployment engineers, whether Upstage has a repeatable end-to-end FDE contract, and how much independently measured customer value is attributable to provider FDEs rather than customer teams or Korean delivery partners (Anthropic, Upstage, Palantir–KT).

7.2 AI-native deployment and software specialists

Korea’s AI-native specialist market is best understood as a spectrum of product-plus-delivery models rather than a list of companies using the FDE title: Upstage, MakinaRocks, Superb AI, Allganize, and Nota show the strongest evidence of combining proprietary technology with customer-specific production work, while Selectstar supplies a narrower deployment-assurance function and VUNO illustrates the more productized vertical-software boundary (Upstage, MakinaRocks, Superb AI, Allganize, Nota, Selectstar, VUNO). The practical inclusion test is functional: the specialist must carry proprietary models, software, or evaluation assets into domain adaptation, systems integration, secure deployment, production evaluation, operations, or customer capability transfer—not merely provide an API or advice (Upstage, Superb AI, Selectstar).

Upstage — domain-model and document-AI deployment. In the K Bank consortium, the parties set out to create a customized financial-sector LLM environment; Upstage led the technical work and financial-data fine-tuning, KT and kt cloud supplied compute infrastructure, and K Bank identified use cases and evaluated implementation effectiveness (Upstage–K Bank). This is a pod-like delivery model in which the AI specialist owns model adaptation while infrastructure and domain acceptance remain distributed across partners (Upstage–K Bank). Upstage’s AWS relationship adds a scalable build, train, and deploy route for future Solar models, and the company reported deployment by hundreds of organizations across finance, healthcare, legal, education, and government by August 2025 (Upstage–AWS). ⚠️

MakinaRocks — industrial and mission-critical AI deployment. MakinaRocks won a KRW 1.46 billion Agency for Defense Development project to build an AI-agent development environment for South Korean weapons systems using its proprietary Runway AI OS (MakinaRocks–ADD). Its committed scope includes infrastructure for an air-gapped, high-security network plus an agent-development environment and battlefield knowledge-base framework, which is direct evidence of customer-environment engineering rather than ordinary software licensing (MakinaRocks–ADD). MakinaRocks is also testing a partner-scaled model: it retains Runway infrastructure and difficult-environment support, while Dagyeom delivers predictive-maintenance, machine-vision inspection, and anomaly-detection applications directly to manufacturing customers (MakinaRocks partner program). The program adds technical enablement, training, delivery certification, and joint business opportunities, providing a concrete mechanism for converting direct delivery knowledge into partner capacity (MakinaRocks partner program).

Superb AI — vision/physical-AI platform plus managed execution. Superb AI explicitly offers a package spanning project design, model development, systems integration, periodic deployment, and operations, making it the clearest outsourced FDE-pod analogue in this set (Superb AI). It supports on-premises deployments, links to existing infrastructure and MLOps pipelines, and assigns onboarding and data-migration support (Superb AI). A separate internalization package trains customers to operate and improve systems with the Superb platform, so the company can sell either continuing managed delivery or a build-and-transfer path (Superb AI).

Allganize — enterprise agents and regulated-workflow integration. Allganize’s Hanwha Life implementation combined on-premises and Azure/SaaS components with RAG, LLMOps, and real-time MCI integration (Allganize–Hanwha Life). The deployed workflow drew customer coverage data from the legacy MCI system and ranked that information according to a financial planner’s natural-language intent, demonstrating last-mile integration into a live financial process (Allganize–Hanwha Life). Its principal repeatability mechanism is architectural: Alli uses one codebase for SaaS and on-premises delivery while isolating customer-specific SSO, OCR, and encryption work as plug-ins (Allganize). Allganize also advertises specialist-assisted proofs of concept producing working results within weeks and connections to ERP, groupware, collaboration, and custom systems (Allganize). ⚠️

Nota — edge optimization platform plus project-delivered vision solutions. Nota reported KRW 2.71 billion of solution revenue in Q1 2026, about 76% of quarterly revenue, and began applying percentage-of-completion accounting to new solution contracts, showing that substantial delivery obligations still sit inside project work (Nota). At the same time, NetsPresso platform contracts accounted for KRW 10.2 billion, or about 84%, of its KRW 12.1 billion order backlog, indicating that license-based platform work was the larger forward revenue base (Nota). Nota therefore provides unusually clear evidence of the central transition in this market: near-term revenue can remain solution-heavy while backlog shifts toward reusable platform economics (Nota).

Selectstar — the production-assurance specialist. Selectstar reports that Datumo Eval and tailored evaluation work were applied to projects for Shinhan Bank, Woori Bank, and NH NongHyup Bank (Selectstar). Its delivery sequence covers industry-specific evaluation criteria, representative test data, automated accuracy and safety evaluation, red-team testing, and result analysis, so it functions as a specialized production gate rather than as the builder of the complete application (Selectstar). This makes Selectstar both a complement to model vendors and SIs and a potential substitute for an internal AI-assurance team (Selectstar).

VUNO — a productized vertical benchmark, not a proven FDE analogue. VUNO reported Q3 2025 consolidated revenue of KRW 10.8 billion and operating profit of KRW 1.0 billion, evidence that a narrow regulated AI workflow can support mature product economics (VUNO). VUNO also stated that DeepCARS was deployed across approximately 50,000 Korean hospital beds by November 2025 (VUNO). ⚠️ The cited evidence demonstrates scaled clinical deployment but does not disclose comparable discovery, bespoke integration, or field-to-core-product responsibilities, so VUNO should be treated as a competitor for healthcare transformation budgets at the productized edge of this market rather than counted automatically as FDE capacity (VUNO).

The clearest scalability mechanisms are therefore shared code with bounded plug-ins at Allganize, platform-license backlog at Nota, partner training and certification at MakinaRocks, cloud distribution at Upstage, modular managed-versus-transfer packages at Superb AI, and automation of recurring evaluation steps at Selectstar (Allganize, Nota, MakinaRocks, Upstage, Superb AI, Selectstar). The reviewed evidence does not disclose comparable pricing, gross margin, billability, engineering effort per account, customer concentration, renewal, churn, or Korea-specific deployment headcount, so it cannot establish which model has the best unit economics (Upstage, MakinaRocks, Superb AI, Allganize, Nota, Selectstar). Provider-authored cases also contain much more information about selected successes than about failed deployments, total implementation cost, counterfactual ROI, or the division of labor among the specialist, customer, SI, cloud provider, and hardware partner (Upstage, Allganize, Nota).

Tensions: Product differentiation depends on reusable IP, yet the strongest Korean deployment evidence still involves tuning, legacy integration, secure infrastructure, and project execution (Upstage, MakinaRocks, Allganize, Nota). Partner delivery can increase throughput but may weaken direct product feedback and outcome accountability; managed execution can lower the buyer’s talent requirement but increase vendor dependence; and capability transfer can reduce dependence while demanding a stronger customer team (MakinaRocks, Superb AI). Assurance can be automated in a platform, but credible go/no-go decisions still require customer-specific criteria, test data, and analysis (Selectstar). Finally, VUNO’s productized scale shows that mature vertical software may displace bespoke deployment labor once a workflow and its regulatory path become sufficiently standardized (VUNO). ⚠️

Unresolved questions:

  • How many customer-facing deployment engineers does each specialist employ in Korea, and under which role titles and organizational units?
  • What share of account work is reusable configuration versus customer-specific code, integration, and long-run support?
  • How do pricing, gross margin, utilization, renewal, and customer concentration compare across direct-delivery, partner-led, and productized models?
  • Which reported deployments achieved durable adoption and ROI after accounting for the customer, SI, cloud, and hardware partners’ contributions?

7.3 Cloud, SI, IT-service, MSP, and captive-group delivery firms

Korean IT-service and cloud-delivery firms occupy a phase-specific position between competitor and partner to a model vendor’s forward-deployed team (Samsung SDS, MegazoneCloud–AWS). They substitute most directly when the work is dominated by local discovery, enterprise architecture, data and application integration, Korean security approval, production deployment, and continuing operations; they remain partners when the engagement requires proprietary model expertise, product-roadmap access, or vendor escalation (Samsung SDS, Bespin Global). Samsung SDS illustrates the hybrid structure: its OpenAI agreement makes it the Korean reseller and local delivery party for technical support, consulting, security, API-based implementation, and operations, while OpenAI remains the underlying product authority (Samsung SDS).

This is materially broader than license resale (Samsung SDS). Samsung SDS states that it accompanies the customer from ChatGPT Enterprise adoption through management and also performs consulting, build, and run services using the OpenAI API, giving it functional coverage over much of the FDE lifecycle (Samsung SDS). Its own Brity Copilot business shows the clearest captive-to-commercial flywheel: by the end of 2024, Samsung SDS had deployed the service to about 180,000 users across 17 Samsung affiliates and nine external companies (Samsung SDS earnings call). The company then used feedback from that combined internal and external user base to refine functions and split the offer into full groupware-plus-Copilot packages or narrower meeting and messenger deployments (Samsung SDS earnings call). This pattern converts an affiliate environment into a large-scale test bed, turns field feedback into reusable packaging, and then carries the resulting offer to unaffiliated customers (Samsung SDS earnings call).

LG CNS is a stronger substitute where regulated-domain integration matters more than direct access to a model vendor’s product organization (LG CNS). In its Shinhan Bank deployment, LG CNS built document parsing, chunking-based hybrid retrieval, automated updating of unstructured data, and its SecuXper AI security control rather than merely configuring a chatbot (LG CNS). Its broader financial-sector offer includes cloud interconnection architecture, generative-AI implementation consulting, security-governance establishment and operation, and pseudonymization support for network-separation requirements (LG CNS). The associated role design is correspondingly FDE-like: LG CNS defines AI engineers as people who design customer-specific agents and systems, deploy them in the field, improve performance, and customize the technology (LG CNS careers). LG CNS also reported that its “AI 100 in 100” program completed about 140 projects in roughly 60 working days and targeted 500 by year-end, but this throughput claim is provider-authored and not independently verified (LG CNS). ⚠️

SK AX demonstrates a second captive-to-commercial pattern built around the company itself as the first customer (SK AX). SK AX says its “Being AX” model was validated internally across strategy, technology, data, operating systems, talent, and change management before being taken to group and external engagements (SK AX). Public examples span SK hynix’s digital-twin and autonomous-fab work, CS Wind’s production-planning and scheduling agents, and Shinhan Financial Group’s security-, permission-, and regulation-aware employee-agent model (SK AX). Its stated deployment philosophy—redesign existing processes for agent participation, convert tacit knowledge into agent assets, and reconnect AI to the endpoint of work—places workflow redesign and knowledge capture, not model installation, at the center of delivery (SK AX).

Independent MSPs are converging on the same territory through platformized consulting-to-run services (MegazoneCloud, Bespin Global). MegazoneCloud’s GenAI360 covers consulting, implementation, and operations and has documented projects at Hansol Paper, HanaTour, and hy (MegazoneCloud). Its AWS strategic agreement explicitly targets movement from pilots into operational use while giving MegazoneCloud vendor training and dedicated AWS solution architects, so the MSP acts as the customer-facing delivery substitute while retaining an upstream technical channel to the cloud vendor (MegazoneCloud–AWS). MegazoneCloud’s disclosed base of approximately 2,800 cloud professionals and KRW 1.5106 trillion in 2023 revenue indicates delivery scale, although the evidence does not reveal AI-project utilization or margins (MegazoneCloud–AWS).

Bespin Global’s recent financial-sector work shows an MSP moving beyond infrastructure management into model, data, evaluation, governance, and workflow engineering (Bespin Global, Bespin Global). For AIA Life, a dedicated expert team standardized documents, tested combinations of small language models, tuned retrieval, and established continuous performance evaluation inside a closed private-cloud environment (Bespin Global). For Woori Financial Group, Bespin designed an AWS-based isolated research environment that links and validates multiple models and services while supporting agent design, operation, management, and governance under network-separation constraints (Bespin Global). These responsibilities closely match forward-deployed technical execution, but the employment and commercial model is an MSP-led project and managed-service relationship rather than a product-company FDE engagement (Bespin Global, Bespin Global).

Captive industrial specialists possess a different advantage: deep access to group factories, OT, process owners, and long-lived operating systems (Hyundai AutoEver). Hyundai AutoEver’s software-defined-factory offer integrates AI-driven IT and OT across the manufacturing value chain and extends from MES, digital twins, AI agents, cloud, network, and security to capability assessment, process-improvement consulting, and operations (Hyundai AutoEver). This breadth makes a captive delivery firm a strong internal substitute for an outside FDE in factory transformation, but the available evidence does not quantify how much of Hyundai AutoEver’s AI delivery is repeatedly commercialized to unaffiliated Korean customers (Hyundai AutoEver).

The practical market structure is therefore a three-party delivery stack: the model or cloud vendor supplies the core technology and escalation path; the Korean SI or MSP owns local architecture, integration, security, and run; and the buyer retains domain truth, risk acceptance, and business-outcome ownership (Samsung SDS, MegazoneCloud–AWS, Bespin Global). A Korean delivery firm is the most credible FDE substitute when it can carry an ambiguous use case through customer-specific engineering and governed production without privileged access to the upstream vendor, and it is primarily a partner when model internals, roadmap influence, or frontier-product debugging remain decisive (Samsung SDS, MegazoneCloud–AWS).

Tensions: Scale gives large IT-service firms industry specialists, security staff, and continuing run capacity, but project governance and handoffs can weaken the tight field-to-product loop associated with canonical FDE (Samsung SDS earnings call, LG CNS). Reusable platforms and affiliate learning can improve delivery leverage, yet regulated financial and industrial work still requires customer-specific data preparation, retrieval, security, integration, and process redesign (LG CNS, Bespin Global, Hyundai AutoEver). Captive lighthouse environments provide realistic users and systems, but shared ownership and internal mandates make affiliate adoption easier than arm’s-length commercialization (Samsung SDS earnings call, SK AX). Finally, end-to-end commercial promises do not eliminate fragmented liability because model behavior, cloud availability, data governance, and business acceptance remain distributed across the vendor, delivery prime, and buyer (Samsung SDS, Bespin Global).

7.4 Consulting, advisory, engineering, and flexible-talent channels

The relevant Korean competitive set is wider than firms carrying an FDE title: it includes strategy consultancies with code-writing teams, technology consultancies with lifecycle delivery capacity, Big Four practices that combine engineering with assurance, specialist boutiques, and flexible-talent platforms (McKinsey careers; Deloitte Korea; MADEITUP; Wanted Gigs). ⚠️ The practical inclusion test is whether a provider operating in Korea supplies client-facing engineers or accepts responsibility for building, integrating, deploying, or operating a system—not whether it publishes AI advice (McKinsey careers; EY Korea; Wanted Gigs). BCG X’s South Korea practice has advertised a principal role with the exact title “Forward Deployed AI Engineer,” while McKinsey’s Seoul QuantumBlack team is recruiting a client-facing data engineer to build scalable components and production-ready AI foundations for machine-learning, agentic, and autonomous systems (BCG X careers; McKinsey careers).

Strategy consultancies. BCG X and McKinsey/QuantumBlack now compete with narrow FDE providers when they place engineers inside ambiguous, high-value transformation work rather than stopping at recommendations (BCG X careers; McKinsey careers). Their comparative advantage is the combination of executive alignment, operating-model redesign, adoption, and technical implementation; OpenAI explicitly assigns McKinsey and BCG a strategy-and-adoption role in its Frontier Alliances while placing their teams alongside OpenAI FDEs (OpenAI). This arrangement makes them competitors when they sell end-to-end ownership, complements when frontier-product authority remains with OpenAI, and substitutes when a customer needs more transformation capacity than the vendor can supply directly (OpenAI).

Technology consultancies. Accenture and Capgemini occupy a more delivery-heavy position in OpenAI’s model: the alliance description emphasizes end-to-end transformation, global delivery capacity, systems integration, and lifecycle support, while all four partners are responsible for strategy, integration, workflow redesign, and global deployment scaling (OpenAI). These firms gain more product access than an ordinary independent implementer because OpenAI says alliance teams receive technical resources, roadmap insight, and access to its product and research organizations (OpenAI). That access can narrow the knowledge gap with a first-party FDE team, but it does not transfer ownership of the underlying model or eliminate the need to define which party handles model escalation, integration, adoption, and post-launch support (OpenAI).

Big Four practices. EY Korea consolidated consulting, audit, tax, strategy and transactions, and financial-services AI capabilities into an enterprise-wide AI Hub in September 2025 (EY Korea). EY also says its Korean consulting organization covers strategy, implementation, operations, and enhancement, including model construction and field operation (EY Korea). ⚠️ Deloitte Korea’s engineering catalogue covers the full software-delivery lifecycle from strategy and product development through aftercare and explicitly lists custom enterprise applications, AI/GenAI applications, and data products and platforms (Deloitte Korea). Samjong KPMG states that it builds AI agents and platforms for Korean finance and public-sector organizations, extending its proposition beyond governance advice alone (Samjong KPMG). ⚠️

The Big Four’s distinctive value is the ability to combine implementation with controls, auditability, tax, risk, and regulated-industry knowledge under a broad transformation program, but a service catalogue does not establish the engineering depth of any particular account team (EY Korea; Deloitte Korea). Buyers should therefore diligence the named local builders, production references, source-code and IP treatment, operational runbooks, and incident ownership rather than procuring against the firm-wide brand alone. Accounting-linked providers also require an explicit independence review when advisory, implementation, and assurance touch the same AI system.

Engineering boutiques and agencies. Seoul-based MADEITUP describes a one-team model in which the same group performs AI consulting, implementation, and operations, including in-house LLM, RAG, agent, monitoring, and operational-tooling work (MADEITUP). ⚠️ This model can preserve continuity from workflow discovery to production and reduce the coordination layers of a global firm, but the provider’s own page does not establish audited outcomes, utilization, margins, or 24/7 support capacity (MADEITUP). ⚠️ A second pattern is co-delivery: Deloitte Consulting Korea and Korean AICC specialist MindwareWorks formed an AI-agent workflow alliance in December 2024 that assigned process-transformation consulting to Deloitte and workflow-platform technology and technical support to MindwareWorks (Deloitte Korea). This illustrates how a boutique may be a subcontractor or product specialist beside a prime consultancy rather than a direct competitor on every engagement (Deloitte Korea).

Flexible talent and independent specialists. Wanted Gigs offers freelancer, sub-one-year contract, and dispatched-worker arrangements, with a matching manager supporting engagements from project request through contract close (Wanted Gigs). It publicly lists a 6% Basic fee and a 10% Pro fee after contract formation, making its intermediation economics more transparent than the project economics of major consultancies (Wanted Gigs). The platform reports more than 35,000 verified freelancers and KRW 90 billion in cumulative project value, although these figures are self-reported (Wanted Gigs). ⚠️ This channel is most substitutable for an FDE pod when the buyer already owns architecture, security, product decisions, acceptance, and operations and only lacks temporary engineering capacity; matching and contract administration do not themselves establish product authority or production accountability (Wanted Gigs).

Across all five channels, vendor partner programs are beginning to make delivery evidence more comparable (OpenAI; Anthropic). Anthropic’s Services Track measures certified active practitioners, customers running Claude in production, and public customer endorsements, and applies the same requirements to a ten-person AI-native shop and a global consultancy (Anthropic). These criteria suggest a better Korean procurement scorecard than title or headcount alone: named local engineers, certified product competence, comparable production references, prototype-to-production conversion, operational support, adoption, capability transfer, and reusable delivery assets. A boutique becomes a plausible acquisition target when retained engineering talent, regulated references, repeatable IP, or hard-to-reproduce vendor standing is more valuable than hiring organically; no reviewed evidence establishes a Korea-specific FDE consultancy acquisition or valuation.

Tensions:

  • Breadth versus engineering intimacy. Large firms can combine strategy, delivery, change, and governance, while smaller pods can keep senior engineers closer to users and code; the public sources do not disclose the actual engineering mix or deployable local headcount of individual Korean practices (EY Korea; Deloitte Korea; MADEITUP). ⚠️
  • Accountability versus flexibility. An integrated consultancy or boutique can accept delivery obligations, whereas a matching platform principally reduces search and contracting friction and leaves more architecture, acceptance, reliability, and continuity risk with the buyer (Wanted Gigs).
  • Vendor neutrality versus privileged access. Alliance firms receive certification, roadmap insight, and product/research access, which can improve delivery quality but may also bias recommendations toward the partner platform (OpenAI; Anthropic).
  • Complement versus substitute. OpenAI explicitly puts consultancies alongside its FDEs, but the same firms can replace scarce first-party capacity or compete for end-to-end ownership depending on the account boundary (OpenAI).
  • Provider claims versus verified outcomes. Job pages and service descriptions establish roles, offerings, and alliances, but several build and capacity claims remain provider-reported rather than independently verified (EY Korea; Samjong KPMG; MADEITUP; Wanted Gigs). ⚠️

  • How many locally deployable AI engineers do the named practices have, and what share of Korean pilots reach reliable production?
  • How much model-vendor roadmap and research access reaches Korea-based partner teams on individual accounts?

7.5 Buyer-owned AI, data, platform, and transformation teams

Buyer-owned teams are a genuine competitive force in Korea’s FDE market, but chiefly among large groups, digital platforms, telecoms, financial institutions, and AI-intensive manufacturers (OECD). Across the broader economy, buying remains more common than building: roughly two Korean firms purchase AI solutions for every one that develops internally, and SMEs particularly favor externally supplied, relatively basic applications (OECD). Capable internal teams therefore reduce addressable external labor at strategic accounts, while most smaller buyers still require vendor or partner delivery (OECD).

When internal teams substitute for external FDEs. An internal team can replace outside capacity when it owns the complete deployment system—not merely data science or model experimentation (SK Telecom—AI CIC, KB Careers). It must repeatedly define workflows, modify integrations, operate shared platforms, evaluate models, manage risk, support adoption, handle incidents, and maintain audit evidence (SK Telecom—governance, KB Careers). SK Telecom illustrates this breadth: its CEO-led AI CIC consolidated consumer and enterprise AI products, AI data centers, alliances, and R&D, while a separate governance process spans planning, development, testing, operations, incidents, and improvement (SK Telecom—AI CIC, SK Telecom—governance).

Substitution is most credible for repeatable integrations, reusable platforms, incremental use cases, and steady-state operations (LG, SK Telecom). LG’s internal capability system spans AI literacy, applied expert training, project-leader development, and graduate degrees; its first accredited corporate graduate-school cohort drew employees from five industrial affiliates and emphasizes domain problem definition and applied problem solving (LG). This type of group-wide talent pipeline can progressively displace routine implementation across affiliates (LG).

When internal teams complement external FDEs. Strong internal capability does not eliminate external demand; it changes what the buyer purchases (Hyundai Motor Group, Kakao). Hyundai Motor Group and NVIDIA moved from platform adoption toward joint physical-AI innovation, pairing Hyundai’s Physical AI Application Center with an NVIDIA AI Technology Center and an integrated environment planned around 50,000 Blackwell GPUs (Hyundai Motor Group). Kakao retains proprietary models and orchestration while incorporating external APIs and pursuing technical collaboration and joint product development with OpenAI (Kakao). These buyers use external FDEs less for generic implementation and more for frontier-product access, co-development, specialized infrastructure, difficult production escalation, and technology transfer (Hyundai Motor Group, Kakao).

The hybrid pattern extends to governance. Woori Financial Group’s internal AI Strategy Center tendered an external professional service to design its AI-governance structure and risk-assessment process, requiring financial-sector AI-governance and consulting experience (Woori Financial Group). The internal team remains the governance owner and capability-transfer destination while the specialist supplies scarce design expertise and temporary execution capacity (Woori Financial Group). Samsung similarly combined official use of Gemini, ChatGPT, and Claude across affiliates with role-specific policies and executive-to-employee education in June 2026 (Samsung).

The minimum internal operating model. A substitute-capable enterprise needs four linked layers (SK Telecom, KB Careers, LG):

  1. A platform team owning approved models, access control, data connectors, agent infrastructure, evaluation tooling, deployment pipelines, observability, cost controls, reusable components, and vendor abstraction (SK Telecom).
  2. Federated domain pods pairing business owners and subject-matter experts with engineers to redesign workflows, define acceptance criteria, gather feedback, and measure value (LG).
  3. An independent control plane for data ownership, security and privacy, risk tiers, validation, red teaming, release approval, incidents, audit evidence, and retirement (SK Telecom, KB Careers).
  4. An adoption and operations layer that trains users, updates procedures, supports production, and transfers prototypes to named long-term owners (LG, Samsung).

Korean evidence shows the control layer becoming a permanent specialty. SK Telecom separates business-team self-assessment from second-line AI-governance and Red-Team review (SK Telecom). KB Kookmin Bank advertised an internal role covering generative-AI validation frameworks, evaluation data and metrics, tools, dashboards, model and guardrail testing, audit artifacts, and coordination with developers and business units (KB Careers). These are buyer-side decision and assurance rights, not responsibilities an external FDE can ultimately own for management (SK Telecom, KB Careers).

The practical handoff test is whether the enterprise can reproduce a deployment, change integrations, rerun evaluations, handle incidents, manage vendor or model changes, and preserve audit evidence without the original external team (SK Telecom, KB Careers). Relevant metrics include production-to-pilot conversion, time to production, adoption, business KPI movement, reliability, incident recovery, evaluation pass rates, compliance, reuse, cost per successful workflow, and declining external effort on repeat deployments (SK Telecom, LG). Sourcing should therefore be phase-specific: internal teams own recurring and differentiating capabilities, while external capacity remains rational for frontier knowledge, early uncertainty, independent challenge, unusual assurance needs, and temporary backlogs (Hyundai Motor Group, Woori Financial Group).

The economics remain opaque in the reviewed public evidence (OECD/KLI). Most surveyed Korean AI-using firms invested no more than 5% of sales in AI development, maturity and uncertain returns encouraged incremental investment, and only 26.2% had developed internal AI-use guidelines (OECD/KLI). SK Telecom announced approximately KRW 5 trillion of AI investment over five years, but that target does not establish realized returns or prove displacement of external delivery (SK Telecom).

Tensions:

  • Control versus frontier speed: Internal teams retain architecture, data, and learning, while Hyundai, Kakao, and Samsung still use external models, infrastructure, or co-development because frontier capabilities remain distributed across firms (Hyundai Motor Group, Kakao, Samsung).
  • Substitution versus complementary demand: Strong buyer teams displace routine implementation yet create the technical and governance counterpart needed for higher-value co-development and production escalation (Hyundai Motor Group, Woori Financial Group).
  • Central scale versus domain proximity: LG and SK Telecom centralize scarce expertise, while their affiliates or business teams retain domain ownership and first-line responsibilities (LG, SK Telecom).
  • Delivery speed versus independent challenge: SK Telecom and KB institutionalize separate validation work, making it important to incorporate evidence requirements before launch rather than as a late gate (SK Telecom, KB Careers).
  • Organizational activity versus demonstrated outcomes: Public sources confirm teams, partnerships, tenders, investments, and training programs, while comparable independent evidence on productivity, ROI, and durable reduction in external dependence remains unavailable (OECD/KLI).

Unresolved questions: What are the comparable headcount, utilization, fully loaded cost, attrition, and internal-versus-external break-even points for Korean buyer teams? How much FDE or consulting spend is displaced after a Korean enterprise creates an internal AI platform team? Do the announced reorganizations improve sustained productivity, ROI, and capability-transfer quality?

7.6 Alliances, routes to market, positioning, and whitespace

Korea’s emerging forward-deployment ecosystem is best understood as a layered delivery stack rather than a list of mutually exclusive competitors: the upstream model or platform vendor supplies product authority, a Korean prime or certified specialist supplies local delivery capacity, and the buyer’s internal team retains domain, risk, adoption, and long-term operating ownership. The clearest routes are direct FDE delivery, local resale with services, exclusive sector distribution, certified consulting and SI channels, platform-plus-domain-partner delivery, buyer-vendor co-development, and acquisition of deployment capacity (Palantir–KT, Samsung SDS, OpenAI Frontier Alliances, OpenAI Deployment Company, MakinaRocks, Hyundai Motor Group).

The strongest Korean reseller-plus-delivery example is Samsung SDS, which reported more than ten ChatGPT Enterprise customers after becoming OpenAI’s first official Korean reseller and described a One-Team spanning AI consulting and development, cloud and operations, and security (Samsung SDS). Its stated lifecycle extends from strategy and full-stack design through implementation, enterprise rollout, and operational advancement, making Samsung SDS a complement to OpenAI at the product layer but a potential substitute for a direct vendor FDE across local discovery, integration, security, change, and operations (Samsung SDS).

KT and Palantir use a more controlled localization route: KT specialists work on joint projects with Palantir global engineers, KT’s internal processes serve as a proof-of-concept environment, and the companies act as exclusive mutual sales partners in Korean finance (Palantir–KT). That configuration combines lighthouse deployment, capability transfer, certification, sector exclusivity, and joint selling, giving Palantir product control while building KT into the Korean commercial and delivery channel (Palantir–KT).

Global vendors are also formalizing consultancy and SI channels. OpenAI’s Frontier Alliances place BCG and McKinsey primarily around strategy, operating-model redesign, and adoption, while Accenture and Capgemini cover integration, scaled deployment, and lifecycle support; all are intended to work beside OpenAI FDEs with certification, technical resources, roadmap insight, and product and research access (OpenAI). Anthropic’s Services Track separately evaluates partner delivery depth through active certified practitioners, customers running Claude in production, and public customer references, while treating referral credit and deal protection as a separate commercial mechanism (Anthropic). These programs make consultancies and SIs more credible substitutes for direct FDE labor in later delivery phases, but the alliance relationship keeps the model vendor central for product escalation and proprietary knowledge (OpenAI, Anthropic).

Domestic physical-AI partnerships divide work differently. MakinaRocks lets domain specialists build and deliver applications on Runway while it retains the AI operating-system layer and support for difficult environments; its first program includes joint selling, reference development, training, and delivery certification (MakinaRocks). Hyundai Motor Group and NVIDIA use buyer-vendor co-development rather than outsourced implementation, pairing Hyundai’s Physical AI Application Center with an NVIDIA AI Technology Center and shifting their relationship from platform adoption toward joint innovation (Hyundai Motor Group).

Practical positioning gaps

  1. A productized mid-market bridge. The large-account routes combine direct vendor access, Korean prime delivery, and internal buyer capability, but the reviewed programs do not show a standardized Korean offer that bundles discovery, secure implementation, managed operation, and scheduled transfer behind fixed production gates (Samsung SDS, Palantir–KT, OpenAI). A provider could differentiate through a time-boxed bridge with explicit acceptance, handoff, and exit rather than indefinite bespoke support.
  2. Independent multi-model assurance and portability. Vendor and partner programs validate competence on their own platforms, creating room for a Korean evaluation and red-team layer that tests quality, cost, reliability, switching, and exit independently of the reseller, builder, or model vendor (Anthropic, OpenAI).
  3. Accountable agent and physical-AI operations. MakinaRocks’ separation of platform and domain application and Hyundai-NVIDIA’s division between buyer and vendor centers show that operational failures can span model, application, edge or OT, safety, and human-fallback boundaries (MakinaRocks, Hyundai Motor Group). A differentiated operator would provide one cross-stack incident command, shared telemetry, and tested recovery ownership.
  4. A verified Korea delivery directory. Anthropic demonstrates a public model based on active certifications, production deployments, and customer references, but the reviewed sources do not join those signals to Seoul-based staffing, regulated-environment evidence, on-site availability, lifecycle ownership, and service performance (Anthropic). A Korea-specific registry could make alliance diligence materially more decision-useful.
  5. A Korea-to-core-product feedback channel. OpenAI gives selected alliance partners roadmap and research access, while KT-Palantir and MakinaRocks explicitly build capability through joint projects and certification (OpenAI, Palantir–KT, MakinaRocks). Providers can differentiate by proving that Korean language, regulation, legacy, and industrial findings change upstream products rather than remaining account-specific customization.

Consolidation is becoming a parallel scaling route. OpenAI’s planned acquisition of Tomoro would add approximately 150 FDEs and deployment specialists to a majority-controlled Deployment Company while preserving work with consulting and integration partners, directly combining internalized delivery depth with external channel breadth (OpenAI). This makes applied-AI engineering boutiques, assurance and security specialists, vertical workflow firms, and agent or OT operations teams plausible strategic targets for product vendors and large SIs, although no reviewed source establishes a specific Korean target or transaction (OpenAI).

Tensions:

  • Direct FDE versus channel scale: Direct teams preserve product context and field learning, while Korean resellers and SIs provide broader integration, procurement, and run capacity; the two can compete for account control and high-value services (OpenAI, Samsung SDS).
  • Exclusivity versus buyer choice: KT-Palantir’s finance-sector exclusivity creates an incentive to train and co-deliver while narrowing alternative routes to the same platform (Palantir–KT).
  • Certification versus accountability: OpenAI and Anthropic use certification and production evidence to qualify partners, but those signals do not establish Korean staffing, regulated or OT fit, or contractual incident ownership (OpenAI, Anthropic).
  • Alliance access versus advisory independence: Product and roadmap access can improve consulting delivery while increasing the risk that platform affiliation influences architecture choice (OpenAI, Anthropic).
  • Platform reuse versus domain ownership: MakinaRocks can standardize the operating substrate while Dagyeom owns the application, but defects, IP, and incident responsibility can cross that boundary (MakinaRocks).
  • Buyer control versus outsourced speed: Hyundai’s internal application center preserves industrial knowledge and validation authority, while joint work with NVIDIA requires shared governance over technology, data, safety, and deployment (Hyundai Motor Group).
  • Acquisition versus ecosystem neutrality: Internalizing deployment capacity can tighten the field-to-product loop, while increasing platform dependence and competitive pressure on independent delivery firms (OpenAI).
  • Observed structure versus proven outcome: The sources establish alliances, customers, certifications, roles, and delivery structures, but none provides an independent matched comparison of Korean ROI, reliability, adoption, or transfer durability across routes (Palantir–KT, Samsung SDS, OpenAI, Anthropic).
